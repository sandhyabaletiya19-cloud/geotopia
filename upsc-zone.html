Geomorphology & Earth's Interior

+

1. Origin of Universe - Big Bang Theory

The universe originated approximately 13.8 billion years ago from an extremely hot, dense point called a singularity. This theory was proposed by Georges Lemaître and expanded by Edwin Hubble.

📖 Key Concept

The Big Bang was not an explosion IN space, but an expansion OF space itself. The universe continues to expand even today.

Evidence for Big Bang:

  • Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR): Uniform radiation at 2.7 Kelvin discovered by Penzias and Wilson (1965) - the "afterglow" of the Big Bang
  • Red Shift (Hubble's Law): Distant galaxies are moving away from us; light is stretched to red wavelengths
  • Abundance of Light Elements: Universe is ~75% Hydrogen, ~25% Helium - matches Big Bang predictions
  • Expansion of Universe: Confirmed by observations of distant supernovae

2. Formation of Solar System

The most accepted theory is the Nebular Hypothesis, proposed by Immanuel Kant (1755) and Pierre-Simon Laplace (1796).

Process of Solar System Formation:

  1. A massive cloud of gas and dust (Solar Nebula) began to collapse under gravity
  2. As it collapsed, it spun faster and flattened into a disk
  3. The center became hot and dense, forming the proto-Sun
  4. In the disk, dust particles collided and stuck together (accretion)
  5. These grew into planetesimals, then protoplanets, and finally planets
  6. The Sun ignited nuclear fusion about 4.6 billion years ago
⚠️ Important for UPSC

Frost Line: Inside this line (closer to Sun), only rocky materials could condense, forming Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars. Beyond it, ices and gases condensed, creating gas giants - Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune.

3. Interior of the Earth

We cannot directly observe Earth's interior (deepest drill is only 12 km, while Earth's radius is 6,371 km). We rely on indirect evidence, mainly seismic waves.

Seismic Waves:

Wave Type Speed Travels Through Motion
P-Waves (Primary) Fastest (6-14 km/s) Solids, Liquids, Gases Compressional (push-pull)
S-Waves (Secondary) Slower (3-8 km/s) Solids ONLY Transverse (up-down)
Surface Waves Slowest Along surface only Rolling motion
⚠️ Critical UPSC Fact

S-waves cannot travel through liquids. This is how we know the outer core is liquid - S-waves are blocked, creating a "shadow zone" between 103° and 143° from the earthquake epicenter.

Layers of Earth:

Layer Depth Composition State
Crust 0-70 km SIAL (continental) + SIMA (oceanic) Solid
Upper Mantle 70-700 km Silicates (Fe, Mg) Includes plastic asthenosphere
Lower Mantle 700-2900 km Silicates Solid but flows slowly
Outer Core 2900-5150 km Iron + Nickel (NIFE) LIQUID - generates magnetic field
Inner Core 5150-6371 km Iron + Nickel SOLID (due to extreme pressure)

Important Discontinuities:

  • Mohorovičić (Moho): Separates Crust and Mantle (~35 km depth)
  • Gutenberg: Separates Mantle and Core (~2900 km depth)
  • Lehmann: Separates Outer Core and Inner Core (~5150 km depth)
  • Conrad: Within continental crust (~15-20 km)

4. Types of Rocks

A. Igneous Rocks (Primary Rocks)

Formed by cooling and solidification of magma/lava.

  • Intrusive (Plutonic): Cool slowly underground, large crystals - Granite, Gabbro
  • Extrusive (Volcanic): Cool rapidly on surface, fine/no crystals - Basalt, Obsidian

B. Sedimentary Rocks

Formed by deposition and lithification of sediments. Cover 75% of Earth's surface.

  • Clastic: From rock fragments - Sandstone, Shale
  • Chemical: From precipitation - Limestone, Rock Salt
  • Organic: From organic matter - Coal, Chalk

Note: Only sedimentary rocks contain fossils.

C. Metamorphic Rocks

Existing rocks transformed by heat and pressure without melting.

  • Limestone → Marble
  • Sandstone → Quartzite
  • Shale → Slate
  • Granite → Gneiss
  • Coal → Graphite → Diamond

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Universe age: 13.8 billion years | Solar System: 4.6 billion years
  • S-waves cannot pass through liquids → proof outer core is liquid
  • Moho: Crust-Mantle boundary | Gutenberg: Mantle-Core boundary
  • Asthenosphere: Semi-plastic layer where plates "float"
  • Continental crust: Thicker (35-70 km), lighter (SIAL)
  • Oceanic crust: Thinner (5-10 km), denser (SIMA)
  • Inner core is solid despite 6000°C due to extreme pressure
  • Only sedimentary rocks contain fossils

Plate Tectonics

+

1. Continental Drift Theory

Proposed by Alfred Wegener (1912) in his book "The Origin of Continents and Oceans". He suggested all continents were once joined as a supercontinent called PANGAEA (meaning "all lands"), surrounded by a super-ocean PANTHALASSA.

Breakup of Pangaea:

  • ~200 million years ago: Pangaea began to break
  • Laurasia: Northern landmass (North America, Europe, Asia)
  • Gondwanaland: Southern landmass (South America, Africa, India, Australia, Antarctica)
  • Tethys Sea: Ocean between Laurasia and Gondwanaland

Evidence for Continental Drift:

A. Jigsaw Fit: Eastern coast of South America fits into Western coast of Africa. Best fit at continental shelf level.

B. Fossil Evidence:

Fossil Found In Significance
Mesosaurus South America, Africa Freshwater reptile - couldn't cross oceans
Glossopteris India, Australia, Antarctica, S. America, Africa Seed fern found on all Gondwana continents
Lystrosaurus India, Antarctica, Africa Land reptile - couldn't swim

C. Geological Evidence: Matching rock types and mountain chains across continents. Appalachian Mountains (USA) continue as Caledonian Mountains (Scotland).

D. Paleoclimatic Evidence: Ancient glacial deposits (Tillites) found in India, Africa, Australia - regions now tropical. Coal deposits in Antarctica indicate it was once in warmer latitudes.

⚠️ Why Wegener's Theory Was Initially Rejected

He couldn't explain the MECHANISM for continent movement. He suggested centrifugal and tidal forces - both too weak. It wasn't until the 1960s that sea-floor spreading provided the mechanism.

2. Sea Floor Spreading

Proposed by Harry Hess (1962). New oceanic crust is continuously created at mid-ocean ridges through volcanic activity, pushing older crust away. Older crust is destroyed at subduction zones.

Evidence:

  • Magnetic Striping: Alternating bands of normal and reversed magnetic polarity on either side of ridges - symmetrical pattern
  • Age of Ocean Floor: Youngest at ridges, oldest near continents. No oceanic crust older than 200 million years
  • Sediment Thickness: Thinnest at ridges, thickest near continents
  • Heat Flow: Highest at mid-ocean ridges

3. Plate Tectonics Theory

Earth's lithosphere is broken into several rigid plates that "float" on the plastic asthenosphere. Movement is driven by convection currents in the mantle.

Major Tectonic Plates (7):

Plate Type Area (million km²)
Pacific Plate Mostly Oceanic 103.3 (Largest)
North American Plate Continental + Oceanic 75.9
Eurasian Plate Mostly Continental 67.8
African Plate Continental + Oceanic 61.3
Antarctic Plate Continental + Oceanic 60.9
Indo-Australian Plate Continental + Oceanic 58.9
South American Plate Continental + Oceanic 43.6

Minor Plates: Nazca, Philippine, Arabian, Caribbean, Cocos, Juan de Fuca, Scotia

Types of Plate Boundaries:

A. Divergent (Constructive):

  • Plates move APART
  • New crust created
  • Features: Mid-ocean ridges, rift valleys
  • Volcanoes: Gentle, basaltic
  • Examples: Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East African Rift

B. Convergent (Destructive):

  • Plates move TOGETHER
  • Crust destroyed at subduction zones
  • Features: Trenches, mountains, island arcs
  • Volcanoes: Explosive
  • Examples: Himalayas, Andes, Ring of Fire

C. Transform (Conservative):

  • Plates slide PAST each other
  • Crust neither created nor destroyed
  • Features: Fault lines
  • Examples: San Andreas Fault

Types of Convergent Boundaries:

Type Process Result Example
Oceanic-Oceanic Denser plate subducts Island Arc + Trench Japan, Philippines
Oceanic-Continental Oceanic subducts Coastal Mountains + Trench Andes Mountains
Continental-Continental Collision (no subduction) Fold Mountains Himalayas, Alps
⚠️ Formation of Himalayas

Indian Plate (originally part of Gondwanaland) began moving north ~70 million years ago. It collided with Eurasian Plate ~50 million years ago. Since both are continental plates, neither subducted - the crust crumpled to form Himalayas. India is still moving north at ~5 cm/year. Himalayas are still rising! Marine fossils found on Mt. Everest are from the ancient Tethys Sea sediments.

Ring of Fire:

  • Horseshoe-shaped zone around Pacific Ocean
  • Contains 75% of world's active volcanoes
  • 90% of world's earthquakes occur here
  • Length: ~40,000 km
  • Caused by subduction of Pacific Plate

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Wegener's Continental Drift (1912) → lacked mechanism
  • Hess's Sea Floor Spreading (1962) → magnetic striping evidence
  • Pangaea → Laurasia (North) + Gondwanaland (South)
  • Key fossils: Mesosaurus, Glossopteris, Lystrosaurus
  • 7 major plates; Pacific is largest (mostly oceanic)
  • Divergent = create crust | Convergent = destroy crust | Transform = neither
  • Himalayas = Continental-Continental collision
  • Ring of Fire = 75% volcanoes + 90% earthquakes

Earthquakes

+

1. Definition & Key Terms

An earthquake is the sudden shaking of Earth's surface caused by the release of energy accumulated in rocks due to tectonic stress.

  • Focus (Hypocenter): Point inside Earth where earthquake originates
  • Epicenter: Point on surface directly above focus
  • Focal Depth: Distance from epicenter to focus
  • Seismic Waves: Energy waves from the focus

Classification by Focal Depth:

Type Depth Characteristics
Shallow Focus 0-70 km Most destructive (75% of energy released)
Intermediate Focus 70-300 km Moderate damage
Deep Focus 300-700 km Less damaging at surface

2. Causes of Earthquakes

A. Tectonic Causes (90%):

  • Movement at plate boundaries
  • Fault movements releasing accumulated stress
  • Elastic Rebound Theory: Rocks bend under stress, then snap back suddenly

B. Non-Tectonic Causes:

  • Volcanic Activity: Magma movement
  • Collapse: Underground cavities (mining areas)
  • Reservoir-Induced Seismicity: Weight of water in large dams
  • Nuclear Explosions: Underground tests
⚠️ Koyna Earthquake (1967)

India's most significant example of Reservoir-Induced Seismicity.
• Magnitude: 6.5
• Deaths: ~200
• Koyna Dam filled in 1962; earthquake in 1967
• Weight of water triggered existing fault systems
This is frequently asked in UPSC!

3. Measurement of Earthquakes

A. Richter Scale (Magnitude):

  • Developed by Charles Richter (1935)
  • Measures ENERGY released at source
  • Logarithmic: Each increase = 10× amplitude, ~31.6× energy
  • One value per earthquake
Magnitude Classification Effects
< 2.0 Micro Not felt
4.0 - 4.9 Light Minor damage
5.0 - 5.9 Moderate Damage to weak buildings
6.0 - 6.9 Strong Serious damage in populated areas
7.0 - 7.9 Major Widespread heavy damage
≥ 8.0 Great Devastating

B. Mercalli Scale (Intensity):

  • Measures DAMAGE at a location (I to XII)
  • Different values at different places for same earthquake
  • Based on observations, not instruments
💡 Key Difference

Richter: One value per earthquake (energy at source)
Mercalli: Different values at different locations (damage observed)

4. Earthquake Zones of India

India is divided into 4 seismic zones (Zone I merged with Zone II in 2002):

Zone Risk Regions
Zone V Very High Entire NE India, Kashmir, Himachal, Uttarakhand, North Bihar, Andaman & Nicobar, Kutch (Gujarat)
Zone IV High Delhi-NCR, remaining J&K, parts of UP, Bihar, West Bengal, Gujarat, Mumbai
Zone III Moderate Parts of Kerala, Goa, Maharashtra, MP, Jharkhand, Odisha
Zone II Low Remaining parts of Peninsular India

Major Earthquakes in India:

Year Location Magnitude Deaths
1897 Shillong, Assam 8.1 1,500+
1905 Kangra, HP 7.8 19,000+
1934 Bihar-Nepal 8.1 11,000+
1950 Assam 8.6 1,500+ (Largest in India)
1967 Koyna, Maharashtra 6.5 200+ (Reservoir-induced)
1993 Latur, Maharashtra 6.2 9,748 (in "stable" Zone II!)
2001 Bhuj, Gujarat 7.7 20,000+ (Deadliest recent)
2005 Kashmir 7.6 86,000+ (mostly Pakistan)

5. Effects and Mitigation

Primary Effects:

  • Ground shaking and rupture
  • Building collapse

Secondary Effects:

  • Tsunamis: From submarine earthquakes
  • Landslides: In hilly areas
  • Liquefaction: Soil behaves like liquid
  • Fires: From broken gas lines

Mitigation:

  • Earthquake-resistant building codes
  • Early warning systems
  • Land use planning (avoid fault lines)
  • Public awareness: "Drop, Cover, Hold On"

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Focus = Inside Earth | Epicenter = On surface directly above
  • 90% earthquakes are tectonic, at plate boundaries
  • Koyna 1967 = India's main example of Reservoir-Induced Seismicity
  • Richter = Energy (one value) | Mercalli = Damage (varies by location)
  • India has 4 seismic zones; Zone V is most dangerous
  • Bhuj 2001 = Deadliest recent Indian earthquake (20,000+ deaths)
  • Latur 1993 = Even "stable" zones can have earthquakes

Volcanoes

+

1. What is a Volcano?

A volcano is an opening in Earth's crust through which molten rock (magma), ash, and gases escape to the surface. When magma reaches the surface, it's called lava.

Key Terms:

  • Magma: Molten rock below surface
  • Lava: Magma that reaches surface
  • Crater: Bowl-shaped depression at summit
  • Caldera: Large depression from collapse after major eruption
  • Magma Chamber: Underground reservoir of magma

2. Causes of Volcanic Activity

A. At Plate Boundaries (80%):

Divergent Boundaries:

  • Plates move apart, magma rises to fill gap
  • Forms mid-ocean ridges
  • Eruptions: Gentle, basaltic lava
  • Example: Mid-Atlantic Ridge, Iceland

Convergent Boundaries:

  • Subducting plate melts, magma rises
  • Forms volcanic arcs
  • Eruptions: Often explosive
  • Example: Pacific Ring of Fire, Andes

B. Hotspots (20%):

Stationary plumes of hot mantle material. Plate moves over hotspot, creating chain of volcanoes.

  • Hawaiian Islands: Pacific Plate moves northwest over hotspot. Oldest island (Kauai) northwest, youngest (Big Island) over current hotspot.
  • Yellowstone: Continental hotspot
  • Reunion Island: Created Deccan Traps when India passed over it ~65 million years ago

3. Types of Volcanoes

A. By Activity:

Type Definition Examples
Active Currently erupting or showing activity Kilauea, Mt. Etna, Barren Island (India)
Dormant Not active but could erupt Mt. Fuji, Narcondam (India)
Extinct Unlikely to erupt again Mt. Kilimanjaro
⚠️ Indian Volcanoes

Barren Island (Andaman) - India's ONLY active volcano
Narcondam Island (Andaman) - Dormant volcano
Deccan Traps - Ancient flood basalt (~66 million years ago), may have contributed to dinosaur extinction

B. By Shape:

Type Characteristics Lava Examples
Shield Volcano Broad, gentle slopes Basaltic (fluid) Mauna Loa, Hawaii
Composite/Stratovolcano Steep, symmetrical cone Andesitic (viscous) Mt. Fuji, Vesuvius
Cinder Cone Small, steep Basaltic-Andesitic Paricutin (Mexico)
Caldera Large depression Various Yellowstone, Crater Lake

4. Global Distribution

Ring of Fire:

  • Horseshoe-shaped zone around Pacific
  • 75% of world's active volcanoes
  • 90% of world's earthquakes
  • Length: ~40,000 km
  • Countries: Chile, Peru, Mexico, USA, Japan, Philippines, Indonesia, New Zealand

Other Volcanic Regions:

  • Mid-Atlantic Ridge: Iceland (sits on ridge + hotspot)
  • Mediterranean: Italy (Vesuvius, Etna, Stromboli)

5. Lava Types

Type Silica Viscosity Eruption Style
Basaltic ~50% Low (fluid) Gentle, effusive
Andesitic ~60% Medium Intermediate
Rhyolitic ~70% High (sticky) Explosive, violent
💡 Remember

Higher silica = More viscous = More explosive eruption

6. Hazards and Benefits

Hazards:

  • Pyroclastic Flows: Fast-moving hot gas and debris (MOST DEADLY)
  • Lava Flows: Destroy everything in path
  • Lahars: Volcanic mudflows
  • Ash Fall: Collapses roofs, disrupts aviation
  • Climate Effects: Major eruptions can cause global cooling

Benefits:

  • Fertile Soils: Volcanic ash is extremely fertile
  • Geothermal Energy: Iceland, Philippines
  • Mineral Deposits: Sulfur, gold, silver
  • Tourism: Millions of visitors

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Ring of Fire = 75% of world's volcanoes around Pacific
  • Barren Island = India's only active volcano (Andaman)
  • Deccan Traps = Ancient flood basalt, possibly contributed to dinosaur extinction
  • Shield volcanoes = Gentle, basaltic (Hawaii)
  • Composite volcanoes = Steep, explosive (Fuji, Vesuvius)
  • Higher silica = More viscous = More explosive
  • Hotspots create island chains (Hawaii)
  • Pyroclastic flows = Most deadly volcanic hazard

Cyclones

+

1. What is a Cyclone?

A cyclone is a large-scale air mass that rotates around a strong center of low atmospheric pressure. In the Northern Hemisphere, cyclones rotate counter-clockwise; in the Southern Hemisphere, clockwise (due to Coriolis effect).

2. Types of Cyclones

A. Tropical Cyclones:

  • Form over warm tropical oceans (sea temp > 26-27°C)
  • Derive energy from warm, moist air rising from ocean
  • Have a calm "eye" at center
  • Called different names in different regions
Region Name
Atlantic & NE Pacific Hurricanes
NW Pacific Typhoons
Indian Ocean & South Pacific Cyclones
Australia Willy-Willies

Conditions for Tropical Cyclone Formation:

  1. Sea surface temperature > 26-27°C
  2. Coriolis force (5-30° latitude from equator)
  3. Low wind shear (uniform wind speed with height)
  4. Pre-existing low-pressure disturbance
  5. Upper atmosphere divergence

B. Temperate (Extra-tropical) Cyclones:

  • Form in mid-latitudes (30-60°)
  • Form along polar front (meeting of warm and cold air)
  • Derive energy from temperature contrast, not ocean
  • Larger but less intense than tropical cyclones
  • Have fronts (warm and cold)
  • No clear "eye"
  • Bring winter rainfall to Mediterranean regions

3. Structure of a Tropical Cyclone

  • Eye: Calm center, 20-40 km diameter, clear skies
  • Eye Wall: Most intense winds and rain, surrounds eye
  • Spiral Rain Bands: Bands of thunderstorms spiraling outward

4. Cyclones in India

Cyclone-Prone Coasts:

  • Bay of Bengal coast: More prone - West Bengal, Odisha, Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu. Bay is warmer, gets more cyclones.
  • Arabian Sea coast: Less prone - Gujarat, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Kerala
  • Ratio: 4:1 (Bay of Bengal : Arabian Sea)

Cyclone Seasons:

  • Pre-monsoon: April-June (more in Bay of Bengal)
  • Post-monsoon: October-December (peak season)

Recent Major Cyclones:

Cyclone Year Region Impact
Super Cyclone 1999 Odisha 10,000+ deaths
Fani 2019 Odisha Extensive damage, fewer deaths due to evacuation
Amphan 2020 West Bengal Super cyclone, $13 billion damage
Tauktae 2021 Gujarat (Arabian Sea) Most intense cyclone in Arabian Sea
Biparjoy 2023 Gujarat Extremely severe cyclone

5. Cyclone Naming

Names are decided by WMO (World Meteorological Organization) and regional bodies.

For North Indian Ocean (Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea): Names given by 13 countries including India, Bangladesh, Pakistan, Sri Lanka, etc.

Recent names suggested by India: Gati, Tej, Murasu, Aag, Vyom, Jhar, Probaho, Neer

6. Cyclone Management

  • IMD: India Meteorological Department - forecasting and warnings
  • NDMA: National Disaster Management Authority - coordination
  • Cyclone Shelters: Multi-purpose shelters in coastal areas
  • Early Warning Systems: Doppler radars, satellites
  • Odisha Model: Successful evacuation (Fani 2019 - minimal deaths despite intensity)

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Tropical cyclones need sea temp > 26-27°C
  • Northern Hemisphere: Counter-clockwise rotation
  • Hurricane = Atlantic | Typhoon = NW Pacific | Cyclone = Indian Ocean
  • Bay of Bengal gets 4× more cyclones than Arabian Sea
  • Cyclone seasons: April-June & October-December
  • Eye = Calm center | Eye Wall = Most intense winds
  • Temperate cyclones = Mid-latitudes, form along fronts
  • IMD issues cyclone warnings in India

Indian Rivers & Drainage

+

1. Classification of Indian Rivers

Basis Classification
By Origin Himalayan Rivers vs Peninsular Rivers
By Orientation East-flowing (Bay of Bengal) vs West-flowing (Arabian Sea)
By Nature Perennial vs Seasonal

2. Himalayan Rivers

Characteristics:

  • Perennial: Fed by glaciers + rainfall
  • Long courses: Travel great distances
  • Large basins: Cover extensive areas
  • Antecedent: Older than the mountains they cut through
  • Form gorges in mountains, meanders in plains

A. Indus River System:

Feature Details
Origin Near Lake Mansarovar, Tibet
Total Length 2,880 km (880 km in India)
Tributaries Jhelum, Chenab, Ravi, Beas, Sutlej
Drains into Arabian Sea (Pakistan)

Indus Waters Treaty (1960): Between India and Pakistan. India gets Ravi, Beas, Sutlej. Pakistan gets Indus, Jhelum, Chenab.

B. Ganga River System:

Feature Details
Origin Gangotri Glacier (as Bhagirathi)
Length 2,525 km (longest in India)
Major Tributaries Yamuna, Ghaghara, Gandak, Kosi, Son, Chambal
Drains into Bay of Bengal (forms Sundarbans delta)

Important Facts:

  • Alaknanda + Bhagirathi meet at Devprayag to form Ganga
  • Yamuna is the largest tributary (origin: Yamunotri glacier)
  • Sundarbans is the world's largest delta

C. Brahmaputra River System:

Feature Details
Origin Angsi Glacier, near Mansarovar, Tibet
Total Length 2,900 km
Names Tsangpo (Tibet) → Dihang/Siang (India) → Brahmaputra (Assam) → Jamuna (Bangladesh)
Special Feature Majuli - World's largest river island

3. Peninsular Rivers

Characteristics:

  • Seasonal: Dependent on monsoon rainfall
  • Shorter courses: Compared to Himalayan rivers
  • Fixed courses: Flow through hard rock valleys
  • Non-navigable: Due to rapids and waterfalls

East-Flowing Rivers:

River Origin Length Key Features
Godavari Nasik, Maharashtra 1,465 km Longest Peninsular river, "Dakshin Ganga"
Krishna Mahabaleshwar 1,400 km Second longest Peninsular river
Kaveri Brahmagiri Hills, Karnataka 800 km "Ganga of the South", Shivasamudram Falls
Mahanadi Chhattisgarh 851 km Hirakud Dam (longest dam in India)

West-Flowing Rivers:

River Origin Length Key Feature
Narmada Amarkantak, MP 1,312 km Flows through rift valley, forms estuaries (not delta)
Tapi Satpura Range 724 km Also flows through rift valley
💡 Why Narmada and Tapi flow West?

They flow through rift valleys between the Vindhya and Satpura ranges. These fault-controlled valleys slope westward. Most other peninsular rivers flow east because the Western Ghats form a water divide, and the peninsula tilts eastward.

4. Important Dams

Dam River State Significance
Tehri Dam Bhagirathi Uttarakhand Highest dam in India (260m)
Bhakra Nangal Sutlej HP/Punjab Second highest, "Temple of Resurgent India"
Hirakud Mahanadi Odisha Longest dam in India
Sardar Sarovar Narmada Gujarat Second largest concrete dam
Nagarjuna Sagar Krishna Telangana/AP World's largest masonry dam

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Himalayan rivers: Perennial, antecedent, longer
  • Peninsular rivers: Seasonal, shorter, fixed courses
  • Ganga: Longest in India (2,525 km)
  • Godavari: Longest Peninsular river (1,465 km)
  • Indus Waters Treaty 1960: India-Pakistan water sharing
  • Narmada & Tapi: Flow west through rift valleys, form estuaries
  • Majuli: World's largest river island (Brahmaputra)
  • Sundarbans: World's largest delta (Ganga-Brahmaputra)
  • Tehri: Highest dam | Hirakud: Longest dam

Climate of India

+

1. Factors Affecting Indian Climate

  • Latitude: India extends from 8°4'N to 37°6'N (Tropic of Cancer passes through middle)
  • Himalayas: Act as a barrier, preventing cold Central Asian air from entering India; trap monsoon winds
  • Distance from Sea: Coastal areas have moderate climate; interior has continental climate
  • Monsoon Winds: Dominate Indian climate, bring 80% of annual rainfall
  • Altitude: Temperature decreases with height (6.5°C per 1000m)
  • Jet Streams: Influence onset and withdrawal of monsoon

2. Seasons in India

Season Months Characteristics
Winter December - February Cold, dry; NE Monsoon brings rain to Tamil Nadu; Western Disturbances bring rain to NW India
Summer (Pre-Monsoon) March - May Hot, dry; Loo winds in North; thunderstorms (Nor'westers in Bengal, Mango showers in Kerala)
Southwest Monsoon June - September Rainy season; 75-90% of annual rainfall; arrives Kerala June 1, reaches entire country by July 15
Post-Monsoon (Retreating) October - November Monsoon withdraws; cyclones in Bay of Bengal; NE Monsoon begins

3. The Monsoon

The word "Monsoon" comes from Arabic "Mausim" meaning season. It refers to the seasonal reversal of wind direction.

Southwest Monsoon Mechanism:

  1. In summer, intense heating creates a low-pressure zone over NW India (Thar Desert)
  2. High pressure develops over Indian Ocean (relatively cooler)
  3. Moisture-laden winds blow from sea to land (SW direction)
  4. Coriolis force deflects these winds
  5. These winds are attracted to the low-pressure zone over Tibet (heat low)

Two Branches of Monsoon:

Branch Path Areas Affected
Arabian Sea Branch Hits Western Ghats → Divides into three Western coast, Central India, NW India
Bay of Bengal Branch Hits Myanmar coast → Deflected west by Himalayas NE India, Gangetic Plain, NW India

Role of Jet Streams:

  • Subtropical Westerly Jet: Present over North India in winter; helps Western Disturbances
  • Tropical Easterly Jet: Develops in summer over Peninsular India; associated with monsoon
  • Withdrawal of Westerly Jet from North India allows monsoon onset

4. Rainfall Distribution

Rainfall Category Annual Rainfall Areas
Very High (> 200 cm) 200+ cm Western Ghats (windward), NE India, Andaman
High (100-200 cm) 100-200 cm West Bengal, Odisha, Bihar, Coastal areas
Moderate (50-100 cm) 50-100 cm Deccan Plateau, UP, MP
Low (< 50 cm) < 50 cm Western Rajasthan, Gujarat (Kutch), Ladakh

Extreme Rainfall Locations:

  • Mawsynram, Meghalaya: Highest rainfall in the world (avg 11,871 mm/year)
  • Cherrapunji: Second highest (11,777 mm/year)
  • Jaisalmer, Rajasthan: Lowest rainfall in India (~100 mm)
💡 Why does NE India receive so much rainfall?

The funnel-shaped topography of Meghalaya hills causes the moisture-laden Bay of Bengal branch to be forced upward, causing orographic rainfall. The hills act as a barrier, squeezing out maximum moisture.

5. Local Winds

Wind Season Region Characteristics
Loo Summer (May-June) Northern Plains Hot, dry, dusty winds from Thar Desert
Nor'westers (Kalbaisakhi) Pre-Monsoon Bengal, Assam Violent thunderstorms; beneficial for tea and jute
Mango Showers Pre-Monsoon Kerala, Karnataka Help in ripening of mangoes
Cherry Blossoms Pre-Monsoon Karnataka Help coffee flowering

🔑 Key Points for Revision

  • Monsoon = Seasonal reversal of wind direction
  • SW Monsoon: June-September (75-90% of rainfall)
