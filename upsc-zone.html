🌍 Physical Geography - Complete

Chapter 1: Origin of Earth & Universe

1.1 The Big Bang Theory

Definition: The Big Bang Theory states that the universe originated from an extremely hot, dense point (singularity) approximately 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding ever since.

The Big Bang Theory is the most widely accepted cosmological model explaining the origin of the universe. It was first proposed by Georges Lemaître in 1927 and later supported by Edwin Hubble's observations.

Key Concepts:

  • Singularity: Before the Big Bang, all matter, energy, space, and time were compressed into an infinitely small, hot, dense point called singularity
  • Expansion: The universe has been expanding since the Big Bang. This expansion is still continuing today at an accelerating rate
  • Cosmic Inflation: In the first fraction of a second, the universe underwent rapid exponential expansion called inflation
  • Formation of Matter: As the universe cooled, quarks combined to form protons and neutrons, then hydrogen and helium atoms

Evidence Supporting Big Bang Theory:

Evidence Description Discovered By
Red Shift Light from distant galaxies is shifted toward red end of spectrum, indicating they are moving away from us. The farther the galaxy, the greater the red shift. Edwin Hubble (1929)
Hubble's Law Velocity of recession of galaxies is directly proportional to their distance. V = H₀ × D Edwin Hubble (1929)
Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation (CMBR) Uniform radiation at 2.7 Kelvin from all directions - remnant heat from the Big Bang Penzias & Wilson (1965)
Abundance of Light Elements Universe is 75% Hydrogen, 25% Helium - matches Big Bang nucleosynthesis predictions Various scientists
🔑 UPSC Important Points:
• Universe age: 13.8 billion years
• Hubble constant (H₀): ~70 km/s/Mpc
• CMBR temperature: 2.725 Kelvin
• Nobel Prize 1978: Penzias & Wilson for CMBR discovery
• Nobel Prize 2006: Mather & Smoot for CMBR studies

1.2 Formation of Solar System

The solar system formed approximately 4.6 billion years ago from a giant molecular cloud of gas and dust. Various theories have been proposed to explain its formation:

Theory Proposed By Year Key Concept Limitations
Nebular Hypothesis Immanuel Kant & Pierre Laplace 1755, 1796 A rotating nebula (cloud of gas) slowly contracted due to gravity, forming the sun at center and planets from outer rings Could not explain angular momentum distribution
Planetesimal Theory Chamberlin & Moulton 1904 A passing star pulled gaseous material from the sun, which condensed into planetesimals and then planets Hot material would disperse, not condense
Tidal Theory James Jeans & Harold Jeffreys 1917 A larger star passed close to the sun, pulling out a cigar-shaped filament that formed planets Star encounters are extremely rare
Binary Star Theory H.N. Russell & R.A. Lyttleton 1937 Sun had a companion star which exploded or was disrupted, leaving material that formed planets Mathematical improbability
Solar Nebular Model (Modern) Viktor Safronov & others 1969+ A rotating disk of gas and dust (protoplanetary disk) formed around young sun; dust grains accreted to form planetesimals and planets Currently accepted with modifications

Stages of Solar System Formation (Modern View):

Molecular Cloud → Gravitational Collapse → Protoplanetary Disk → Planetesimals → Protoplanets → Planets

1.3 Formation of Earth

Earth formed through a process called accretion approximately 4.6 billion years ago. Planetesimals collided and merged due to gravity, gradually building up the proto-Earth.

Timeline of Earth's Early History:

Time (Years Ago) Event Details
4.6 billion Earth Formation Accretion of planetesimals; Earth was molten (Magma Ocean)
4.5 billion Moon Formation Giant Impact (Theia hypothesis): Mars-sized body collided with Earth
4.4 billion Oldest Minerals Zircon crystals from Jack Hills, Western Australia
4.0 billion Oldest Rocks Acasta Gneiss, Canada (4.03 billion years)
4.1-3.8 billion Late Heavy Bombardment Intense asteroid/comet impacts on Earth and Moon
3.8 billion First Oceans Water condensed on Earth's surface as it cooled
3.5-3.8 billion First Life Prokaryotic cells (bacteria-like organisms)
2.4 billion Great Oxidation Event Oxygen levels rose due to cyanobacteria photosynthesis
⚡ UPSC Frequently Asked:
• Age of Earth: 4.6 billion years (determined by radiometric dating)
• Age of Universe: 13.8 billion years
• Oldest mineral: Zircon (4.4 billion years, Australia)
• Oldest rock: Acasta Gneiss (4.03 billion years, Canada)
• Moon formation theory: Giant Impact/Theia Hypothesis
• Method used for age determination: Radiometric dating (using isotopes like U-Pb, K-Ar)

Chapter 2: Interior of Earth

2.1 Sources of Information About Earth's Interior

Since direct observation of Earth's interior is impossible beyond a few kilometers, scientists rely on indirect methods:

Direct Sources

  • Mining & Drilling: Deepest drill hole - Kola Superdeep Borehole, Russia (12.26 km)
  • Volcanic Eruptions: Bring material from upper mantle (xenoliths, magma composition)
  • Hot Springs & Geysers: Indicate internal heat

Indirect Sources

  • Seismic Waves: Most important source
  • Gravity Anomalies: Variations indicate density differences
  • Magnetic Field: Suggests liquid iron outer core
  • Meteorites: Represent primordial solar system material
  • Temperature & Pressure Studies: Increase with depth

2.2 Seismic Waves - Detailed Study

Seismic Waves: Energy waves that travel through Earth's interior or along its surface, generated by earthquakes, volcanic eruptions, or explosions.

Types of Seismic Waves:

Wave Type Nature Particle Motion Speed Medium Characteristics
P-Waves
(Primary/Compressional)		 Body Wave Back & forth (parallel to wave direction) 5-8 km/s in crust;
13 km/s in core		 Solid, Liquid, Gas First to arrive; Cause push-pull motion; Speed up in denser material
S-Waves
(Secondary/Shear)		 Body Wave Side to side (perpendicular to wave direction) 3-5 km/s in crust Solid Only Second to arrive; Cannot pass through liquid; More destructive than P-waves
L-Waves
(Love Waves)		 Surface Wave Horizontal shaking (transverse) 2-4.5 km/s Surface only Named after A.E.H. Love; Cause horizontal ground movement
R-Waves
(Rayleigh Waves)		 Surface Wave Elliptical rolling motion 2-4 km/s Surface only Named after Lord Rayleigh; Most destructive; Cause ground to roll
🔑 Key Discovery: S-waves cannot pass through liquid. When seismologists found that S-waves don't reach the opposite side of Earth through the core, they concluded that the outer core is liquid. This is one of the most important applications of seismology.

Shadow Zones:

When seismic waves travel through Earth, they create shadow zones where certain waves don't reach:

Wave Type Shadow Zone Location Reason
P-Wave 103° to 142° from epicenter Refraction (bending) at core-mantle boundary
S-Wave Beyond 103° from epicenter (entire opposite side) Cannot pass through liquid outer core

Special Layers:

  • Lithosphere: Crust + uppermost mantle; rigid; 100-200 km thick; broken into tectonic plates
  • Asthenosphere: Upper mantle (100-700 km); partially molten; plastic; convection currents occur here; plates "float" on it
  • Mesosphere: Lower mantle; solid but can flow slowly
  • D" Layer (D-double prime): Transition zone just above outer core (200-300 km); may contain partially molten material

2.4 Discontinuities

Discontinuity: A zone within Earth where seismic wave velocities change abruptly, indicating a change in physical properties (density, composition, or state).
Discontinuity Depth Location Discovered By Significance
Conrad ~15-20 km Within continental crust (upper/lower crust) V. Conrad (1925) Separates SIAL (granitic) from SIMA (basaltic) in continental crust
Mohorovičić (Moho) 5-70 km
(avg 35 km)		 Crust-Mantle boundary Andrija Mohorovičić (1909) Most well-defined; P-wave velocity increases from 6.9 to 8 km/s
Repetti ~700 km Upper-Lower Mantle boundary W. Repetti Mineralogical change; phase transition
Gutenberg 2,900 km Mantle-Core boundary Beno Gutenberg (1914) P-wave velocity drops; S-waves stop; indicates liquid outer core
Lehmann 5,100 km Outer Core-Inner Core boundary Inge Lehmann (1936) P-wave velocity increases; indicates solid inner core
📝 Mnemonic: "Can Men Really Grow Legs" = Conrad → Moho → Repetti → Gutenberg → Lehmann (Top to Bottom)
⚡ UPSC Important Points:
• Moho discontinuity is the most important and well-defined
• Gutenberg discontinuity is where S-waves stop (proof of liquid core)
• Lehmann discontinuity discovered by female scientist Inge Lehmann
• Earth's core temperature: ~5,500°C (as hot as Sun's surface)
• Outer core generates Earth's magnetic field through dynamo effect

Chapter 7: Weathering

7.1 Introduction to Weathering

Weathering: The in-situ (in place) disintegration and decomposition of rocks at or near the Earth's surface through physical, chemical, and biological processes, without involving transportation of material.

Weathering vs Erosion:

Aspect Weathering Erosion
Definition Breaking down of rocks in place Removal and transport of weathered material
Movement No movement of material Material is transported
Agents Temperature, water, chemicals, organisms Wind, water, ice, gravity, waves
Location At or near surface Can occur anywhere
Energy Requirement Lower Higher (kinetic energy needed)

7.2 Physical (Mechanical) Weathering

Physical weathering involves the disintegration of rocks without any change in their chemical composition. It is dominant in arid, semi-arid, and cold regions.

Type Process Where Common Examples/Features
Frost Weathering
(Freeze-Thaw)		 Water enters cracks → Freezes → Expands (9% volume increase) → Widens crack → Repeated cycles break rock Cold regions; Mountains; Temperate areas with freeze-thaw cycles Talus/Scree slopes; Angular rock fragments; Common in Himalayas
Thermal Expansion
(Insolation Weathering)		 Rocks expand when heated, contract when cooled → Repeated cycles cause stress → Outer layers crack and peel Hot deserts with large diurnal temperature range (day-night difference) Sahara, Thar; Rock flaking; Block disintegration
Exfoliation
(Onion-skin Weathering)		 Concentric layers peel off like onion skin due to differential expansion/contraction of rock layers Hot deserts; Granite regions Exfoliation domes (Half Dome, Yosemite); Spheroidal weathering
Salt Weathering
(Haloclasty)		 Salt solution enters pores → Water evaporates → Salt crystals grow → Exert pressure → Rock crumbles Coastal areas; Arid regions; Near salt lakes Honeycomb weathering (tafoni); Weathering of buildings near coast
Pressure Release
(Unloading)		 Overlying rock erodes → Pressure reduces → Deep rock expands upward → Parallel cracks form Areas where deep rocks are exposed by erosion Sheet jointing; Granite exfoliation domes
Wetting & Drying
(Slaking)		 Rocks (esp. clay-rich) absorb water → Expand → Dry → Contract → Repeated cycles cause cracking Regions with alternating wet/dry seasons Mudcracks; Black soil regions (India)

7.3 Chemical Weathering

Chemical weathering involves the decomposition of rocks through chemical reactions that change their mineral composition. It is dominant in hot, humid tropical regions where water and high temperatures accelerate chemical reactions.

Type Chemical Process Affected Rocks/Minerals Results/Examples
Oxidation Minerals combine with oxygen (especially in presence of moisture)
4Fe + 3O₂ + water → 2Fe₂O₃		 Iron-bearing minerals (Biotite, Pyrite, Hornblende) Rusting; Red/yellow/brown color; Limonite formation; Rock weakens
Carbonation CO₂ dissolves in water → Carbonic acid (H₂CO₃) → Dissolves carbonates
CaCO₃ + H₂CO₃ → Ca(HCO₃)₂ (soluble)		 Limestone, Dolomite, Marble (carbonate rocks) Karst topography; Caves; Sinkholes; Limestone pavements
Hydration Water molecules chemically combine with minerals → Volume increases
CaSO₄ + 2H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O (gypsum)		 Anhydrite, Feldspars Swelling; Rock expansion; Conversion of anhydrite to gypsum
Hydrolysis Silicate minerals react with water → H⁺ and OH⁻ ions replace mineral ions
Feldspar + Water → Clay + Silica		 Silicate minerals (Feldspar, Mica) Clay formation; Kaolinite; Most important in granite weathering
Solution
(Dissolution)		 Minerals dissolve directly in water
NaCl → Na⁺ + Cl⁻		 Halite (rock salt), Gypsum, Calcite Salt disappears; Caves in salt deposits
Chelation Organic compounds (from plants) bind with metal ions and remove them from rock Various minerals Important in soil formation; Podzolization
🔑 Key Insight: Chemical weathering is most effective in hot and humid climates because:
  • High temperature increases reaction rates (doubles with every 10°C rise)
  • Abundant water is needed for most chemical reactions
  • Vegetation produces organic acids that accelerate weathering

7.4 Biological Weathering

Biological weathering is caused by living organisms including plants, animals, and microorganisms. It can involve both physical and chemical processes.

Agent Mechanism Type (Physical/Chemical)
Plant Roots Roots grow into cracks → Exert pressure → Widen cracks → Break rock Physical
Burrowing Animals Earthworms, termites, rodents dig tunnels → Mix soil → Expose rock to weathering agents Physical
Lichens & Mosses Attach to rock → Produce organic acids → Dissolve minerals → Physical penetration Both
Bacteria & Fungi Secrete acids and chelating compounds → Break down minerals Chemical
Decaying Organic Matter Produces CO₂ and humic acids → Increases carbonation and chemical weathering Chemical

7.5 Factors Affecting Weathering

Factor Effect on Weathering
Climate • Hot & humid: Maximum chemical weathering
• Hot & dry: Physical weathering (thermal, salt)
• Cold: Frost weathering dominant
• Temperate: Both types active
Rock Type & Structure • Soluble rocks (limestone) → Chemical weathering
• Hard rocks (granite) → Slow weathering
• Jointed rocks → Faster weathering
• Mineral composition affects susceptibility
Topography • Steep slopes: Less weathering (material removed quickly)
• Flat areas: More weathering (material accumulates)
Vegetation • Accelerates biological and chemical weathering
• Roots contribute to physical breakdown
• Humus increases soil acidity
Time • Longer exposure = More weathering
• Young landscapes less weathered than old
⚡ UPSC Important - Weathering Zones:
• Polar regions: Frost weathering dominant
• Temperate regions: All types, seasonal variation
• Hot deserts: Physical weathering (thermal, salt)
• Humid tropics: Chemical weathering maximum → Deep weathering profiles
• Goldich Dissolution Series: Minerals that crystallize first from magma (olivine, pyroxene) weather fastest; quartz most resistant

Chapter 8: Geomorphology - Landforms

8.1 Introduction to Geomorphology

Geomorphology: The scientific study of landforms, their evolution, and the processes that shape them. The term comes from Greek: geo (Earth) + morphe (form) + logos (study).

Geomorphic Agents:

Agent Type Dominant Environment Landforms Created
Running Water (Rivers) Fluvial Humid regions Valleys, gorges, waterfalls, deltas, floodplains
Groundwater Karst Limestone regions Caves, sinkholes, stalactites, stalagmites
Glaciers Glacial Polar, high mountains U-valleys, cirques, moraines, fjords
Wind Aeolian Deserts, coastal areas Sand dunes, yardangs, loess, mushroom rocks
Waves & Currents Coastal/Marine Coastlines Cliffs, beaches, spits, bars, stacks
Gravity Mass Wasting Slopes everywhere Landslides, talus, soil creep

8.2 Fluvial Landforms (River Landforms)

Rivers are the most important geomorphic agent in humid regions. They create both erosional and depositional landforms, which vary along the river's course.

A. Erosional Landforms:

Landform Description Formation Process Examples
V-Shaped Valley Valley with steep sides and narrow floor shaped like letter V Vertical erosion by river in upper course; weathering widens sides Upper Ganga valley, most mountain streams
Gorge Deep, narrow valley with nearly vertical sides Rapid vertical erosion in hard rock; common where rivers cut through mountains Grand Canyon (Colorado); Marble Rocks (Narmada)
Canyon Very deep gorge with layered rock visible Long-term erosion in horizontal sedimentary layers Grand Canyon (USA); Fish River Canyon (Namibia)
Waterfall Vertical or near-vertical drop of water Resistant rock overlies soft rock → Soft rock erodes faster → Creates step Jog Falls (Sharavathi); Niagara Falls; Angel Falls (world's highest)
Plunge Pool Deep pool at base of waterfall Water falls with force → Erodes base through hydraulic action and abrasion Below all major waterfalls
Rapids Turbulent, fast-flowing water over rocky bed Where river flows over resistant rock outcrops Common in upper courses
Potholes Cylindrical holes in river bed rock Stones caught in eddies drill into rock (abrasion) Kukdi River (Maharashtra); many rocky river beds
River Terraces Flat surfaces beside river, above current floodplain Remnants of old floodplain left when river erodes down to new level Common in rejuvenated rivers
Incised Meanders Meanders cut deeply into rock Uplift causes river with existing meanders to cut vertically Goose Necks (USA); Lonavala (India)

B. Depositional Landforms:

Landform Description Formation Examples
Alluvial Fan Fan-shaped deposit at mountain foot Stream exits mountains → Velocity decreases → Deposits sediment in fan shape Himalayan foothills; Death Valley
Alluvial Cone Steeper, smaller version of alluvial fan Same as fan but with coarser material and steeper slope Small mountain streams
Floodplain Flat land beside river flooded during high water River deposits fine sediment during floods over thousands of years Ganga plains; Mississippi Valley
Natural Levees Raised banks along river course Coarse material deposited nearest to channel during floods Along major rivers like Mississippi, Ganga
Meanders Winding, S-shaped curves in river Erosion on outer bank (cut bank), deposition on inner bank (point bar) Middle and lower courses of all rivers
Oxbow Lake Horseshoe-shaped lake beside river Meander neck cut through during flood → Old meander isolated Common in Ganga plains; Mississippi region
Delta Triangular/fan-shaped deposit at river mouth River velocity drops at sea → Deposits all sediment → Land builds out Ganga-Brahmaputra; Nile; Mississippi
Estuary Funnel-shaped river mouth (no delta) Strong tides prevent sediment accumulation; river mouth drowned by sea Narmada; Tapti; Thames; Amazon
🔑 Delta vs Estuary (Important for UPSC):

Delta forms when:
  • Large sediment load
  • Calm water (no strong tides or currents)
  • Shallow continental shelf

Estuary forms when:
  • Strong tides carry sediment away
  • Deep water at mouth
  • Low sediment load
  • Rivers: Narmada, Tapti (India) - flow through hard rock, carry less sediment

Types of Deltas:

Type Shape Characteristics Examples
Arcuate (Fan-shaped) Curved, arc-like Most common; multiple distributaries Nile, Ganga, Indus, Rhine
Bird's Foot (Digitate) Long fingers extending into sea Deep water; fine sediment Mississippi
Cuspate Tooth or cusp-shaped Strong wave action shapes it Tiber (Italy), Ebro (Spain)
Estuarine Delta within estuary Drowned river valley Seine, Narmada (partial)

8.3 Aeolian Landforms (Wind Landforms)

Wind is an effective geomorphic agent in arid and semi-arid regions where vegetation is sparse, and in coastal areas with exposed sand.

A. Erosional Landforms:

Landform Description Formation Process
Mushroom/Pedestal Rock Rock with thin stem and wider top, like mushroom Wind erosion (abrasion) stronger near ground where sand particles are concentrated → Lower part eroded more
Zeugen Table-like rock mass on softer rock pedestal Alternating hard and soft horizontal rock layers; soft layer erodes faster
Yardang Long, parallel ridges separated by troughs Wind erosion along joints and bedding planes; aligned with wind direction
Ventifact Rock polished and faceted by wind-blown sand Abrasion creates flat surfaces (facets) on exposed rocks
Inselberg Isolated steep-sided residual hill Resistant rock mass left standing after surrounding area erodes (also called monadnock)
Deflation Hollow/Blowout Depression created by wind removing loose material Wind lifts and removes sand and dust from area
Desert Pavement (Reg) Surface covered with closely packed stones Wind removes fine material, leaving behind gravel and pebbles

B. Depositional Landforms:

Landform Description Formation
Sand Dunes Mounds or ridges of wind-deposited sand Wind deposits sand when velocity decreases or encounters obstacle
Loess Deposits of fine wind-blown silt Dust transported from deserts and glacial regions; forms fertile soil

Types of Sand Dunes:

Dune Type Shape Wind Condition Features
Barchan Crescent-shaped, horns point downwind Unidirectional wind; limited sand supply Most common type; 10-30m high; moves 15-30m/year
Transverse Long ridges perpendicular to wind Unidirectional wind; abundant sand Form in areas with lots of sand
Seif/Longitudinal Long ridges parallel to wind Bidirectional wind (two dominant directions) Can be several km long; common in Sahara
Star Dune Star-shaped with multiple arms Multidirectional/variable winds Tallest dunes; relatively stationary
Parabolic Crescent-shaped, horns point upwind Unidirectional wind; vegetation anchors edges Common in coastal areas with some vegetation
📝 Remember:
• Barchan: Horns point downwind (with wind direction)
• Parabolic: Horns point upwind (against wind direction)
This is a common UPSC question!

8.4 Glacial Landforms

Glaciers are moving masses of ice that erode, transport, and deposit material. They create distinctive landforms in polar regions and high mountains.

A. Erosional Landforms:

Landform Description Formation Examples
Cirque (Corrie/Cwm) Armchair-shaped hollow on mountainside Freeze-thaw weathering + rotational movement of ice scoops out hollow Common in Himalayas, Alps, Rockies
Arête Sharp, knife-like ridge Two cirques erode back-to-back; narrow ridge remains between them Common in glaciated mountains
Horn/Pyramidal Peak Sharp, pointed mountain peak Three or more cirques erode around a central point Matterhorn (Alps); Shivling (Himalayas)
U-Shaped Valley Wide valley with flat floor and steep sides Glacier erodes V-valley into U-shape through abrasion and plucking Yosemite Valley; many Himalayan valleys
Hanging Valley Tributary valley high above main valley floor Main glacier erodes deeper than tributary glacier; waterfall often at junction Bridalveil Falls (Yosemite)
Fjord Deep, narrow inlet with steep sides Glacial valley drowned by sea after ice melts Norway coast; New Zealand; Chile
Tarn Lake in a cirque Water fills cirque after glacier melts Many high-altitude lakes
Roche Moutonnée Asymmetric rock mound; smooth upstream, rough downstream Abrasion on upstream side; plucking on downstream Common in glaciated regions

B. Depositional Landforms:

Landform Description Formation
Moraine Deposits of glacial debris (till) Material carried by glacier deposited when ice melts
- Terminal/End Moraine Ridge at farthest extent of glacier Marks maximum advance of glacier
- Lateral Moraine Ridge along valley sides Debris falls from valley walls onto glacier edges
- Medial Moraine Ridge in center of glacier Two lateral moraines join when glaciers merge
- Ground Moraine Till sheet on valley floor Material deposited beneath glacier
Drumlin Egg-shaped hill; steep end faces upstream Glacier molds existing till into streamlined shape
Esker Long, winding ridge of sand and gravel Deposited by meltwater streams flowing in tunnels under glacier
Kame Irregular mound of stratified sand and gravel Deposited by meltwater at edge or in holes in glacier
Outwash Plain (Sandur) Flat plain of sorted glacial deposits Meltwater spreads sediment beyond glacier terminus
Erratic Large boulder different from local rock Transported long distance by glacier and deposited
🔑 Drumlins vs Eskers (Common UPSC confusion):

Drumlin:
  • Egg/whale-back shaped
  • Made of till (unsorted material)
  • Steep end faces direction glacier came from
  • Often found in groups ("basket of eggs" topography)

Esker:
  • Long, winding ridge (like a snake)
  • Made of stratified (sorted) sand and gravel
  • Formed by meltwater streams, not ice directly

8.5 Karst Landforms (Limestone Topography)

Karst topography develops in regions with soluble rocks (mainly limestone, also dolomite, gypsum) where groundwater dissolves the rock through chemical weathering (carbonation).

A. Surface Karst Features:

Feature Description Formation
Lapies/Karren Grooved and fluted rock surface Solution along joints and cracks creates ridges and grooves
Sinkhole/Doline Circular depression, 10-100m across Surface dissolves; or roof of underground cavity collapses
Uvala Larger depression formed by merging sinkholes Multiple dolines grow and coalesce
Polje Large flat-floored valley in karst Major structural depression; may flood seasonally
Swallow Hole/Ponor Point where surface stream disappears underground Stream enters cave system through enlarged joint
Blind Valley Valley that ends abruptly at rock face Stream disappears into swallow hole at valley end

B. Underground Karst Features:

Feature Description Formation
Caves/Caverns Underground chambers and passages Groundwater dissolves limestone along joints over thousands of years
Stalactite Icicle-shaped deposit hanging from cave ceiling Water drips → CO₂ escapes → CaCO₃ precipitates → Grows downward
Stalagmite Cone-shaped deposit rising from cave floor Water drips to floor → Same process → Grows upward
Column/Pillar Stalactite and stalagmite join Both grow until they meet in middle
Flowstone Sheet-like deposit on cave walls/floor Thin film of water deposits CaCO₃ as it flows
📝 Stalactite vs Stalagmite - Memory Trick:
• StalacTite has a T → hangs from Top (ceiling)
• StalagMite has an M → Mounds up from ground
• Or: StalacTITE = holds TIGHT to ceiling

Karst Regions in India:

  • Meghalaya: Best developed karst; Mawsmai Cave, Krem Liat Prah (longest cave)
  • Chhattisgarh: Kutumsar Cave, Kailash Cave (Bastar region)
  • Andhra Pradesh: Belum Caves, Borra Caves
  • Uttarakhand: Patal Bhuvaneshwar Cave
  • Maharashtra: Panchgani-Mahabaleshwar region

8.6 Coastal Landforms

Coastal landforms are created by the action of waves, tides, and currents along shorelines.

A. Erosional Coastal Landforms:

Landform Description Formation
Cliff Steep rock face at coast Wave erosion at base undermines cliff → Upper part collapses
Wave-Cut Platform Flat rock surface at cliff base As cliff retreats, flat platform left behind at wave level
Sea Cave Cave at base of cliff Wave erosion exploits weakness in rock (joint, fault)
Arch Natural rock bridge over water Caves on both sides of headland erode through and join
Stack Isolated rock pillar offshore Arch roof collapses → Isolated pillar remains
Stump Low rock left when stack erodes Stack continues to erode; only base remains
Blowhole Vertical hole in cliff top spraying water Cave roof erodes through; water forced up during waves

B. Depositional Coastal Landforms:

Landform Description Formation
Beach Accumulation of sand, gravel, shells along shore Waves deposit material in zone between high and low tide
Spit Narrow ridge of sand extending from land into water Longshore drift deposits material where coast changes direction
Bar Ridge of sand connecting two points or closing bay Spit grows across bay entrance
Tombolo Sand bar connecting island to mainland Deposition in wave shadow behind island
Lagoon Shallow water body cut off from sea by bar Bar encloses part of sea; water becomes calm and shallow
Barrier Island Long, narrow island parallel to coast Large-scale deposition offshore creates island

1. Consider the following statements about Mohorovičić Discontinuity:
1. It separates the crust from the mantle
2. P-wave velocity increases across it
3. It was discovered using seismic waves from an earthquake
Which of the above statements is/are correct?
A 1 only
B 1 and 2 only
C 2 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
Answer: D (All statements are correct)

• Statement 1: Moho discontinuity marks the boundary between Earth's crust and mantle at approximately 35 km depth (average).
• Statement 2: P-wave velocity increases from about 6.9 km/s in lower crust to 8 km/s in upper mantle.
• Statement 3: Andrija Mohorovičić discovered it in 1909 while studying seismic waves from a Croatian earthquake.
2. Barchan dunes are characterized by:
A Crescent shape with horns pointing upwind
B Crescent shape with horns pointing downwind
C Linear ridges parallel to wind direction
D Star-shaped with multiple arms
Answer: B

Barchan dunes are crescent-shaped sand dunes with horns (wings) pointing in the direction of wind flow (downwind). They form under unidirectional winds with limited sand supply. The windward slope is gentle while the leeward slope (slip face) is steep.
3. Which of the following pairs is/are correctly matched?

2. Drumlin - Glacial deposition
3. Yardang - Wind erosion
Select the correct answer:
A 1 only
B 1 and 2 only
C 2 and 3 only
D 1, 2 and 3
Answer: D (All are correctly matched)

• Cirque: Armchair-shaped hollow carved by glacial erosion on mountain side
• Drumlin: Egg-shaped hill of glacial till (depositional feature)
• Yardang: Long, parallel ridges carved by wind erosion in deserts
