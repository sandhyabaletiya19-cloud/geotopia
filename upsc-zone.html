Chapter 1: Origin of Earth & Universe

1.1 The Big Bang Theory

Big Bang Theory: The universe originated from an extremely hot, dense singularity approximately 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding ever since.

Key Concepts:

  • Singularity: All matter, energy, space compressed into infinitely small, hot, dense point
  • Expansion: Universe has been expanding since Big Bang; still accelerating
  • Cosmic Inflation: Rapid exponential expansion in first fraction of second
  • Matter Formation: As universe cooled, quarks → protons/neutrons → atoms

Evidence for Big Bang:

Evidence Description Discovered By
Red Shift Light from galaxies shifted to red; galaxies moving away Edwin Hubble (1929)
CMBR Cosmic Microwave Background Radiation at 2.7K from all directions Penzias & Wilson (1965)
Hubble's Law V = H₀ × D (velocity proportional to distance) Edwin Hubble (1929)
Element Abundance 75% Hydrogen, 25% Helium matches predictions Various

1.2 Formation of Solar System

Theory Proposed By Key Concept
Nebular Hypothesis Kant & Laplace (1755, 1796) Rotating nebula contracted → sun + planets
Planetesimal Theory Chamberlin & Moulton (1904) Passing star pulled material from sun
Tidal Theory Jeans & Jeffreys (1917) Larger star pulled cigar-shaped filament
Solar Nebular Model Modern (Accepted) Rotating disk of gas/dust ~4.6 billion years ago

Chapter 2: Interior of Earth

2.1 Sources of Information

Direct Sources:

  • Mining/Drilling: Deepest - Kola Superdeep Borehole, Russia (12.26 km)
  • Volcanic Eruptions: Bring material from upper mantle

Indirect Sources:

  • Seismic Waves: Most important source
  • Gravity Anomalies: Indicate density differences
  • Magnetic Field: Suggests liquid iron outer core
  • Meteorites: Primordial solar system material

2.2 Seismic Waves

Wave Nature Speed Medium
P-Waves Longitudinal (Push-Pull) 5-8 km/s (Fastest) Solid, Liquid, Gas
S-Waves Transverse (Side-to-side) 3-5 km/s Solid Only
L-Waves Surface (Love + Rayleigh) Slowest Surface - Most destructive
🔑 Key Discovery: S-waves cannot pass through liquid outer core - proof that outer core is liquid!

Shadow Zones:

  • P-Wave Shadow Zone: 103° to 142° from epicenter
  • S-Wave Shadow Zone: Beyond 103° (cannot pass through liquid core)

2.3 Layers of Earth

Layer Depth Density Composition
Continental Crust 0-35 km 2.7 g/cm³ SIAL (Silica + Alumina)
Oceanic Crust 0-7 km 3.0 g/cm³ SIMA (Silica + Magnesium)
Upper Mantle 35-670 km 3.4-4.4 g/cm³ Peridotite, Olivine
Lower Mantle 670-2900 km 4.4-5.6 g/cm³ Fe-Mg silicates
Outer Core 2900-5100 km 9.9-12.2 g/cm³ Iron + Nickel (LIQUID)
Inner Core 5100-6371 km 12.8-13.1 g/cm³ NIFE (SOLID)

2.4 Discontinuities

Discontinuity Depth Location Discovered By

Chapter 3: Plate Tectonics

3.1 Continental Drift Theory

Continental Drift: Proposed by Alfred Wegener (1912) - All continents were once joined as a supercontinent called Pangaea surrounded by Panthalassa ocean, which began breaking apart ~200 million years ago.

Evidence for Continental Drift:

Evidence Type Description
Jigsaw Fit Coastlines of South America & Africa fit together
Fossil Evidence Mesosaurus (freshwater reptile) found in both S. America & Africa
Geological Fit Rock formations & mountain ranges match across continents
Glacial Evidence Glacial deposits in tropical areas (India, Africa, S. America)
Paleoclimatic Coal deposits in Antarctica suggest warmer past climate
🔑 Limitation: Wegener couldn't explain the mechanism (force) behind continental movement. This was later explained by Sea Floor Spreading and Convection Currents.

3.2 Sea Floor Spreading

Sea Floor Spreading: Proposed by Harry Hess (1960s) - New oceanic crust is created at mid-ocean ridges and spreads outward, pushing continents apart.

Evidence:

  • Magnetic Stripes: Alternating magnetic polarity in ocean floor rocks on both sides of mid-ocean ridges
  • Age of Rocks: Oceanic rocks youngest near ridges, oldest near continents
  • Heat Flow: Higher heat flow at mid-ocean ridges
  • Sediment Thickness: Thinnest at ridges, thickest near continents

3.3 Plate Tectonics Theory

Developed in 1960s by McKenzie, Parker, Morgan. Earth's lithosphere is divided into rigid plates that float on the plastic asthenosphere.

Major Tectonic Plates (7):

Plate Area (Million km²) Type
Pacific Plate103.3Oceanic (Largest)
North American75.9Continental + Oceanic
Eurasian67.8Continental + Oceanic
African61.3Continental + Oceanic
Antarctic60.9Continental + Oceanic
Indo-Australian58.9Continental + Oceanic
South American43.6Continental + Oceanic

Minor Plates:

Arabian, Caribbean, Cocos, Juan de Fuca, Nazca, Philippine Sea, Scotia

3.4 Plate Boundaries

Type Movement Features Examples
Divergent Plates move apart Mid-ocean ridges, Rift valleys, New crust forms Mid-Atlantic Ridge, East African Rift
Convergent Plates move together Mountains, Trenches, Subduction, Island arcs Himalayas, Mariana Trench, Andes
Transform Plates slide past Faults, Earthquakes, No crust created/destroyed San Andreas Fault

Types of Convergent Boundaries:

Type Process Features Example
Oceanic-Oceanic One plate subducts Trench + Island Arc Japan, Philippines, Mariana
Oceanic-Continental Oceanic subducts Trench + Coastal Mountains Andes, Cascades
Continental-Continental Collision (no subduction) Fold Mountains Himalayas, Alps
⚡ UPSC Important:
• India is on Indo-Australian Plate (moving NE at 5 cm/year)
• Himalayas formed by collision of Indian & Eurasian plates (~50 MYA)
• Ring of Fire: Pacific Plate boundaries - 75% of world's volcanoes
• Driving force: Mantle Convection Currents

Chapter 4: Earthquakes

4.1 Basic Terminology

  • Earthquake: Sudden shaking of Earth's surface due to release of energy
  • Focus (Hypocenter): Point of origin inside Earth
  • Epicenter: Point on surface directly above focus
  • Seismograph: Instrument to record seismic waves
  • Seismology: Scientific study of earthquakes

4.2 Causes of Earthquakes

Type Cause Examples
Tectonic Plate movements; most common and destructive Most major earthquakes
Volcanic Magma movement; localized Near active volcanoes
Collapse Cave/mine roof collapse Mining areas
Reservoir-Induced Weight of water in large dams Koyna (1967), Hoover Dam
Explosion Nuclear tests, mining blasts Nuclear test sites

4.3 Earthquake Measurement

Scale Measures Range Notes
Richter Scale Magnitude (Energy) 1-10 (logarithmic) Each unit = 10× amplitude, 31.6× energy
Mercalli Scale Intensity (Damage) I-XII (Roman) Based on observed effects
Moment Magnitude Modern, accurate Open-ended Used for large earthquakes now
📝 Remember: Richter scale is logarithmic - a magnitude 7 earthquake is 10× stronger than magnitude 6 in terms of amplitude, and releases about 31.6× more energy!

4.4 Earthquake Zones in India

Zone Risk MSK Intensity Regions
Zone V Very High IX and above Kashmir, Himachal, Uttarakhand, NE States, Kutch, Andaman
Zone IV High VIII Delhi, UP, Bihar, N. Punjab, parts of Maharashtra
Zone III Moderate VII Kerala, Goa, Mumbai, Kolkata, parts of Rajasthan
Zone II Low VI Remaining Peninsular India
⚡ Major Indian Earthquakes:
• 1934 Bihar-Nepal: 8.1 magnitude
• 1950 Assam: 8.6 magnitude (one of largest recorded)
• 1993 Latur (Maharashtra): 6.2 magnitude, ~10,000 deaths
• 2001 Bhuj (Gujarat): 7.7 magnitude, ~20,000 deaths
• 2005 Kashmir: 7.6 magnitude, ~80,000 deaths

4.5 Global Distribution

Major Earthquake Belts:

  • Circum-Pacific Belt (Ring of Fire): 68% of earthquakes; Japan, Philippines, Indonesia, W. Americas
  • Mid-Atlantic Ridge: Divergent boundary earthquakes
  • Alpine-Himalayan Belt: 21% of earthquakes; Alps to Himalayas

6.5 Rock Cycle

Rock Cycle: Continuous process by which rocks are formed, broken down, and reformed. Any rock type can transform into any other type given appropriate conditions.

Processes: Weathering & Erosion → Transportation → Deposition → Lithification → Metamorphism → Melting → Crystallization

Chapter 7: Weathering

7.1 Definition

Weathering: In-situ disintegration and decomposition of rocks at or near Earth's surface, without transportation of material.

Weathering vs Erosion:

Weathering Erosion
No movementMaterial transported
Static processDynamic process
In situ breakdownRemoval + transport

7.2 Physical Weathering

Type Process Where Common
Frost Weathering Water freezes in cracks → expands 9% → widens cracks Cold regions, mountains
Thermal Expansion Rocks expand when heated, contract when cooled Hot deserts (large temp. range)
Exfoliation Concentric layers peel off (onion-skin) Hot deserts, granite regions
Salt Weathering Salt crystals grow in pores → pressure Coastal areas, arid regions
Pressure Release Overlying rock erodes → deep rock expands Exposed deep rocks

7.3 Chemical Weathering

Type Process Affects
Oxidation Minerals + Oxygen → oxides (rusting) Iron-bearing minerals
Carbonation CO₂ + Water → Carbonic acid → dissolves carbonates Limestone (karst features)
Hydration Water absorbed by minerals → swelling Anhydrite → Gypsum
Hydrolysis Silicates react with water → clay Feldspar → Kaolinite
Solution Minerals dissolve in water Rock salt, Gypsum
🔑 Key Point: Chemical weathering is most effective in hot and humid climates (tropical regions). Physical weathering dominates in arid and cold regions.

7.4 Biological Weathering

  • Plant Roots: Grow into cracks, widen them (physical)
  • Burrowing Animals: Mix soil, expose rock
  • Lichens & Mosses: Secrete acids (chemical)
  • Bacteria: Break down minerals

Practice MCQs - Physical Geography

Test Your Knowledge (UPSC Pattern)
1. Which discontinuity separates Earth's crust from mantle?
A Gutenberg
B Mohorovičić
C Lehmann
D Conrad
Answer: B - Moho discontinuity at ~35 km depth separates crust from mantle.
2. Which seismic waves cannot pass through liquid?
A P-Waves
B S-Waves
C L-Waves
D All of above
Answer: B - S-waves (Secondary) are transverse and cannot pass through liquids. This proved outer core is liquid.
3. India's only active volcano is:
A Barren Island
B Narcondam
C Dhinodhar
D Deccan Traps
Answer: A - Barren Island (Andaman) is India's only active volcano.
4. Barchan dunes have horns pointing:
A Upwind
B Downwind
C No specific direction
D Perpendicular to wind
Answer: B - Barchan horns point downwind (with wind). Parabolic horns point upwind.
5. Stalactites hang from cave:
A Ceiling
B Floor
C Walls
D Entrance
Answer: A - StalacTite = T = Top (ceiling). StalagMite = M = Mound (floor).
6. Which layer contains ozone?
A Troposphere
B Stratosphere
C Mesosphere
D Thermosphere
Answer: B - Stratosphere (12-50 km) contains the ozone layer which absorbs UV radiation.
7. Spring tides occur during:
A New & Full Moon
B First & Third Quarter
C Spring season only
D Summer solstice
Answer: A - Spring tides (highest) occur during New Moon and Full Moon when Sun-Earth-Moon are aligned.
8. Limestone metamorphoses into:
A Slate
B Marble
C Quartzite
D Gneiss
Answer: B - Limestone → Marble. Sandstone → Quartzite. Shale → Slate. Granite → Gneiss.
