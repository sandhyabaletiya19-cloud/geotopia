0001
Aa /ˈɑːɑː/
Geology Volcanology
Rough, jagged lava flow with sharp edges...
Diagram
Rough blocky lava surface

Full Definition

Aa (pronounced "ah-ah") is a type of basaltic lava flow characterized by a rough, jagged, clinkery surface composed of broken lava blocks called "clinkers." The term comes from Hawaiian, where it describes the painful experience of walking barefoot on this sharp, rough surface. Aa flows typically move slower than pahoehoe flows (10-100 meters per hour) and form when lava with higher viscosity or lower temperature flows down steeper slopes. The rough surface develops as the cooled outer crust breaks apart while the molten interior continues flowing. Aa lava is common in Hawaii, Iceland, and other volcanic regions worldwide.

Ablation /əˈbleɪʃən/
Glaciology
Loss of ice/snow from glacier via melting...
Process
Glacier ice loss diagram

Full Definition

Ablation refers to all processes that remove snow, ice, or water from a glacier or snowfield. This includes melting (most common), sublimation (ice to vapor), calving (icebergs breaking off), and wind erosion. The ablation zone is the lower part of a glacier where annual ice loss exceeds accumulation. Global warming has dramatically increased ablation rates worldwide, causing glacier retreat and contributing to sea level rise.

0003
Ablation Zone /əˈbleɪʃən zoʊn/
Glaciology
Lower glacier area where ice loss exceeds gain...
Zone
Below equilibrium line

Full Definition

The Ablation Zone is the lower region of a glacier where annual mass loss from melting, evaporation, and calving exceeds snow accumulation. Located below the equilibrium line altitude (ELA), this zone experiences net ice loss each year. Typically found at lower, warmer elevations, it displays features like meltwater streams, crevasses, and debris-covered ice surfaces.

Abrasion /əˈbreɪʒən/
Erosion Geology
Wearing of rock by friction from particles...
Process
Sandpaper effect on rock

Full Definition

Abrasion is the mechanical wearing away of rock surfaces through friction caused by rock particles carried by water, wind, ice, or gravity. Like natural sandpaper, transported sediments scrape against bedrock, smoothing and eroding surfaces over time. Types include glacial abrasion (striations), fluvial abrasion (river bed smoothing), aeolian abrasion (desert rock shaping), and coastal abrasion (wave action).

0005
Abrasion Platform /əˈbreɪʒən ˈplætfɔːrm/
Coastal
Flat rock surface cut by wave erosion at cliff base...
Landform
Wave-cut platform

Full Definition

Abrasion Platform (also called wave-cut platform or shore platform) is a flat or gently sloping rock surface created at the base of a sea cliff by wave erosion. As waves undercut the cliff through hydraulic action and abrasion, the cliff retreats landward, leaving behind this rocky platform. Typically found in the intertidal zone, it may extend hundreds of meters seaward and is often covered with rock pools and marine organisms.

Absolute Humidity /ˈæbsəluːt hjuːˈmɪdəti/
Meteorology
Actual water vapor mass in air (g/m³)...

Grams per cubic meter

Full Definition

Absolute Humidity is the total mass of water vapor present in a given volume of air, typically expressed in grams per cubic meter (g/m³). Unlike relative humidity, it doesn't depend on temperature—it's simply "how much water is actually in the air." Warm air can hold more water vapor than cold air. At 30°C, air can hold ~30g/m³; at 0°C, only ~5g/m³.

0007
Absolute Location /ˈæbsəluːt loʊˈkeɪʃən/
Geography
Exact position using coordinates (lat/long)...

Full Definition

Absolute Location is the exact position of a place on Earth using a coordinate system. The most common system uses latitude (distance north/south of equator, 0-90°) and longitude (distance east/west of Prime Meridian, 0-180°). Example: New York City is at 40.7128°N, 74.0060°W. Other systems include UTM coordinates, street addresses, and GPS coordinates. Absolute location never changes, unlike relative location which describes position in relation to other places.

0008
Abyssal Plain /əˈbɪsəl pleɪn/
Oceanography
Flat ocean floor at 3,000-6,000m depth...
Depth
3-6 km below surface

Full Definition

Abyssal Plain is an extremely flat region of the deep ocean floor, typically at depths of 3,000-6,000 meters. These are Earth's flattest surfaces, with slopes less than 1°, formed as sediments (clay, silt, organic matter) settle and bury the underlying oceanic crust over millions of years. Covering about 40% of the ocean floor, abyssal plains are found between continental margins and mid-ocean ridges. Despite extreme conditions (darkness, cold ~2°C, high pressure), specialized life exists here.

0009
Abyssal Zone /əˈbɪsəl zoʊn/
Oceanography Ecology
Ocean layer at 4,000-6,000m; dark, cold, high pressure...
Environment
No light, 2-4°C

Full Definition

The Abyssal Zone (from Greek "abyss" = bottomless) is the ocean layer between 4,000-6,000 meters depth. It's characterized by: complete darkness (aphotic), near-freezing temperatures (2-4°C), extreme pressure (400-600 atmospheres), low oxygen, and scarce food. Despite these conditions, unique life forms thrive here—bioluminescent fish, giant isopods, deep-sea corals, and bacteria that survive on "marine snow" (organic debris falling from above). Covers 83% of ocean area and 60% of Earth's surface.

Accretion /əˈkriːʃən/
Geology Coastal
Gradual growth by accumulation of material...
Process
Building up over time

Full Definition

Accretion is the gradual growth of land or objects through the accumulation of additional material. In geography: (1) Coastal accretion: beach grows seaward as sediment deposits; (2) Tectonic accretion: continents grow as oceanic terranes attach during subduction; (3) Planetary accretion: how Earth formed by collecting space debris 4.5 billion years ago. Opposite of erosion.
