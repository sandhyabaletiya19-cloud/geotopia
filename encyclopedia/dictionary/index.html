Aa /ˈɑːɑː/
Volcanology
Rough, jagged lava with sharp, clinkery surface formed from viscous flow...
Diagram
Blocky lava surface

Complete Definition

Aa (pronounced "ah-ah") is a type of basaltic lava flow characterized by a rough, jagged, clinkery surface composed of broken lava blocks called "clinkers." The term comes from Hawaiian, describing the painful experience of walking barefoot on this sharp surface. Aa forms when lava with higher viscosity or cooler temperature flows, causing the solidifying crust to break into angular fragments while the molten interior continues moving.

Ablation /əˈbleɪʃən/

Complete Definition

Ablation is the combined processes that remove snow, ice, or water from a glacier or ice sheet. The main ablation mechanisms include: melting (most significant), sublimation (ice directly becoming vapor), calving (icebergs breaking off), and wind erosion. The ablation zone is the lower part of a glacier where annual loss exceeds accumulation. Global warming has dramatically accelerated ablation rates worldwide.

0003
Ablation Zone /əˈbleɪʃən zoʊn/
Glaciology
Lower glacier region where annual ice loss exceeds accumulation; below ELA...
Location
Below equilibrium line

Complete Definition

The Ablation Zone is the lower region of a glacier where mass loss from melting, sublimation, and calving exceeds annual snow accumulation. Located below the Equilibrium Line Altitude (ELA), this zone experiences net ice loss each year. Features include meltwater streams, crevasses, supraglacial lakes, and often debris-covered surfaces. As climate warms, ablation zones expand upward, causing glacier retreat.

Abrasion /əˈbreɪʒən/
Erosion Geology
Mechanical wearing of rock surfaces by friction from transported particles...
Process
Sandpaper effect

Complete Definition

Abrasion is the mechanical wearing away of rock surfaces through friction caused by particles carried by water, wind, ice, or gravity—acting like natural sandpaper. Types include: Glacial abrasion (creates striations), Fluvial abrasion (smooths river beds), Aeolian abrasion (desert rock shaping), and Coastal abrasion (wave-driven erosion). It's one of the most powerful erosion processes shaping Earth's surface.

Absolute Humidity /ˈæbsəluːt hjuːˈmɪdəti/
Meteorology
Actual mass of water vapor in air, measured in grams per cubic meter (g/m³)...
Unit
g/m³ (grams per m³)

Complete Definition

Absolute Humidity is the total mass of water vapor present in a given volume of air, expressed in grams per cubic meter (g/m³). Unlike relative humidity (which is a percentage), absolute humidity is independent of temperature—it tells you exactly how much water is in the air. Warm air can hold more water vapor than cold air: at 30°C, air can hold ~30 g/m³; at 0°C, only ~5 g/m³.

The coldest possible temperature where all atomic motion ceases: -273.15°C or 0 Kelvin. At this point, atoms have minimum energy and entropy approaches zero. Outer space averages about 2.7K above absolute zero.
