Minerals

What are Minerals?

Minerals are naturally occurring, inorganic solid substances with a definite chemical composition and crystalline structure. They are the building blocks of rocks and are found throughout Earth's crust.

Characteristics of Minerals

  • Naturally Occurring: Formed by natural geological processes
  • Inorganic: Not derived from living organisms (with rare exceptions)
  • Solid: Have a definite volume and shape
  • Definite Chemical Composition: Can be expressed by a chemical formula
  • Crystalline Structure: Atoms arranged in an orderly, repeating 3D pattern

Physical Properties of Minerals

Color

The appearance of a mineral in reflected light

  • Can be misleading due to impurities
  • Same mineral can have different colors
  • Example: Quartz (clear, purple, pink, yellow)

Streak

Color of mineral powder when scraped across unglazed porcelain

  • More reliable than surface color
  • Consistent for a specific mineral
  • Example: Hematite (black surface, red streak)

Luster

How light reflects from mineral surface

  • Metallic: Shiny like metal (gold, pyrite)
  • Vitreous: Glassy (quartz)
  • Pearly: Pearl-like (talc)
  • Silky: Fibrous appearance (gypsum)
  • Dull: No shine (kaolinite)

Hardness

Resistance to scratching (Mohs Scale 1-10)

  • 1 = Talc (softest)
  • 5 = Apatite (knife blade)
  • 7 = Quartz (scratches glass)
  • 10 = Diamond (hardest)

Cleavage

Tendency to break along smooth, flat planes

  • Related to crystal structure
  • Can have 1, 2, 3, or more directions
  • Example: Mica (perfect 1-direction cleavage)

Fracture

Breaking pattern that isn't along cleavage

  • Conchoidal: Smooth, curved (glass-like)
  • Irregular: Rough, uneven
  • Splintery: Wood-like splinters

Crystal Form

External shape reflecting internal structure

  • Cubic (halite, pyrite)
  • Hexagonal (quartz, beryl)
  • Prismatic (tourmaline)
  • Needle-like (natrolite)

Specific Gravity

Density compared to water

  • Indicates mineral composition
  • Gold: 19.3 (very heavy)
  • Quartz: 2.65 (average)
  • Amber: 1.1 (light)

  • Hardness: 2.5-3

  • Uses: Jewelry, cutting tools, industrial applications

  • Hardness: 2.5-3

  • Major Deposits: Cerro Rico (Bolivia), Cannington (Australia), Fresnillo (Mexico)

  • Hardness: 4-4.5

  • Uses: Catalytic converters, jewelry, medical equipment

  • Minerals: Hematite, Magnetite, Goethite

Lithium

  • Minerals: Spodumene, Lepidolite

Uranium

  • Mineral: Uraninite (Pitchblende)
  • Top Producers: Kazakhstan, Canada, Australia, Namibia, Niger
  • Major Deposits: Cigar Lake (Canada), Olympic Dam (Australia)

Types of Rocks

What are Rocks?

Rocks are naturally occurring solid aggregates of one or more minerals or mineraloids. They are classified into three major groups based on their formation process: igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic.

Igneous Rocks

Formation: Crystallization of molten magma or lava

Global Distribution: Found worldwide, especially in volcanic regions, mid-ocean ridges, and areas of past volcanic activity

Intrusive (Plutonic) Igneous

Formation: Slow cooling beneath Earth's surface

Texture: Coarse-grained (visible crystals)

Examples & Locations:

  • Granite: USA (Yosemite), Brazil, India, China, Scotland
  • Diorite: Andes Mountains, USA, Norway, Canada
  • Gabbro: Bushveld Complex (South Africa), Skaergaard (Greenland)
  • Peridotite: Oman ophiolite, Norway, New Zealand

Extrusive (Volcanic) Igneous

Formation: Rapid cooling at Earth's surface

Texture: Fine-grained or glassy

Examples & Locations:

  • Basalt: Hawaii (USA), Iceland, India (Deccan Traps), Ethiopia
  • Rhyolite: Yellowstone (USA), New Zealand, Chile
  • Obsidian: Iceland, USA, Mexico, Japan, Kenya
  • Pumice: Italy, Greece, Turkey, New Zealand, Ecuador
  • Andesite: Andes Mountains, Japan, Indonesia, Philippines

Sedimentary Rocks

Formation: Lithification (compaction and cementation) of sediments

Global Distribution: Cover ~75% of Earth's land surface, found in all continents

Clastic Sedimentary

Formation: From weathered rock fragments

Classification: By grain size

Examples & Locations:

  • Sandstone: Australia (Uluru), USA (Colorado Plateau), India, Jordan (Petra)
  • Shale: USA (Appalachians), UK (Scottish Highlands), Argentina
  • Conglomerate: Spain, USA, South Africa
  • Breccia: Fault zones worldwide

Chemical Sedimentary

Formation: Precipitation from solution

Process: Evaporation or chemical reactions

Examples & Locations:

  • Limestone: USA (Indiana), China, Egypt (pyramids), France, Italy
  • Dolomite: Dolomite Alps (Italy), Canada, USA
  • Rock Salt (Halite): Dead Sea, Atacama Desert (Chile), Poland
  • Gypsum: USA (White Sands), Mexico, Iran
  • Chert/Flint: UK (Dover Cliffs), USA, Denmark

Organic Sedimentary

Formation: From organic material remains

Source: Plant or animal matter

Examples & Locations:

  • Coal: China, USA (Appalachia), India, Australia, Russia, Poland
  • Chalk: UK (White Cliffs of Dover), France, Denmark, Kansas (USA)
  • Diatomite: USA, China, Denmark, France
  • Oil Shale: Estonia, USA (Green River Formation), Brazil, China

Metamorphic Rocks

Formation: Transformation of existing rocks under heat and pressure

Global Distribution: Mountain belts, ancient continental shields, collision zones

Foliated Metamorphic

Characteristic: Layered or banded appearance

Cause: Directed pressure

Examples & Locations:

  • Slate: Wales (UK), Vermont (USA), Spain, Brazil
  • Phyllite: Appalachians (USA), Alps (Europe)
  • Schist: Scotland, Alps, Himalayas, Brazil
  • Gneiss: Norway, Canada (Canadian Shield), India, Finland

Non-Foliated Metamorphic

Characteristic: No layering

Cause: Uniform pressure or chemical changes

Examples & Locations:

  • Marble: Italy (Carrara), Greece, India (Taj Mahal), USA (Vermont)
  • Quartzite: USA, Brazil, South Africa, India
  • Hornfels: Contact zones worldwide
  • Serpentinite: California (USA), New Caledonia, Oman

The Rock Cycle

Understanding the Rock Cycle

The rock cycle is the continuous process by which rocks are created, changed, destroyed, and re-formed through geological processes. This cycle demonstrates that Earth's materials are constantly being recycled.

Key Processes in the Rock Cycle

  • Crystallization: Cooling and solidification of magma/lava → Igneous rocks
  • Weathering & Erosion: Breaking down rocks into sediments
  • Deposition: Settling of sediments
  • Compaction & Cementation: Sediments → Sedimentary rocks
  • Metamorphism: Heat and pressure → Metamorphic rocks
  • Melting: Extreme heat → Magma (restarts cycle)

Rock Cycle Diagram

Igneous Rocks

Formed from cooling magma/lava

↓ Weathering & Erosion

Sedimentary Rocks

Formed from sediment lithification

↓ Heat & Pressure

Metamorphic Rocks

Formed by transformation

↓ Extreme Heat (Melting)

Magma

Molten rock beneath surface

↑ Cooling & Crystallization ↑

Alternative Pathways

  • Igneous → Metamorphic (direct heat/pressure)
  • Sedimentary → Igneous (deep burial, melting)
  • Metamorphic → Sedimentary (uplift, weathering)
  • Any rock → Same type (continuing processes)

Global Examples

  • Iceland: Active cycle - basalt formation
  • Himalayas: Metamorphism from collision
  • Grand Canyon: Sedimentary deposition
  • Hawaii: Igneous rock formation

Timescales

  • Complete cycle: millions to billions of years
  • Fastest: Igneous formation (days to years)
  • Slowest: Metamorphism (millions of years)
  • Continuous process since Earth formed

Fossils

What are Fossils?

Fossils are the preserved remains, impressions, or traces of organisms from past geological ages. They provide crucial evidence for understanding the history of life on Earth and environmental changes over time.

Requirements for Fossilization

  • Rapid Burial: Quick coverage by sediment to protect from scavengers and decay
  • Hard Parts: Bones, shells, teeth more likely to fossilize than soft tissue
  • Lack of Oxygen: Anaerobic conditions slow decomposition
  • Time: Usually requires thousands to millions of years
  • Mineral Replacement: Original material replaced by minerals (petrification)

Types of Fossils

Body Fossils

Preserved remains of organism parts

  • Bones & Teeth: Most common vertebrate fossils
  • Shells: Marine invertebrates
  • Petrified Wood: Mineral replacement of plant tissue
  • Amber Inclusions: Insects preserved in tree resin

Trace Fossils (Ichnofossils)

Evidence of organism activity

  • Footprints: Dinosaur tracks, hominid prints
  • Burrows: Dwelling tunnels
  • Coprolites: Fossilized feces
  • Feeding Marks: Bite marks, borings

Mold & Cast Fossils

Impressions and fillings

  • External Mold: Impression of outside
  • Internal Mold: Cast of inside
  • Cast: Sediment fills mold cavity
  • Common for shells and leaves

Petrified Fossils

Mineral replacement process

  • Permineralization: Minerals fill pore spaces
  • Replacement: Original material replaced
  • Often preserves fine detail
  • Example: Petrified Forest (Arizona, USA)

Preserved Remains

Actual organism preserved

  • Ice: Mammoths in Siberia
  • Amber: Insects (Dominican Republic, Baltic)
  • Tar Pits: La Brea (California, USA)
  • Peat Bogs: Bog bodies (Europe)

Carbonization

Carbon film remains

  • Volatile elements escape, carbon remains
  • Common for plants and soft-bodied animals
  • Preserved as thin black/dark film
  • Example: Burgess Shale (Canada)

Index Fossils

Index fossils are used to determine the age of rock layers. They must be widespread geographically, abundant, easy to identify, and have existed for a relatively short geological time period.

Trilobites

  • Period: Cambrian to Permian (521-252 mya)
  • Location: Worldwide
  • Use: Paleozoic Era dating
  • Famous finds: Morocco, USA, Czech Republic, China

Ammonites

  • Period: Devonian to Cretaceous (400-66 mya)
  • Location: Worldwide marine rocks
  • Use: Mesozoic Era dating
  • Famous finds: UK, Germany, Madagascar, USA

Brachiopods

  • Period: Cambrian to present (540 mya-now)
  • Location: Worldwide
  • Use: Paleozoic stratigraphy
  • Still exist: But less common today

Graptolites

  • Period: Cambrian to Carboniferous (540-320 mya)
  • Location: Worldwide
  • Use: Early Paleozoic dating
  • Famous finds: Wales, Scotland, Scandinavia

Foraminifera

  • Period: Cambrian to present
  • Location: Marine environments worldwide
  • Use: Oil exploration, climate studies
  • Microscopic: But extremely abundant

Crinoids (Sea Lilies)

  • Period: Ordovician to present (485 mya-now)
  • Location: Worldwide shallow seas
  • Use: Paleozoic correlations
  • Famous: "Crinoidal limestone" formations

Geological Time Scale

Understanding Deep Time

The geological time scale divides Earth's 4.6 billion year history into eons, eras, periods, and epochs based on major geological and biological events. It provides a framework for understanding the timing and relationships of events in Earth's history.

Earth's Timeline Overview

  • Earth Formation: ~4.6 billion years ago
  • Oldest Rocks: ~4.0 billion years (Canada, Australia)
  • First Life: ~3.8-3.5 billion years ago (bacteria)
  • Oxygen Atmosphere: ~2.4 billion years ago (Great Oxidation Event)
  • Complex Life: ~600 million years ago
  • Land Plants: ~470 million years ago
  • Dinosaurs: 252-66 million years ago
  • Humans: ~2 million years ago (genus Homo)

  • First mammals (small, shrew-like)
  • Recovery from Permian extinction

  • Vast coal swamp forests
  • Giant insects (dragonflies 2ft wingspan)
  • First reptiles
  • Key Locations: USA, UK, Germany, China (coal deposits)

  • Age of Fishes
  • First tetrapods (land vertebrates)

  • Key Locations: Scotland, USA, Australia

  • First jawed fish
  • First land plants
  • Coral reefs expand

  • Marine life diversity explosion
  • First land plants (mosses)
  • Ends with mass extinction (ice age)

  • Cambrian Explosion (rapid animal diversification)
  • Trilobites abundant
  • First vertebrates (jawless fish)
  • Key Locations: Canada (Burgess Shale), China (Chengjiang)

PRECAMBRIAN (4.6 billion - 541 mya)

88% of Earth's History

  • Ediacaran Period (635-541 mya): First complex multicellular life
  • Cryogenian Period (720-635 mya): "Snowball Earth" glaciations
  • Great Oxidation Event (~2.4 billion years ago)
  • First eukaryotic cells (~2 billion years ago)
  • Key Locations: Australia (Ediacara Hills), Canada, Namibia

  • First life (bacteria, archaea) ~3.8 billion years ago
  • Stromatolites (bacterial colonies)
  • No oxygen in atmosphere
  • Key Locations: Australia (Pilbara), South Africa (Barberton)

Hadean Eon (4.6 - 4 billion years ago)

  • Earth forms
  • Moon-forming impact
  • Surface molten, no rocks preserved
  • Oceans form ~4.4 billion years ago

Dating Methods

Relative Dating

Determines sequence, not exact age

  • Superposition: Older layers below younger
  • Cross-cutting: Features cutting through rock are younger
  • Inclusions: Rock fragments inside are older
  • Fossil succession: Fossils appear in predictable order
  • Index fossils: Widely distributed, short time range

Radiometric Dating

Absolute ages from radioactive decay

  • Carbon-14: 0-50,000 years (organic material)
  • Potassium-Argon: 100,000+ years (volcanic rocks)
  • Uranium-Lead: 1 million - 4.5 billion years (zircon crystals)
  • Rubidium-Strontium: 10 million+ years (igneous/metamorphic)

Other Dating Methods

  • Dendrochronology: Tree rings (10,000+ years)
  • Varve counting: Annual lake sediment layers
  • Ice cores: Trapped air bubbles (800,000+ years)
  • Amino acid racemization: Protein decay in fossils
  • Thermoluminescence: Ceramics, burnt materials

Weathering & Erosion

Understanding Weathering

Weathering is the breakdown of rocks at or near Earth's surface. Erosion is the transport of weathered material. Together, they shape landscapes and create sediments that eventually become sedimentary rocks.

Weathering vs. Erosion

  • Weathering: Breaking down rocks in place (no movement)
  • Erosion: Movement of weathered material by water, wind, ice, or gravity
  • Both work together to reshape Earth's surface
  • Time scale: Can take thousands to millions of years

Physical (Mechanical) Weathering

Breaking rocks into smaller pieces without changing their chemical composition

Frost Wedging (Ice Wedging)

  • Water freezes in rock cracks
  • Ice expands ~9%, creating pressure
  • Repeated freeze-thaw cycles break rock apart
  • Common in: Mountains, polar regions
  • Examples: Alps, Rocky Mountains, Antarctica

Thermal Expansion

  • Rocks expand when heated, contract when cooled
  • Repeated heating/cooling causes stress
  • Outer layers may peel off (exfoliation)
  • Common in: Deserts
  • Examples: Sahara (Africa), Mojave (USA), Australian Outback

Biological Weathering

  • Tree roots grow into cracks, splitting rocks
  • Burrowing animals break up soil/rock
  • Lichens physically break rock surfaces
  • Common: Worldwide in vegetated areas
  • Examples: Angkor Wat (Cambodia), Amazon rainforest

Salt Crystal Growth

  • Saltwater seeps into rock pores
  • Water evaporates, salt crystals grow
  • Growing crystals exert pressure
  • Common in: Coastal areas, arid regions
  • Examples: Dead Sea coasts, Mediterranean, Persian Gulf

Pressure Release (Unloading)

  • Overlying rock removed by erosion
  • Underlying rock expands upward
  • Creates curved fractures (sheeting)
  • Examples: Half Dome (Yosemite, USA), Sugar Loaf (Brazil)

Abrasion

  • Rock particles carried by wind/water/ice
  • Scrape and wear down other rocks
  • Creates smooth, polished surfaces
  • Examples: Desert rocks, glacial valleys, river pebbles

Chemical Weathering

Chemical reactions that alter or dissolve rock minerals

Dissolution

  • Minerals dissolve in water
  • Especially effective on limestone, rock salt
  • Creates caves, sinkholes, karst landscapes
  • Examples: Carlsbad Caverns (USA), Mammoth Cave (USA), Stone Forest (China)

Oxidation

  • Oxygen reacts with iron in rocks
  • Creates rust (iron oxide)
  • Gives rocks red/brown color
  • Examples: Red rocks of Sedona (USA), Uluru (Australia), Jordan's deserts

Hydrolysis

  • Water reacts with minerals
  • Feldspar → Clay minerals
  • Major clay-forming process
  • Creates: Soil, clay deposits worldwide

Carbonation

  • CO₂ in rainwater forms carbonic acid
  • Reacts with limestone/marble
  • Dissolves calcium carbonate
  • Examples: Limestone landscapes worldwide, damaged stone monuments

Biological Chemical Weathering

  • Lichens/mosses produce acids
  • Plant roots release organic acids
  • Bacteria chemically alter minerals
  • Common: Tropical rainforests, temperate forests

Acid Rain

  • Industrial pollution + rainwater
  • Sulfuric/nitric acid in rain
  • Damages limestone buildings, statues
  • Affected areas: Europe, USA, China, India

Agents of Erosion

Water Erosion

Most powerful erosional force

  • Rivers: Grand Canyon (USA), Yangtze Gorges (China)
  • Waves: Coastal cliffs worldwide
  • Rainfall: Gully erosion, sheet erosion
  • Flooding: Rapid landscape change
  • Examples: Mississippi Delta (USA), Nile Delta (Egypt), Amazon Basin

Wind Erosion

Dominant in arid regions

  • Deflation: Removes fine particles
  • Abrasion: Sand-blasting effect
  • Creates: Sand dunes, desert pavements
  • Examples: Sahara (Africa), Arabian Desert, Gobi (Asia)

Glacial Erosion

Powerful sculptors of landscapes

  • Plucking: Ice pulls rock fragments
  • Abrasion: Rocks embedded in ice scrape bedrock
  • Creates: U-shaped valleys, fjords, cirques
  • Examples: Norway fjords, Alps, Yosemite Valley (USA), New Zealand

Gravity (Mass Wasting)

Direct downslope movement

  • Landslides: Rapid movement
  • Rockfalls: Free-falling rocks
  • Slumps: Rotational movement
  • Creep: Slow, gradual downhill
  • Examples: Himalayan landslides, California coastal slumps

Soil Formation

Soil is the result of weathering combined with organic matter. It forms in layers (horizons) over long periods.

Soil Forming Factors

  • Parent Material: Type of underlying rock
  • Climate: Temperature and rainfall
  • Organisms: Plants, animals, microbes
  • Topography: Slope and drainage
  • Time: Mature soil takes 1,000+ years

Soil Horizons

  • O Horizon: Organic matter (leaves, humus)
  • A Horizon (Topsoil): Minerals + organic matter
  • B Horizon (Subsoil): Accumulated minerals
  • C Horizon: Weathered parent material
  • R Horizon: Bedrock

Major Soil Types Worldwide

  • Laterite: Tropics (Brazil, Africa, India)
  • Chernozem: Grasslands (Ukraine, USA Great Plains)
  • Podzol: Coniferous forests (Canada, Russia, Scandinavia)
  • Desert Soil: Arid regions (Sahara, Middle East, Australia)
