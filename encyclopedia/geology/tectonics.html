Plate Tectonics

What is Plate Tectonics?

Plate tectonics is the scientific theory that Earth's outer shell (lithosphere) is divided into several plates that glide over the mantle, the rocky inner layer above the core. The theory explains the occurrence of earthquakes, volcanoes, and mountain formation, and how continents move over geological time.

Key Facts

  • Earth's lithosphere is broken into 7 major and many minor tectonic plates
  • Plates move at rates of 1-10 cm per year (about as fast as fingernails grow)
  • The theory was proposed by Alfred Wegener in 1912 (Continental Drift)
  • Modern plate tectonics theory was established in the 1960s
  • Plate movements are driven by mantle convection, ridge push, and slab pull

Types of Plate Boundaries

Divergent Boundaries

Description: Plates move apart, creating new crust

Features:

  • Mid-ocean ridges
  • Rift valleys
  • Volcanic activity
  • Shallow earthquakes

Examples:

  • Mid-Atlantic Ridge (Iceland)
  • East African Rift (Kenya, Ethiopia, Tanzania)
  • Red Sea Rift (Egypt, Saudi Arabia)

Convergent Boundaries

Description: Plates collide, destroying crust

Features:

  • Mountain ranges
  • Deep ocean trenches
  • Volcanic arcs
  • Deep earthquakes

Examples:

  • Himalayas (India-Asia collision)
  • Andes Mountains (South America)
  • Japan Trench (Pacific-Philippine plates)
  • Cascadia Subduction Zone (USA, Canada)

Transform Boundaries

Description: Plates slide past each other horizontally

Features:

  • Fault lines
  • Shallow earthquakes
  • No volcanic activity
  • Linear valleys

Examples:

  • San Andreas Fault (California, USA)
  • Alpine Fault (New Zealand)
  • Dead Sea Transform (Middle East)
  • North Anatolian Fault (Turkey)

Earthquakes

What Causes Earthquakes?

Earthquakes are caused by the sudden release of energy in Earth's crust that creates seismic waves. This energy is typically stored as stress in rocks along fault lines. When the stress exceeds the strength of the rocks, they break or slip, releasing energy in the form of seismic waves.

Tectonic Earthquakes

Most common type (90% of all earthquakes)

  • Caused by plate movements
  • Occur at plate boundaries
  • Can be very powerful (magnitude 9+)
  • Examples: Japan 2011, Chile 1960, Alaska 1964

Volcanic Earthquakes

Associated with volcanic activity

  • Caused by magma movement
  • Usually smaller magnitude
  • Precede volcanic eruptions
  • Common in: Iceland, Hawaii, Indonesia, Philippines

Induced Earthquakes

Caused by human activities

  • Mining operations
  • Reservoir filling (dams)
  • Fracking/oil extraction
  • Examples: Oklahoma (USA), UK fracking sites

Earthquake Anatomy

Key Terms

  • Focus (Hypocenter): The point within Earth where the earthquake originates
  • Epicenter: The point on Earth's surface directly above the focus
  • Fault: A fracture in Earth's crust along which movement has occurred
  • Seismic Waves: Energy waves that travel through Earth's layers
  • Aftershock: Smaller earthquake following the main shock
  • Foreshock: Smaller earthquake preceding the main shock

Types of Seismic Waves

P-Waves (Primary Waves)

  • Speed: Fastest (5-8 km/s)
  • Motion: Compression (push-pull)
  • Travel through: Solids, liquids, gases
  • First to arrive at seismograph stations
  • Damage: Generally minimal

S-Waves (Secondary Waves)

  • Speed: Slower than P-waves (3-5 km/s)
  • Motion: Shear (side-to-side)
  • Travel through: Solids only
  • Second to arrive at seismograph stations
  • Damage: More destructive than P-waves

Love Waves (Surface Waves)

  • Speed: Slower than body waves
  • Motion: Horizontal shearing
  • Travel: Along Earth's surface
  • Effect: Side-to-side ground motion
  • Damage: Very destructive to foundations

Rayleigh Waves (Surface Waves)

  • Speed: Slowest of all waves
  • Motion: Rolling/elliptical
  • Travel: Along Earth's surface
  • Effect: Up-down and back-forth motion
  • Damage: Most destructive to structures

Measuring Earthquakes

Richter Scale (1935)

Measures: Magnitude (energy released)

Scale: Logarithmic (0-10+)

Status: Largely replaced by Moment Magnitude

Limitation: Less accurate for large earthquakes

Moment Magnitude Scale (Mw)

Measures: Total energy released

Scale: Logarithmic (similar to Richter)

Status: Current standard worldwide

Advantage: Accurate for all sizes

Mercalli Intensity Scale

Measures: Observed damage and effects

Scale: I (not felt) to XII (total destruction)

Type: Qualitative assessment

Use: Damage assessment, insurance

Global Seismic Zones

What are Seismic Zones?

Seismic zones are geographic regions classified by their earthquake risk based on historical seismicity, tectonic setting, and geological conditions. Countries worldwide use seismic zoning maps to inform building codes, insurance rates, and disaster preparedness.

The Ring of Fire

The Circum-Pacific Belt, known as the "Ring of Fire," is a 40,000 km horseshoe-shaped zone where approximately 90% of the world's earthquakes and 75% of active volcanoes occur.

Ring of Fire Statistics

  • Length: ~40,000 kilometers (25,000 miles)
  • Earthquakes: ~90% of world's total
  • Volcanoes: 452 active volcanoes (75% of global total)
  • Population at risk: Over 500 million people

Volcanic Activity

Volcanoes and Plate Tectonics

Volcanoes are openings in Earth's crust through which molten rock, gases, and debris can escape. About 75% of the world's volcanoes are located along the Ring of Fire, where tectonic plates meet.

Global Volcano Statistics

  • Total volcanoes: ~1,500 potentially active worldwide
  • Currently active: ~50-70 erupting in any given year
  • Submarine volcanoes: Estimated 75% of all volcanic activity
  • People at risk: Over 800 million live within 100km of an active volcano

Earthquake Safety & Preparedness

Before an Earthquake

Prepare Your Home

  • Secure heavy furniture and appliances
  • Install latches on cabinets
  • Know how to shut off utilities
  • Repair structural defects
  • Store hazardous materials safely

Emergency Kit

  • Water (3-day supply, 1 gallon/person/day)
  • Non-perishable food
  • First aid kit and medications
  • Flashlight and batteries
  • Radio (battery or hand-crank)
  • Cash and important documents

Make a Plan

  • Identify safe spots in each room
  • Plan evacuation routes
  • Designate meeting points
  • Practice "Drop, Cover, Hold On"
  • Keep emergency contacts updated

During an Earthquake

DROP, COVER, and HOLD ON

  • DROP to your hands and knees
  • COVER your head and neck under a sturdy table or desk
  • HOLD ON until shaking stops
  • If indoors: Stay inside, away from windows
  • If outdoors: Move away from buildings, trees, powerlines
  • If driving: Pull over safely, stay in vehicle
  • If in bed: Stay there, cover head with pillow

After an Earthquake

Immediate Actions

  • Check for injuries
  • Inspect for damage
  • Expect aftershocks
  • If trapped, signal for help
  • Use text messages (not calls)
  • Stay away from damaged buildings

Safety Checks

  • Check for gas leaks
  • Inspect electrical systems
  • Look for structural damage
  • Clean up hazardous spills
  • Listen to emergency broadcasts

Do NOT

  • Use elevators
  • Light matches/lighters (gas risk)
  • Use phone except for emergencies
  • Enter damaged buildings
  • Touch downed power lines
  • Drive unless absolutely necessary
