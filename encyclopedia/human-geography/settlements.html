1 What is a Settlement?

A settlement is any place where people live and carry out daily activities. Settlements can range from a single isolated farmhouse to a massive megacity with millions of inhabitants.

🔑 Key Characteristics of Settlements

  • Population: The number of people living in the area
  • Infrastructure: Buildings, roads, water systems, electricity
  • Economic Activities: Jobs and industries that sustain the population
  • Social Organization: Community structure, governance, and services
  • Permanence: Whether people live there temporarily or permanently

Settlements have existed since humans transitioned from nomadic hunter-gatherer lifestyles to settled agricultural communities around 10,000 BCE. Today, over 55% of the world's population lives in urban settlements.

2 Classification of Settlements

Settlements are primarily classified into two broad categories based on their size, population density, and economic activities.

🌾 Rural Settlements

  • Population: Generally fewer than 10,000 people
  • Density: Low population density with open spaces
  • Economy: Primary activities — agriculture, fishing, forestry, mining
  • Services: Limited — basic shops, primary schools, small clinics
  • Examples: Hamlets, villages, farming communities

🏙️ Urban Settlements

  • Population: Generally more than 10,000 people
  • Density: High population density with compact housing
  • Economy: Secondary & tertiary — manufacturing, services, commerce
  • Services: Extensive — hospitals, universities, shopping centers
  • Examples: Towns, cities, metropolitan areas, megacities

3 Settlement Hierarchy

Settlements are organized in a hierarchy based on their population size, the range of services they provide, and their sphere of influence. Larger settlements offer more specialized services and attract people from a wider area.

🌆 Megacity > 10 million
🏙️ City 100K – 10M
🏘️ Town 10K – 100K
🏡 Village 1K – 10K
🏚️ Hamlet 100 – 1K
🏠 Isolated Dwelling < 100

📊 Services Increase with Hierarchy

  • Hamlet: Perhaps a small shop or pub
  • Village: Primary school, post office, church
  • Town: Secondary schools, supermarkets, hospital
  • City: Universities, specialist hospitals, theaters
  • Megacity: International airports, stock exchanges, headquarters of multinational corporations

4 Site and Situation

Two of the most important geographical concepts in understanding settlement location are site and situation.

📍 Site

Definition: The physical characteristics of the actual land on which a settlement is built.

Factors Influencing Site:

  • Water Supply: Proximity to rivers, lakes, or springs for drinking and agriculture
  • Defense: Hilltops, islands, river meanders for protection
  • Building Materials: Availability of stone, timber, or clay
  • Fertile Soil: Good agricultural land nearby
  • Drainage: Avoiding flood-prone or waterlogged areas
  • Shelter: Protection from harsh winds and weather
  • Flat Land: Easier construction and transportation

🗺️ Situation

Definition: The location of a settlement relative to its surrounding features and other settlements.

Factors Influencing Situation:

  • Transport Routes: Along major roads, railways, or sea routes
  • Market Access: Proximity to consumers and trading partners
  • Natural Resources: Near forests, mineral deposits, or fishing grounds
  • Other Settlements: Strategic position between major towns
  • Bridging Points: Lowest crossing point of a river
  • Route Junctions: Where multiple transport routes meet

🌟 Case Study: London, United Kingdom

Site: London was originally built on the north bank of the River Thames, which provided fresh water, fish, and transport. The area had gravel terraces above the flood plain, making it suitable for building.

Situation: London is located at the lowest bridging point of the Thames, making it a natural crossing point. It sits at the hub of road and rail networks connecting all parts of Britain, and its position near the English Channel made it ideal for trade with continental Europe.

5 Settlement Patterns

The settlement pattern refers to the spatial arrangement and distribution of buildings within a settlement. Three main patterns are recognized:

Nucleated Pattern

Description: Buildings are tightly clustered around a central focal point such as a church, village green, water source, or crossroads.

Reasons for formation: Defense, shared water supply, social cohesion, fertile valley surrounded by hills.

Examples: Traditional European villages, oasis settlements in deserts.

Linear Pattern

Description: Buildings are arranged in a line along a transportation route, river, coastline, or valley floor.

Reasons for formation: Limited flat land (e.g., narrow valleys), importance of transport route, coastal fishing villages.

Examples: Alpine valley villages, ribbon developments along main roads.

Dispersed Pattern

Description: Buildings are scattered across a wide area with significant gaps between individual dwellings.

Reasons for formation: Isolated farms, availability of abundant land, improved modern transport, pastoral farming.

Examples: American Midwest farms, Australian sheep stations, Scottish Highlands.
