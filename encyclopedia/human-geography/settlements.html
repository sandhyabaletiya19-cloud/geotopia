🏘️ What is a Settlement?
📍

Definition

A settlement is any place where people establish a community and live, ranging from isolated farmhouses to sprawling megacities.

🔑 Key Characteristics

Population
👥
Number of inhabitants
Infrastructure
🏗️
Buildings, roads, utilities
Economic Activities
💼
Agriculture, industry, services
Social Organization
🤝
Community structure

📚 Classification Criteria

Criteria Rural Urban
Population Size Usually < 10,000 Usually > 10,000
Population Density Low (dispersed) High (concentrated)
Primary Economic Activity Agriculture, fishing, forestry Industry, commerce, services
Built Environment Open spaces, scattered buildings Continuous built-up area
Services & Amenities Basic (limited shops, schools) Extensive (hospitals, universities)
Transportation Personal vehicles, limited public Extensive public transport
🏛️ Settlement Hierarchy

Settlements are organized in a hierarchy based on population size, range of services, and sphere of influence. Larger settlements provide more specialized services and attract people from wider areas.

📊 The Settlement Pyramid

🌆

Megacity

> 10 million people
  • Global economic centers
  • International airports & hubs
  • Stock exchanges, HQs
  • World-class universities
  • Diverse population
  • Examples: Tokyo (37M), Delhi (32M), Shanghai (29M)
🏙️

City

100,000 – 10 million
  • Regional centers
  • Major hospitals, universities
  • Theaters, museums, stadiums
  • Diverse employment
  • Public transport systems
  • Examples: Amsterdam, Melbourne, Vancouver
🏘️

Town

10,000 – 100,000
  • Secondary schools
  • District hospital
  • Shopping centers
  • Local government offices
  • Sports facilities
  • Examples: Small market towns, county seats
🏡

Village

1,000 – 10,000
  • Primary school
  • Place of worship
  • Small shops, post office
  • Community hall
  • Agricultural focus
  • Examples: Rural villages, small farming communities
🏚️

Hamlet

100 – 1,000
  • Very limited services
  • Perhaps a pub or small shop
  • No school or church (usually)
  • Clustered dwellings
  • Residents commute for services
  • Examples: Small clusters of houses
🏠

Isolated Dwelling

< 100 people
  • Individual farmhouse
  • Small family group
  • No services locally
  • Self-sufficient (agriculture)
  • Remote location
  • Examples: Lone farms, ranches, homesteads

📍 Site and Situation

Two critical geographical concepts explain why settlements are located where they are.

📍 SITE

Definition: The physical characteristics of the actual land on which a settlement is built.

Key Factors:

  • Water Supply: Proximity to rivers, lakes, springs for drinking, irrigation, and transport
  • Defense: Hilltops, islands, meanders provide protection from invaders
  • Building Materials: Availability of stone, timber, clay nearby
  • Fertile Soil: Good agricultural land to support population
  • Drainage: Avoiding flood-prone or waterlogged areas
  • Shelter: Protection from harsh winds and extreme weather
  • Flat Land: Easier for construction and infrastructure

🗺️ SITUATION

Definition: The location of a settlement relative to its surroundings and other places.

Key Factors:

  • Transport Routes: Along roads, railways, rivers, or coastlines
  • Market Access: Proximity to consumers and trading partners
  • Natural Resources: Near forests, mineral deposits, fishing grounds
  • Other Settlements: Strategic position between major towns
  • Bridging Points: Lowest river crossing or ford
  • Route Junctions: Where multiple transport routes meet
  • Political Boundaries: National or regional borders

🌟 Case Study: London, United Kingdom

🏛️

London's Geographic Advantages

Site: Built on gravel terraces north of the River Thames (above flood plain), providing fresh water, fish, and navigation. Roman bridge at narrowest point.

Situation: Lowest bridging point of Thames, gateway to continental Europe via English Channel, hub of road & rail networks, strategic for trade & defense.

🗺️ Settlement Patterns

The settlement pattern refers to the spatial arrangement and distribution of buildings and dwellings within a settlement. Three main patterns are recognized:

🏘️ Nucleated Pattern

Description: Buildings tightly clustered around a central focal point (church, village green, crossroads, water source).

Why it forms: Defense, shared resources, social cohesion, fertile valley surrounded by hills.

Examples: Medieval European villages, oasis settlements in deserts.

🛣️ Linear Pattern

Description: Buildings arranged in a line along a transport route (road, river, railway, coastline, valley floor).

Why it forms: Limited flat land (narrow valleys), importance of transport route, coastal fishing.

Examples: Alpine valley villages, ribbon development along main roads.

🌾 Dispersed Pattern

Description: Buildings scattered across a wide area with significant gaps between individual dwellings.

Why it forms: Isolated farms, abundant land, improved transport, pastoral farming.

Examples: American Midwest farms, Australian sheep stations, Scottish Highlands.

💼 Settlement Functions

Every settlement serves one or more functions — the economic and social roles it performs. Most large settlements are multifunctional.

🏘️

Residential

Housing & Communities
  • Primary function: providing homes
  • Suburbs, housing estates
  • Dormitory towns (commuter settlements)
  • Examples: Levittown (USA), Milton Keynes (UK)
🏭

Industrial

Manufacturing & Production
  • Factories, production facilities
  • Often near raw materials or ports
  • Historically in coalfield areas
  • Examples: Detroit (cars), Sheffield (steel)
🛍️

Commercial

Trade & Business
  • Shopping centers, markets
  • Business districts (CBDs)
  • Banks, offices, retail
  • Examples: Dubai, Hong Kong, Singapore
🏛️

Administrative

Government & Civic
  • Capital cities, county towns
  • Government offices, courts
  • Often planned settlements
  • Examples: Canberra, Brasília, Naypyidaw
⛱️

Recreational

Tourism & Leisure
  • Tourist resorts, spa towns
  • Coastal, mountain, or heritage sites
  • Hotels, attractions, entertainment
  • Examples: Las Vegas, Cancún, Queenstown
🎓

Educational

Learning & Research
  • University towns
  • Student population dominates
  • Research centers, laboratories
  • Examples: Oxford, Cambridge, Heidelberg
⛪

Religious

Pilgrimage & Faith
  • Holy sites, pilgrimage centers
  • Monasteries, religious communities
  • Seasonal population spikes
  • Examples: Mecca, Jerusalem, Varanasi
🚢

Port / Transport

Trade & Logistics
  • Seaports, river ports
  • Transport hubs, airports
  • Warehousing, distribution
  • Examples: Rotterdam, Los Angeles, Felixstowe

🏙️ Urban Land Use Models

Geographers have developed models to explain how land use is organized within cities. These are simplified representations that help us understand urban structure.

🎯 Burgess Concentric Zone Model (1925)

📍

Concept

Cities grow outward from the CBD in a series of concentric rings. Developed by Ernest Burgess while studying Chicago in the 1920s.

Zone Description Characteristics
1. CBD Central Business District Shops, offices, entertainment • High land values • Daytime population peak
2. Transition Inner City Zone Old housing, light industry • Mixed-use • Often run-down • Immigrants
3. Working Class Low-cost Housing Terraced houses • Close to factories • Factory workers
4. Middle Class Better Residential Semi-detached houses • Gardens • Professionals, managers
5. Commuter Zone Suburbs Detached houses • Green spaces • Affluent • Commute to CBD

⚠️ Limitations:

  • Assumes flat land with no physical barriers (rivers, hills)
  • Doesn't account for influence of transport routes
  • Based on 1920s American cities — may not apply globally
  • Ignores cultural, historical, and political factors

🎯 Hoyt Sector Model (1939)

📍

Concept

Cities develop in wedge-shaped sectors along transport routes. Proposed by Homer Hoyt after studying 142 American cities.

Sector Land Use Explanation
CBD Central Business District Heart of the city
Industrial Factories & Warehouses Develops along railways, roads, canals
Low-income Poor Housing Near industry (pollution, noise, cheaper land)
Middle-income Moderate Housing Better quality, away from industry
High-income Expensive Housing Most desirable locations — higher ground, views, away from pollution

✅ Advantages:

  • Recognizes the importance of transport routes
  • Explains why similar land uses cluster in sectors
  • More realistic than simple concentric rings
