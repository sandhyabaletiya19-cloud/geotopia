Understanding the Science

What is Biogeography?

Biogeography is the scientific study of the distribution of species and ecosystems across geographic space and through geological time. It integrates ecology, evolutionary biology, geology, palaeontology, and climatology to answer one deceptively profound question: Why do organisms live where they do?

The field was born from the voyages of Alexander von Humboldt, who in the early 19th century observed that vegetation follows predictable patterns with altitude and latitude. Alfred Russel Wallace then formalized biogeographic realms — noting that Bali and Lombok, separated by just 35 km, harbour entirely different faunas — what we now call the Wallace Line.

Modern biogeography employs satellite remote sensing, molecular phylogenetics, species distribution models, and plate tectonic reconstructions to map life's history across 4.5 billion years of Earth's surface. It explains why marsupials dominate Australia, why the Amazon holds one in ten known species, and why oceanic islands foster explosive adaptive radiation.

Today, with 1 million species facing extinction (IPBES Global Assessment, 2019), biogeography has become indispensable — guiding where to protect, how to restore, and how to anticipate the reshuffling of life as Earth's climate transforms.

Core Branches

Three Pillars of the Discipline

Biogeography is a broad field divided into complementary branches, each illuminating a different dimension of life's distribution across space and time.

🕰️

Historical Biogeography

Examines how plate tectonics, glaciations, sea-level changes, and mountain-building events have shaped species distributions over millions of years. Explains Gondwanan flora in southern continents, the lemurs of Madagascar, and the unique marsupial fauna of Australia. Uses fossil records, molecular clocks, and vicariance analysis to reconstruct past distributions and lineage origins.

🌡️

Ecological Biogeography

Focuses on present-day environmental drivers — climate, topography, soil, and species interactions — that determine where organisms can persist. Encompasses island biogeography theory (MacArthur & Wilson, 1967), species-area relationships, latitudinal diversity gradients, and dispersal mechanisms. Explains patterns of diversity from equator to poles and from lowland to alpine zones.

🛡️

Conservation Biogeography

The applied frontier: uses biogeographic principles to combat the biodiversity crisis. Guides protected area design, identifies irreplaceable hotspots, predicts range shifts under climate scenarios, and plans wildlife corridors. With the 30×30 global target — protecting 30% of Earth by 2030 — this branch directly shapes international conservation policy and funding priorities.

Why It Matters

The Global Importance of Biogeography

In an era of unprecedented environmental change, biogeography provides the scientific framework for understanding, predicting, and protecting life on our planet.

🛡️

Conservation Planning & Protected Areas

Biogeography identifies regions of exceptional richness and endemism — the 36 biodiversity hotspots that harbour over 50% of all plant species on just 2.5% of Earth's land. It informs the design of 300,000+ protected areas using principles of island biogeography, gap analysis, and systematic reserve selection. The Global 200 Ecoregions (WWF) and Key Biodiversity Areas (KBAs) are entirely biogeographic constructs, now embedded in national and international policy.

🌡️

Climate Change & Species Range Shifts

Species Distribution Models (SDMs) — rooted in biogeographic theory — predict how organisms will respond to warming. Current science projects that one in six species faces extinction risk under RCP 8.5 warming (Urban, 2015). Biogeography identifies climate refugia and guides the design of connectivity corridors such as the Yellowstone-to-Yukon (Y2Y) Initiative spanning 3,200 km, allowing species to track suitable climates northward and upslope.

🦠

Disease Ecology & Pandemic Preparedness

The geography of zoonotic disease spillover is a biogeographic problem. Habitat fragmentation, wildlife trade routes, and biodiversity loss all alter the spatial relationships between pathogen reservoirs and human populations. Biogeography maps high-risk interfaces between wildlife, livestock, and humans — providing the spatial intelligence for early warning systems against future pandemics, as highlighted by COVID-19 and successive Ebola outbreaks.

🌾

Food Security & Crop Wild Relatives

The Vavilov Centres of Origin — biogeographic regions where crop species were first domesticated — contain wild relatives essential for breeding climate-resilient varieties. Global agriculture depends on only 12 plant species for 75% of food supply. Mapping the biogeography of crop wild relatives across their native ranges is critical for maintaining the genetic diversity needed to feed a world of 10 billion people by 2050.

💰

Ecosystem Services & Economic Value

Earth's ecosystems provide services valued at $125–145 trillion per year (Costanza et al., 2014) — pollination, water purification, carbon sequestration, flood attenuation, and climate regulation. Biogeography maps where these services originate and how biodiversity loss erodes them. Tropical forests alone store 250 billion tonnes of carbon, making their protection — guided by biogeographic data — a primary global climate strategy.

🧭

Invasive Species & Biosecurity

Invasive alien species cost the global economy over $423 billion annually (IPBES Invasives Assessment, 2023) and are the primary driver of island extinctions. Biogeography explains why certain species become invasive in some contexts but not others — a question about the match between organismal traits and geographic context. This understanding underpins national biosecurity systems and international trade regulations governing biological movement.

Founding Minds

Pioneers of Biogeography

The visionaries whose expeditions, observations, and theoretical leaps built the foundations of biogeographic science across three centuries.

🧭

Alexander von Humboldt

1769 – 1859

Father of biogeography. First to describe systematic vegetation zones by altitude and latitude. His Cosmos envisioned nature as one interconnected living whole — a revolutionary synthesis that inspired Darwin, Wallace, and Thoreau.

🗺️

Alfred Russel Wallace

1823 – 1913

Co-discoverer of natural selection by correspondence with Darwin. Defined the world's six biogeographic realms. The Wallace Line — separating Asian and Australasian faunas — remains a cornerstone of zoogeography.

🐢

Charles Darwin

1809 – 1882

Galápagos observations revealed how geographic isolation drives explosive speciation. On the Origin of Species (1859) fused biogeography and evolutionary theory into a unified framework that still underpins the science today.

🏝️

MacArthur & Wilson

1960s

Robert MacArthur and E.O. Wilson's Theory of Island Biogeography (1967) quantified the relationship between island area, isolation, and species richness — transforming reserve design and conservation biology forever.
