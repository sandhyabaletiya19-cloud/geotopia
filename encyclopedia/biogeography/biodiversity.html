Understanding the Concept

What is a Biodiversity Hotspot?

From simple explanations to scientific definitions

Simple Definition

Biodiversity hotspots are special areas on Earth with lots of unique plants and animals that can't be found anywhere else — and these places are disappearing fast due to human activities.

Think of them as nature's treasure chests that are in danger of being lost forever.

Scientific Definition

A biodiversity hotspot is a biogeographic region characterized by exceptional levels of plant endemism and serious levels of habitat loss. To qualify as a hotspot, a region must meet two strict criteria:

  • Contain at least 1,500 species of vascular plants as endemics (>0.5% of world's total)
  • Have lost at least 70% of its primary native vegetation

Etymology

Bio Greek "bios" = Life
Diversity Latin "diversus" = Variety, Difference
Hotspot Area of high activity or concentration
Academic Deep Dive

Scientific Criteria & Research

Understanding the methodology behind hotspot identification

1

Endemic Species Requirement

The region must contain at least 1,500 species of vascular plants found nowhere else on Earth. This threshold represents approximately 0.5% of the world's total plant species diversity.

≥1,500
Endemic plant species required
2

Habitat Loss Threshold

The region must have lost at least 70% of its original natural habitat, indicating severe threat and urgent conservation need. This makes the area a priority for protection.

≥70%
Original habitat lost

Key Scientific References

Myers, N. (1988) — "Threatened biotas: 'hot spots' in tropical forests." The Environmentalist, 8(3), 187-208. First identification of 10 tropical forest hotspots.
Myers, N., et al. (2000) — "Biodiversity hotspots for conservation priorities." Nature, 403(6772), 853-858. Expanded analysis identifying 25 hotspots.
Mittermeier, R.A., et al. (2011) — "Global biodiversity conservation: the critical role of hotspots." Biodiversity Hotspots, 3-22. Updated to 36 hotspots.
Formation & Origin

How Hotspots Form

The natural and human processes that create biodiversity hotspots

Geographic Isolation

Mountains, islands, and other geographic barriers separate populations, allowing unique species to evolve independently over millions of years.

Climate Specialization

Stable climates over long periods allow species to become highly adapted to specific conditions, creating unique ecological niches.

Evolutionary Radiation

When organisms colonize new environments, they rapidly diversify to fill available ecological roles, creating many new species.

Human Impact Acceleration

Deforestation, agriculture, urbanization, and climate change rapidly destroy habitats, making remaining biodiversity-rich areas even more critical.

Historical Timeline

Evolution of the Hotspots Concept

Key milestones in biodiversity hotspot identification and conservation

1988

The Beginning

Norman Myers publishes groundbreaking paper identifying first 10 tropical forest hotspots

1996

Expansion

Conservation International expands the concept to 17 hotspots including Mediterranean-type ecosystems

2000

Major Update

Myers et al. publish Nature paper expanding to 25 hotspots with strict scientific criteria

2005

Further Additions

Additional hotspots recognized, bringing total to 34 including Japan and Horn of Africa

2011

Current 36

List finalized at 36 hotspots covering 2.4% of Earth's land surface

2024

Active Conservation

Over $2 billion invested in hotspot conservation through CEPF and partners
