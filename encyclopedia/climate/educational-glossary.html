🤔 Climate vs Weather: The Key Difference

☁️

Weather

Day-to-day atmospheric conditions at a specific place and time.

Timescale
Minutes to Days
  • "It's raining today"
  • "Tomorrow will be sunny"
  • "The temperature is 25°C right now"
🌍

Climate

Long-term average of weather patterns for a region.

Timescale
30+ Years
  • "London is mild and wet"
  • "The Sahara is a desert"
  • "Summers average 25°C here"

The Classic Analogy

Weather is what you wear today. Climate is what's in your wardrobe.

Or: Climate is your personality. Weather is your mood.

📖 Climate Glossary (A-Z)

Albedo ▼

The fraction of incoming solar radiation reflected by a surface. Ranges from 0 (absorbs all) to 1 (reflects all). Fresh snow has high albedo (~0.9), ocean water has low albedo (~0.06). Changes in albedo due to ice melt create positive feedback loops.

Anthropogenic ▼

Caused or produced by human activity. "Anthropogenic climate change" refers to human-caused global warming, primarily from burning fossil fuels and deforestation.

Arid ▼

A climate or region with very little precipitation, typically less than 250mm per year. Evaporation exceeds precipitation. Deserts are arid regions.

Biome ▼

A major ecological community characterized by dominant plant forms and climate. Examples include tropical rainforest, desert, tundra, taiga, and grassland. Biomes are determined primarily by temperature and precipitation patterns.

Carbon Cycle ▼

The circulation of carbon between atmosphere, oceans, land, and living things. Carbon moves through photosynthesis, respiration, decomposition, ocean absorption, and fossil fuel formation/combustion. Human activities have disrupted this cycle by releasing stored carbon.

Climate Sensitivity ▼

The amount of warming expected from doubling atmospheric CO₂. Current estimates: 2.5-4°C. Includes feedbacks like water vapor, ice-albedo, and cloud effects.

Coriolis Effect ▼

The apparent deflection of moving objects (including air and water) caused by Earth's rotation. Objects deflect to the right in Northern Hemisphere, left in Southern. Creates hurricane spin, prevailing wind directions, and ocean gyre circulation.

Drought ▼

Extended period of abnormally low precipitation. Types include meteorological (lack of rain), agricultural (soil moisture deficit), hydrological (low water supplies), and socioeconomic (water demand exceeds supply).

El Niño ▼

Periodic warming of the eastern Pacific Ocean near the equator. Part of ENSO cycle. Causes worldwide weather disruptions: drought in Australia/Indonesia, floods in Peru, reduced Atlantic hurricanes, warmer global temperatures.

Feedback (Climate) ▼

A process that amplifies (positive) or dampens (negative) an initial change. Positive: Ice melts → Less reflection → More warming → More ice melts. Negative: Warmer → More evaporation → More clouds → More reflection → Cooler.

Greenhouse Effect ▼

The process by which certain gases in Earth's atmosphere trap heat. Without it, Earth would average -18°C instead of +15°C. The "enhanced" greenhouse effect from human emissions is causing additional warming.

IPCC ▼

Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change. UN body that assesses climate science. Produces comprehensive reports every 5-7 years reviewing thousands of studies. Won Nobel Peace Prize 2007. Current report: AR6 (2021-2023).

Köppen Classification ▼

The most widely used climate classification system, developed by Wladimir Köppen in 1884. Divides climates into 5 main groups (Tropical, Dry, Temperate, Continental, Polar) with 30 subtypes based on temperature and precipitation patterns.

Lapse Rate ▼

The rate at which temperature decreases with altitude. Environmental lapse rate averages 6.5°C per 1000m. Dry adiabatic rate: 10°C/km. Saturated adiabatic rate: 5-6°C/km (slower because condensation releases heat).

Monsoon ▼

Seasonal reversal of prevailing winds, causing distinct wet and dry seasons. Caused by differential heating of land and ocean. The South Asian monsoon provides 80% of India's rainfall and supports 1.5 billion people.

Permafrost ▼

Ground that remains frozen for at least two consecutive years. Found in Arctic regions and high mountains. Contains ~1,500 billion tonnes of carbon - twice the atmosphere. Thawing permafrost releases CO₂ and methane, creating a dangerous feedback loop.

Radiative Forcing ▼

The change in energy balance at Earth's surface due to a factor like greenhouse gases. Measured in watts per square meter (W/m²). Positive forcing = warming, negative = cooling. Current total human forcing: ~2.7 W/m².

Sea Level Rise ▼

Increase in global mean sea level due to thermal expansion of warming water and melting of land-based ice. Currently ~3.4mm/year (accelerating). Could reach 0.3-1m by 2100 depending on emissions. Multi-meter rise committed long-term.

Tipping Point ▼

A threshold beyond which a system undergoes rapid, potentially irreversible change. Examples: ice sheet collapse, Amazon dieback, permafrost thaw, coral reef death. Some may be crossed between 1.5-2°C warming.

🚫 Climate Myths Debunked

❌ "Climate has always changed"

Truth: Yes, but past changes took thousands of years and were driven by orbital variations. Current change is 10-100x faster and caused by human CO₂ emissions.

❌ "It's the sun"

Truth: Solar output has been flat or slightly declining since 1980 while temperatures rose. The "fingerprint" of warming (stratospheric cooling) proves it's greenhouse gases, not the sun.

❌ "Scientists disagree"

Truth: 97-99% of climate scientists agree humans are causing warming. Every major scientific organization worldwide concurs. The "debate" is manufactured.

❌ "CO₂ is plant food"

Truth: While plants use CO₂, the negative effects of warming (drought, heat, pests) outweigh CO₂ fertilization. Crop yields are declining in many regions.

❌ "It's cold today, so no warming"

Truth: Weather ≠ climate. Local cold snaps still occur in a warming world. What matters is the long-term global average, which is clearly rising.

❌ "Models are unreliable"

Truth: Climate models have accurately predicted warming since the 1970s. They're based on well-understood physics and validated against observations.
