Waterfalls of Bharat

India is home to over 500 major waterfalls, predominantly concentrated in the Western Ghats, Eastern Ghats, Northeast hills, and the Himalayan foothills. Each waterfall tells a story of geological upheaval, river determination, and the raw power of water.

🏆 India's 5 Highest Waterfalls

#1
Kunchikal Falls
📍 Shimoga District, Karnataka
India's tallest waterfall and one of the highest in Asia. Formed by the Varahi River plunging down a massive cliff in the Western Ghats, Kunchikal is largely inaccessible to tourists as it falls within the restricted zone of the Mani Dam hydroelectric project. The falls cascade in multiple tiers through dense tropical forest. During the monsoon season (June–September), the water volume increases dramatically, creating a thunderous spectacle visible from distant vantage points. The surrounding Agumbe region receives some of the highest rainfall in India, fueling this magnificent cascade.
🌊 Varahi River ⛰️ Tiered Plunge 🌧️ Monsoon Peak
455 metres
#2
Barehipani Falls
📍 Simlipal National Park, Mayurbhanj, Odisha
The second tallest waterfall in India, Barehipani drops in two magnificent tiers within the pristine Simlipal Biosphere Reserve. Fed by the Budhabalanga River, the upper tier is a sheer free-fall of about 259 metres, followed by a shorter cascade. The falls are surrounded by one of India's finest sal and tropical forests, home to tigers, elephants, and the rare mugger crocodile. The Simlipal massif creates its own microclimate, ensuring the falls remain impressive even during drier months. The trek to Barehipani passes through stunning forest corridors and tribal villages of the Ho and Santhal communities.
🌊 Budhabalanga River ⛰️ Two-tiered 🐅 Simlipal Reserve
399 metres
#3
Nohkalikai Falls
📍 Cherrapunji (Sohra), East Khasi Hills, Meghalaya
The tallest plunge waterfall in India, Nohkalikai plummets 340 metres from the edge of the Khasi Hills plateau into a stunning emerald-green pool below. Located near Cherrapunji, one of the wettest places on Earth, the falls are spectacularly full during monsoons but maintain flow year-round. The name "Nohkalikai" translates to "Jump of Ka Likai" — a tragic Khasi legend of a woman who leapt from the cliff after discovering her second husband had killed and cooked her daughter. The plunge pool below displays an otherworldly blue-green color created by mineral-rich water. The viewpoint offers panoramic views of the Bangladeshi plains far below, creating one of the most dramatic landscapes in South Asia.
🌊 Unnamed Stream ⛰️ Single Plunge 🌧️ Wettest on Earth
340 metres
#4
Dudhsagar Falls
📍 Goa–Karnataka Border, Bhagwan Mahavir Wildlife Sanctuary
The "Sea of Milk" — Dudhsagar is a four-tiered cascade on the Mandovi River, straddling the Goa–Karnataka border. The milky-white appearance during peak flow gives it its evocative name. At 310 metres, it is among the tallest and most beautiful waterfalls in India. A railway track runs across the falls, creating one of the most photographed scenes in Indian railroading. The falls lie within the Bhagwan Mahavir Wildlife Sanctuary and Mollem National Park, surrounded by deciduous forests that turn brilliantly green during monsoons. The base pool is popular for swimming, and treks from Castle Rock or Kulem station are iconic monsoon adventures. The falls are a designated protected area with restricted access during heavy monsoons.
🌊 Mandovi River ⛰️ Four-tiered 🚂 Railway Crossing
310 metres
#5
Jog Falls (Gerosoppa Falls)
📍 Sagar Taluk, Shimoga District, Karnataka
One of India's most iconic waterfalls, Jog Falls is created by the Sharavathi River dropping 253 metres in four distinct cascades named Raja (King), Rani (Queen), Rover, and Rocket — each with its own character. The Raja falls in a single majestic column; the Rani cascades gracefully; the Rover twists through rocks; and the Rocket shoots out with tremendous force. Before the Linganamakki Dam was built upstream, Jog was a year-round spectacle. Today, it reaches its full glory during the monsoon when dam gates are opened. The 1,400-step trek to the base is a rewarding adventure. The British termed it "Gerosoppa Falls." The Karnataka government has developed extensive viewpoints, a geological park, and eco-tourism facilities. The falls are a designated National Geological Monument.
🌊 Sharavathi River ⛰️ Segmented (4 cascades) 🏛️ Nat. Geo. Monument
253 metres

Lakes of Bharat

India harbors over 200 notable lakes spanning every imaginable type — freshwater, brackish, saline, glacial, crater, oxbow, and artificial reservoirs. These waterbodies are vital ecosystems, cultural landmarks, and economic lifelines.

🌿
A. Natural Lakes
Freshwater, brackish, saline, and unique natural waterbodies formed by tectonic, fluvial, or coastal processes
🌊 Wular Lake
📍 Bandipora, Jammu & Kashmir
Freshwater

India's largest freshwater lake by area, formed tectonically in the Kashmir Valley. Fed by the Jhelum River and surrounded by the Himalayas, Wular is a critical flood absorption basin, a Ramsar Wetland, and sustains fishing communities. The Mughal Emperor Zain-ul-Abidin built a grand palace (Zaina Lank) on an island here. The lake supports migratory birds from Central Asia and Siberia including the endangered Brahminy duck and white-eyed pochard.

Area189 km²
Depth14 m (max)
Elevation1,580 m
StatusRamsar Site
🦩 Chilika Lake
📍 Puri, Khordha & Ganjam, Odisha
Brackish

Asia's largest brackish water lagoon and India's largest coastal lake. A pear-shaped waterbody connected to the Bay of Bengal via a narrow channel, Chilika is home to the Irrawaddy dolphins, over 160 species of fish, and hosts over a million migratory birds annually from the Caspian Sea, Baikal, and Siberia — including flamingos, white-bellied sea eagles, and bar-headed geese. The lake has a central island called Nalabana (Bird Island). It is a Ramsar site and was once a major ancient port.

Area1,165 km²
Depth4.2 m (avg)
Birds1 Million+/yr
StatusRamsar Site
🏝️ Loktak Lake
📍 Bishnupur, Manipur
Freshwater

The largest freshwater lake in Northeast India, famous for its floating "phumdis" — heterogeneous masses of vegetation, soil, and organic matter. These floating islands support the world's only floating national park — Keibul Lamjao, the last natural habitat of the endangered Sangai deer (Eld's deer/brow-antlered deer). Local fishermen build floating huts called "phumshongs" on phumdis. The lake is vital for Manipur's economy, providing water, hydropower, fisheries, and irrigation.

Area287 km²
UniqueFloating Phumdis
WildlifeSangai Deer
StatusRamsar Site
💎 Pangong Tso
📍 Leh, Ladakh (extends to Tibet)
Saline

An endorheic high-altitude salt lake stretching 134 km across the India-China border — one-third lies in India, two-thirds in Tibet. At 4,350 metres elevation, its waters display mesmerizing color shifts from azure to turquoise to deep blue depending on sunlight and minerals. Despite being saline, it freezes completely in winter. The lake gained worldwide fame through the Bollywood film "3 Idiots." It is a strategically important region near the Line of Actual Control. Breeding ground for bar-headed geese and brahminy ducks.

Length134 km
Elevation4,350 m
TypeEndorheic Saline
Trans-borderIndia–China
🏔️ Tso Moriri
📍 Changthang, Ladakh
Oligosaline

A high-altitude lake at 4,522 metres in the Changthang Plateau of Ladakh. One of the highest lakes in India, Tso Moriri is the largest high-altitude lake entirely within India (28 km long). Surrounded by barren mountains and the Changthang Wildlife Sanctuary, it supports the Changpa nomadic pastoralists who rear Pashmina goats. The lake is a Ramsar Wetland and breeding ground for the black-necked crane, bar-headed goose, and great crested grebe. In winter, temperatures plummet to -40°C.

Area120 km²
Elevation4,522 m
Depth40 m (max)
StatusRamsar Site
🦩 Pulicat Lake
📍 Andhra Pradesh & Tamil Nadu Border
Brackish

The second largest brackish water lagoon in India after Chilika, Pulicat lies on the Coromandel Coast at the AP–TN border. Connected to the Bay of Bengal via the Buckingham Canal and a narrow bar mouth. The lagoon is separated from the sea by Sriharikota Island — home to India's rocket launch centre (ISRO). Pulicat Bird Sanctuary hosts thousands of flamingos, pelicans, herons, and painted storks. The Dutch established a fort and trading post here in the 17th century.

Area759 km²
Depth1.5 m (avg)
IslandSriharikota
BirdsFlamingo Hub
🛶 Vembanad Lake
📍 Kottayam, Alappuzha & Ernakulam, Kerala
Brackish

The longest lake in India (96.5 km) and the largest lake in Kerala. Vembanad is the lifeline of the Kerala backwaters — an interconnected network of canals, rivers, and lagoons. The famous Nehru Trophy Boat Race (Vallam Kali) is held here at Punnamada. Kuttanad, the rice bowl of Kerala and one of the few places in the world where farming occurs below sea level, borders the lake. The Thanneermukkom Bund divides the lake into fresh and saline sections. Rich in aquatic biodiversity and vital for fishing communities.

Area2,033 km²
Length96.5 km
FamousBoat Race
StatusRamsar Site
🌸 Dal Lake
📍 Srinagar, Jammu & Kashmir
Freshwater

The "Jewel in the crown of Kashmir" — an urban lake of extraordinary beauty in the heart of Srinagar. Known for its ornately carved houseboats (originally built by the British who were forbidden to own land), floating gardens called "Rad" for vegetable cultivation, and the famous shikara boats. The Mughal gardens of Nishat Bagh and Shalimar Bagh grace its shores. The Char Chinar island with its four iconic chinar trees is a beloved landmark. Despite pollution challenges, Dal remains one of the most romantic destinations in the world.

Area18 km²
Elevation1,583 m
FamousHouseboats
IconChar Chinar
🧂 Sambhar Lake
📍 Jaipur, Rajasthan
Saline

India's largest inland saline lake and the country's most important source of salt, producing 196,000 tonnes annually — about 8.7% of India's salt production. The lake is an elliptical shallow basin occupying a depression between parallel ridges of the Aravalli Hills. During winter it hosts flamingos and other migratory waterfowl. The Sambhar Salt Lake was designated a Ramsar site. Local legend attributes its creation to Shakambhari Devi. The lake's ecosystem supports unique halophilic organisms.

Area230 km²
Salt/yr196,000 tonnes
TypeInland Saline
StatusRamsar Site
☄️ Lonar Crater Lake
📍 Buldhana, Maharashtra
Crater

One of only four known impact craters in basaltic rock on Earth, created approximately 50,000 years ago by a meteorite impact. The nearly circular lake sits in a crater 1.8 km in diameter and 150 m deep. The water is simultaneously saline and alkaline — a unique combination rarely found. In 2020, the lake turned pink due to Haloarchaea microorganisms. The lake supports microorganisms similar to those found on Mars, making it of interest to NASA. Multiple ancient temples dot the crater rim, including the Daitya Sudan temple.

Diameter1.8 km
Age~50,000 yrs
pH10.5 (alkaline)
StatusNat. Geo Heritage
🐦 Kolleru Lake
📍 West Godavari & Krishna, Andhra Pradesh
Freshwater

One of India's largest freshwater lakes, situated between the Krishna and Godavari river deltas. Kolleru acts as a natural flood-balancing reservoir between these two great river systems. Declared a Wildlife Sanctuary, it hosts grey pelicans, painted storks, and open-billed storks. The lake was historically much larger but has shrunk due to encroachment for aquaculture. The Atapaka Bird Sanctuary within the lake is a critical breeding ground for migratory waterfowl.

Area308 km²
RiversKrishna–Godavari
RoleFlood Basin
StatusRamsar Site
🗿 Hussain Sagar
📍 Hyderabad, Telangana
Artificial

Built in 1563 CE during the reign of Ibrahim Quli Qutb Shah, this heart-shaped lake connects the twin cities of Hyderabad and Secunderabad. A 16-metre tall monolithic Buddha statue (350 tonnes, carved from a single granite rock) stands on Gibraltar Rock island in the lake's center. The Tank Bund road along its edge features 33 statues of notable Telugu personalities. The lake hosted the 2003 National Sailing Championship and is a major urban recreation zone.

❄️ Gurudongmar Lake
📍 North Sikkim
Glacial

One of the highest lakes in the world at 5,183 metres, located near the Tibetan border in North Sikkim. Sacred to both Buddhists and Hindus — named after Guru Padmasambhava (Guru Rinpoche), who is believed to have blessed the lake so a portion never freezes even in winter (when temperatures drop to -25°C). The crystal-clear lake reflects the snow-capped peaks of Kanchenjunga range. Access requires a special Inner Line Permit.

🪷 Tsomgo (Changu) Lake
📍 East Sikkim
Glacial

A glacial lake at 3,753 metres on the Gangtok–Nathula highway. "Tsomgo" means "source of the lake" in Bhutia. The lake surface reflects different colors through seasons — frozen white in winter, emerald in spring, blue in summer. Surrounded by steep mountains with rhododendrons and primulas. Sacred to local Sikkimese who hold the Guru Purnima festival here. Yak rides are popular among tourists.

💀 Roopkund Lake
📍 Chamoli, Uttarakhand
Glacial

The famous "Skeleton Lake" — a high-altitude glacial lake containing the remains of approximately 600-800 human skeletons dating from the 9th century CE. At 5,029 metres in the lap of Trishul peak, the bones are visible when the snow melts. DNA studies revealed people of South Asian and Mediterranean-Greek origin. The most accepted theory suggests they were killed by a sudden, severe hailstorm while on a pilgrimage to the Nanda Devi Raj Jat festival. The Roopkund trek is one of India's most popular Himalayan treks.

Elevation5,029 m
Mystery600+ Skeletons
Diameter40 m
Period~9th Century
🕉️ Satopanth Lake
📍 Chamoli, Uttarakhand
Glacial

A triangular-shaped glacial lake at 4,600 metres, sacred in Hindu mythology. The three corners are believed to be the seats of Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva. Located below the Satopanth and Bhagirath Kharak glaciers near Badrinath. The 25-km trek from Mana village passes through alpine meadows and moraines. The lake has crystal-clear emerald-green water fed by glacial melt.

⛩️ Hemkund Sahib
📍 Chamoli, Uttarakhand
Glacial

A sacred glacial lake at 4,329 metres, surrounded by seven snow-capped peaks forming a ring of mountains. The Sikh Gurudwara Hemkund Sahib on its shore is one of the highest gurudwaras in the world — where Guru Gobind Singh is believed to have meditated in a previous life. A Hindu Lakshman Temple also exists here. Accessible only June–October, the trek from Govindghat is strenuous but spiritually rewarding. The Valley of Flowers National Park is nearby.

🤍 Tso Kar
📍 Rupshu Valley, Ladakh
Hypersaline

The "White Lake" — named for the white salt efflorescence on its shores. A fluctuating high-altitude salt lake at 4,530 metres in the Rupshu Valley. Once a much larger lake that has been shrinking for millennia. The salt was historically traded along ancient caravan routes to Tibet. Tso Kar is a Ramsar site and an important breeding ground for the black-necked crane — one of the rarest crane species on Earth. The Changpa nomads camp near the lake with their yak and Pashmina goat herds.

C. Artificial Reservoirs
Major man-made reservoirs created by dams for irrigation, hydropower, drinking water, and flood control
🏛️ Govind Ballabh Pant Sagar
📍 Sonbhadra, Uttar Pradesh
Reservoir

India's largest artificial lake by area, created by the Rihand Dam across the Rihand River (tributary of the Sone River) in 1962. Named after India's first Deputy PM, the reservoir provides water for the Singrauli Super Thermal Power Station and irrigation. The lake spans the UP–Madhya Pradesh border and has developed into a significant fishery. The surrounding Sonbhadra region is rich in minerals including gold deposits.

Area468 km²
DamRihand Dam
RiverRihand (Sone)
Year1962
💧 Indira Sagar Reservoir
📍 Khandwa, Madhya Pradesh
Reservoir

India's largest reservoir by volume (12.22 billion m³), created by the Indira Sagar Dam on the Narmada River. Completed in 2005, it is a key component of the Narmada Valley Development Project. The dam generates 1,000 MW of hydroelectric power and irrigates 2.5 lakh hectares. The reservoir submerged 249 villages, leading to major displacement controversies (Narmada Bachao Andolan by Medha Patkar). The lake has become an important fishery supporting local livelihoods.

🌅 Hirakud Reservoir
📍 Sambalpur, Odisha
Reservoir

Created by the Hirakud Dam — the longest earthen dam in the world (25.8 km including dykes) — built across the Mahanadi River. Completed in 1957, it was one of independent India's first major multipurpose river valley projects. The reservoir stretches 55 km and is surrounded by forests home to gharials, mugger crocodiles, and migratory birds. The dam has tamed the devastating floods that historically ravaged the Mahanadi delta, provides irrigation to Sambalpur, and generates 347 MW of hydroelectric power.

Power347 MW
🏗️ Nagarjuna Sagar
📍 Nalgonda, Telangana & Guntur, AP
Reservoir

One of the world's largest masonry dams, built on the Krishna River between 1955–1967. Named after the Buddhist scholar Nagarjuna. The reservoir irrigates over 10 lakh acres across Telangana and Andhra Pradesh. The Nagarjunakonda island (now partially submerged) was a major Buddhist center in the 2nd–3rd centuries CE, with archaeological remains relocated to a museum on the island. The dam generates 815.6 MW of hydropower and is an engineering marvel of post-independence India.

⚡ Gobind Sagar
📍 Bilaspur, Himachal Pradesh
Reservoir

Created by the Bhakra Dam — India's highest gravity dam (226 m) — on the Sutlej River. Named after Guru Gobind Singh, the reservoir stores 9.34 billion m³ of water. Jawaharlal Nehru called Bhakra Dam a "Temple of Resurgent India." The dam transformed Punjab and Haryana into India's breadbasket through extensive canal irrigation. It generates 1,325 MW of hydroelectric power. The reservoir also supports commercial fishing and is a scenic destination with the Himalayan foothills as backdrop.

Area168 km²
Dam Height226 m
RiverSutlej
Power1,325 MW
🌾 Stanley Reservoir (Mettur)
📍 Salem, Tamil Nadu
Reservoir

One of the oldest and largest reservoirs in India, created by the Mettur Dam across the Cauvery River, completed in 1934. Named after Sir George Stanley, the then Governor of Madras. The dam is the lifeline of Tamil Nadu's delta agriculture — its annual opening on June 12th is celebrated as a festival. The reservoir irrigates millions of acres of paddy fields in the Thanjavur delta (the "Rice Bowl of Tamil Nadu") and generates hydroelectric power. The reservoir's capacity is 93.47 TMC.

Fascinating Facts

🌊
Widest Waterfall
Chitrakote Falls in Chhattisgarh on the Indravati River is called the "Niagara of India" — with a horseshoe shape spanning nearly 300 metres in width during monsoons, making it the widest waterfall in India.
🏝️
Only Floating National Park
Keibul Lamjao National Park in Loktak Lake, Manipur is the world's only floating national park. The floating "phumdis" support the critically endangered Sangai deer — found nowhere else on Earth.
☄️
Meteorite Lake
Lonar Crater Lake in Maharashtra was formed 50,000 years ago by a meteorite impact. It's the only hyper-velocity impact crater in basaltic rock on Earth. NASA studies its microorganisms for Mars analogues.
💀
Lake of Skeletons
Roopkund Lake at 5,029m in Uttarakhand contains 600-800 human skeletons from the 9th century. DNA reveals people of South Asian and Mediterranean origin, likely killed by a catastrophic hailstorm.
🚂
Railway Through a Waterfall
The Konkan Railway track passes directly in front of Dudhsagar Falls in Goa — creating one of India's most spectacular train journeys. During monsoons, trains are sometimes delayed as water flows across the tracks.
🌸
Lake Turned Pink
In June 2020, Lonar Lake in Maharashtra turned pink overnight due to a bloom of Haloarchaea — salt-loving microorganisms. The phenomenon attracted worldwide media attention and scientific curiosity.
🧂
India's Salt Factory
Sambhar Lake in Rajasthan produces nearly 9% of India's total salt output (196,000 tonnes/year). Salt extraction here has continued for over 2,500 years, making it one of the oldest salt-producing sites in the world.
⛪
Highest Place of Worship
Hemkund Sahib Gurudwara on the shores of the glacial Hemkund Lake at 4,329m is one of the highest places of worship in the world. It remains accessible only 4 months a year due to extreme snow.
🌾
Below Sea Level Farming
Kuttanad region near Vembanad Lake in Kerala is one of the few places on Earth where farming happens below sea level (-2.2m). Known as the "Rice Bowl of Kerala," it uses an ingenious system of bunds and drainage.
