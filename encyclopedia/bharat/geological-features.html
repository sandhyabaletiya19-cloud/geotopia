Geology & Rock Formations

The Ancient Story Written in Stone

India's geological heritage spans over 3.8 billion years — from the oldest Archaean gneisses of the Peninsular Shield to the youngest alluvium of the Indo-Gangetic Plains. The subcontinent is a living museum of Earth's history, bearing witness to continental drift, volcanic cataclysms, and the rise of the mightiest mountain range on the planet.

Section 01

Geological Time Periods in India

India's rock record is one of the most complete on Earth, spanning from the earliest Archaean eon to the present Quaternary period. Each era left its signature in stone, mineral, and fossil.

💡 Did You Know?

The Indian Peninsular Shield contains some of the oldest rocks on Earth. The gneisses and granites of Karnataka and Jharkhand date back to 3.8 billion years, nearly as old as the planet's oldest known rocks in Canada and Greenland.

Oldest

Archaean Eon

4,000 – 2,500 Million Years Ago

The oldest rock formations in India. The Archaean system forms the basement complex of the Indian Peninsula, consisting primarily of gneisses, schists, and granites. These rocks form the core of the Peninsular Shield and are found extensively in Karnataka (Dharwar System), Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, Odisha, Tamil Nadu, and Andhra Pradesh. They are highly metamorphosed and folded, containing some of the oldest mineral deposits — including gold, iron ore, manganese, and chromite. The Dharwar System is the type area, exposed in the valleys of the Krishna and Tungabhadra rivers.

Gneiss Schist Granite Quartzite Marble
Ancient

Proterozoic Era (Purana Group)

2,500 – 540 Million Years Ago

This era witnessed the formation of two major rock systems: the Cuddapah System and the Vindhyan System. The Cuddapah rocks, found in the crescent-shaped basin of Andhra Pradesh, consist of shales, limestones, quartzites, and sandstones — rich in asbestos, barytes, iron, manganese, and diamonds (Kurnool area). The Vindhyan System stretches across central India from Rajasthan to Bihar, forming the great plateau region. Famous for its red sandstone (used in Mughal architecture), the Vindhyan rocks also yield diamonds from the Panna mines. The Great Boundary Fault separates Vindhyan rocks from the Aravallis.

Sandstone Limestone Shale Dolomite Diamond-bearing
Transition

Palaeozoic Era (Dravidian)

540 – 252 Million Years Ago

During this era, India was part of the supercontinent Gondwana. The Cambrian rocks are found in the Salt Range of Punjab (now Pakistan) and parts of Kashmir, containing trilobite fossils. Ordovician to Silurian marine deposits occur in the Spiti Valley of Himachal Pradesh. The most significant development is the formation of the Gondwana System in the late Palaeozoic (Carboniferous–Permian), which hosts India's vast coal reserves. The Gondwana formations stretch along river valleys of the Damodar, Mahanadi, Godavari, and Narmada, preserving plant fossils like Glossopteris that proved continental drift theory.

Coal Measures Tillite Glossopteris Flora Marine Fossils
Age of Reptiles

Mesozoic Era

252 – 66 Million Years Ago

The Mesozoic saw India's dramatic journey northward after breaking away from Gondwana ~130 million years ago. Triassic rocks occur in Spiti, Kumaon, and the Rajmahal Hills. Jurassic marine sediments are found in Kutch (Gujarat) and Rajasthan, containing rich ammonite and belemnite fossils — Kutch is world-famous for its Jurassic fossil beds. Cretaceous deposits mark the beginning of the massive Deccan Volcanic Province. Dinosaur fossils have been found in Gujarat (Balasinor — "India's Jurassic Park"), Madhya Pradesh (Jabalpur — where first Indian dinosaur eggs were discovered), and Maharashtra. The Lameta Formation preserves nesting sites of titanosaurs.

Ammonites Basalt Dinosaur Eggs Lameta Beds Rajmahal Traps
Recent

Cenozoic Era

66 Million Years Ago – Present

The Cenozoic era shaped modern India. The collision of the Indian plate with the Eurasian plate (~50 Ma) thrust up the Himalayas — a process still continuing today at ~5 cm/year. The Siwalik Hills (Tertiary sediments) along the Himalayan foothills preserve a spectacular fossil record of ancient mammals — including early apes (Ramapithecus), elephants, giraffes, and horses. The Indo-Gangetic Alluvial Plains formed as rivers deposited sediment eroded from the rising Himalayas. The Quaternary period brought glaciation in Kashmir and Ladakh, laterite formation in peninsular India, and the development of the Thar Desert. The youngest geological features include the coastal formations, raised beaches, and coral reefs of Lakshadweep.

Section 02

Major Rock Types

India exhibits all three fundamental rock categories in magnificent diversity — from the volcanic basalts of the Deccan Plateau to the metamorphic gneisses of the ancient shield and the sedimentary bounty of the great river basins.

🌋 Primary / First-formed

Igneous Rocks

Formed from the cooling and solidification of magma or lava. India's igneous rocks range from the ancient granites of the Peninsular Shield (3+ billion years old) to the relatively young Deccan Traps basalts (~66 million years). They constitute about 27% of India's surface area.

Basalt (Deccan Traps)
Covers ~500,000 km² across Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP, Karnataka. Up to 2,000m thick. Formed during massive volcanic eruptions at K-Pg boundary. Creates fertile black cotton soil (regur).
Granite
Found across the Peninsular Shield — Bundelkhand massif, Closepet granite (Karnataka), Singhbhum (Jharkhand). Used extensively in construction. Rajasthan & Tamil Nadu are major producers.
Gabbro & Dolerite
Intrusive igneous rocks found as dykes and sills across the Deccan region and Singhbhum. Rich in iron and magnesium minerals. Important for road aggregates.
Rajmahal Traps
Volcanic basalts in Jharkhand-Bengal, older than Deccan Traps (~118 Ma). Associated with India's separation from Australia-Antarctica.
Major Distributions
Maharashtra Gujarat Madhya Pradesh Karnataka Rajasthan Tamil Nadu Jharkhand Andhra Pradesh
🏜️ Layered / Stratified

Sedimentary Rocks

Formed by accumulation and cementation of sediments — transported by water, wind, or ice. These rocks cover about 75% of India's surface area and are economically the most important, containing coal, petroleum, natural gas, limestone, and phosphorites. They preserve the fossil record that tells the story of life on this land.

Vindhyan Sandstone & Limestone
Red and buff sandstones used in iconic monuments (Red Fort, Fatehpur Sikri). Extends from Rajasthan to Bihar. Contains stromatolites (earliest life evidence in India). 1,400-550 Ma old.
Gondwana Coal Measures
India's primary coal-bearing formations (98% of coal reserves). Found in rift basins of Damodar, Mahanadi, Godavari, Satpura. Includes sandstone, shale, and coal seams up to 30m thick.
Indo-Gangetic Alluvium
Youngest sediments — Quaternary. Up to 6,000m deep. Includes bhangar (old alluvium), khadar (new alluvium), bhabar, terai, and deltaic deposits. Most fertile agricultural land.
Siwalik Conglomerates & Sandstones
Tertiary molasse deposits along the Himalayan foothills. Famous for mammalian fossils. Divided into Lower (mudstones), Middle (sandstones), and Upper (conglomerates) Siwaliks.
Major Distributions
Indo-Gangetic Plains Vindhyan Region Gondwana Basins Kutch Siwalik Hills Cuddapah Basin Coastal Areas
💎 Transformed / Recrystallized

Metamorphic Rocks

Formed when existing rocks are transformed by heat, pressure, or chemical activity without melting. India's metamorphic rocks are among the most ancient, forming the core of the Peninsular Shield and the high-grade terrains of the Eastern and Western Ghats. They host significant mineral wealth including precious stones and industrial minerals.

Gneiss (Peninsular Gneissic Complex)
The most widespread metamorphic rock in India. Forms the basement of the Peninsular Shield. Found across Karnataka, Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Jharkhand. Ages range from 3.3 to 2.5 billion years.
Schist (Dharwar Schist Belt)
Green-schist to amphibolite facies rocks containing gold (Kolar Gold Fields), iron ore, and manganese. Major belts in Karnataka (Shimoga, Dharwar, Chitradurga) and Jharkhand (Singhbhum).
Marble
Metamorphosed limestone. Famous deposits at Makrana (Rajasthan) — the marble used for the Taj Mahal. Also found in Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Andhra Pradesh. India is a major marble exporter.
Slate, Phyllite & Quartzite
Slate found in Kangra (HP), Rewari (Haryana). Quartzite forms ridges across Rajasthan (Aravalli), Jharkhand, and the Eastern Ghats. Used in construction and as dimension stone.
Major Distributions
Aravalli Range Eastern Ghats Chota Nagpur Plateau Dharwar Belt Greater Himalayas Nellore Belt
Section 03

Geological Wonders

India's geological landscape includes some of the most remarkable formations on Earth — from the world's largest continental flood basalt province to ancient sedimentary basins that preserve billions of years of planetary history.

🌋
Volcanic Plateau

Deccan Traps

📍 Maharashtra, Gujarat, MP, Karnataka, AP

One of the largest volcanic features on Earth. The Deccan Traps were formed by massive flood basalt eruptions that began approximately 66 million years ago, coinciding with the Cretaceous-Paleogene mass extinction that killed the dinosaurs. Some scientists argue that these eruptions, not the Chicxulub asteroid alone, were a major driver of the extinction event.

▸ Covers approximately 500,000 km² (originally may have covered 1.5 million km²)
▸ Thickness ranges from 200m at edges to over 2,000m near Mumbai (Western Ghats)
▸ Created the Western Ghats escarpment and the fertile black cotton soil (regur)
▸ Eruptions lasted about 30,000 years, releasing vast amounts of sulfur dioxide and CO₂
▸ The word "trap" comes from Swedish word for staircase — describing the step-like topography of layered lava flows

Coal-bearing

Gondwana Formations

📍 Damodar, Mahanadi, Godavari, Narmada Valleys

Named after the Gond tribe of central India, these formations are the most significant coal-bearing rocks on the subcontinent, deposited when India was part of the southern supercontinent Gondwana. The Gondwana System provides 98% of India's coal reserves and proved the theory of continental drift through identical fossil assemblages found across all Gondwana continents.

▸ Age: Carboniferous to Lower Cretaceous (~320–130 Ma)
▸ Contains the famous Glossopteris flora — fern-like plants found identically in India, Australia, Africa, South America, and Antarctica
▸ Talchir Formation at the base shows glacial tillites — evidence of Permian glaciation when India was near the South Pole
▸ Major coalfields: Jharia, Raniganj, Bokaro, Singrauli, Korba, Singareni, Wardha
▸ Total coal reserves estimated at over 300 billion tonnes

Ancient Sedimentary

Vindhyan System

📍 Rajasthan, MP, UP, Bihar

One of the largest and thickest sequences of Proterozoic sedimentary rocks in the world, stretching over 100,000 km². The Vindhyan rocks have been the building blocks of India's architectural heritage for millennia — from Ashoka's pillars to Mughal masterpieces. They also contain some of the earliest evidence of life on Earth.

▸ Age: 1,400 to 550 million years (Lower to Upper Vindhyan)
▸ Total thickness exceeds 4,500 meters in some areas
▸ Contains stromatolites — structures formed by ancient cyanobacteria, among the oldest fossil evidence in India
▸ Panna diamond mines operate within Vindhyan rocks (Kimberlite pipes intruding through Vindhyans)
▸ Red sandstone from Dholpur and Karauli (Rajasthan) used in Red Fort, Fatehpur Sikri, Rashtrapati Bhavan
▸ Great Boundary Fault (GBF) — a major tectonic feature separating Vindhyans from Aravallis

Proterozoic Basin

Cuddapah System

📍 Andhra Pradesh (Cuddapah & Kurnool Districts)

The Cuddapah Basin is one of the largest Proterozoic intracratonic basins in the world, forming a distinctive crescent-shaped outcrop in southern Andhra Pradesh. These ancient rocks, deposited in a shallow marine to continental environment, host a remarkable variety of minerals and have played a crucial role in understanding India's Precambrian geological history.

▸ Age: approximately 1,900 to 1,000 million years
▸ Crescent-shaped basin covering ~44,500 km² with a maximum thickness of ~12,000m
▸ Divided into Lower Cuddapah (Papaghni, Chitravati, Nallamalai, Srisailam) and Upper Cuddapah (Kurnool Group)
▸ Rich in asbestos, barytes (80% of India's production), iron ore, manganese, and diamonds
▸ Contains uranium deposits in the Srisailam and Palnad sub-basins

Section 04

Fossils & Paleontology

India's fossil record is extraordinarily rich and diverse — from billion-year-old stromatolites to dinosaur nesting grounds, from marine fossils atop the Himalayas to the ancestors of modern primates in the Siwalik Hills.

🦕
Dinosaur Remains
Gujarat, Madhya Pradesh, Maharashtra, Tamil Nadu

Balasinor, Gujarat — Known as "India's Jurassic Park," this site in Kheda district preserves hundreds of fossilized dinosaur eggs and bones of titanosaurs. The Rahioli fossil site has yielded the Rajasaurus narmadensis, a carnivorous abelisaurid unique to India.

Jabalpur, Madhya Pradesh — The Lameta Formation near Jabalpur was where the first dinosaur fossils in India were discovered in 1828. Dinosaur eggs, nests, and bones of sauropods have been found in Bagh and Dhar districts. The site preserves evidence of mass nesting behavior.

Ariyalur, Tamil Nadu — Rich in Cretaceous marine and terrestrial fossils including ammonites and dinosaur remains.

Rajasaurus narmadensis Titanosaurus Isisaurus Jainosaurus 66-70 Ma
🐚
Marine Fossils in the Himalayas
Spiti Valley (HP), Ladakh, Kumaon, Nepal Border

The most dramatic evidence that the Himalayas were once beneath the Tethys Sea. Spiti Valley in Himachal Pradesh preserves a nearly continuous marine fossil record from the Cambrian to the Cretaceous (~540–66 Ma). Ammonites, belemnites, brachiopods, corals, and crinoids are found at elevations above 4,000 meters. The Ammonite Beds of Muktinath (Nepal-India border) are sacred — the fossil ammonites are worshipped as "Shaligram" (a form of Lord Vishnu). In Ladakh, marine fossils found above 5,000m altitude conclusively prove the collision of the Indian and Eurasian plates.

Ammonites Trilobites Brachiopods Shaligram Tethys Sea
🌿
Plant Fossils & Ancient Life
Gondwana Basins, Rajmahal Hills, Kutch

The Glossopteris flora — found extensively in Gondwana formations of the Damodar, Mahanadi, and Godavari valleys — provided the key evidence for Alfred Wegener's theory of continental drift. Identical fossilized fern-like plants were found on all southern continents. The Rajmahal Hills (Jharkhand) preserve Jurassic plant fossils including cycads, conifers, and early angiosperms. The Siwalik Hills are a treasure trove of Miocene-Pliocene mammalian fossils — including Sivapithecus (ancestral ape), Stegodon, Hipparion, and ancient giraffids. Kutch yields exceptional Jurassic marine fossils including ammonites and belemnites.

Seismic Zones

India is divided into four seismic zones (II through V) by the Bureau of Indian Standards based on the Modified Mercalli Intensity scale. The entire Himalayan belt, Kutch, and the Andaman-Nicobar Islands fall in the highest risk zones.

1950 Assam-Tibet Border 8.6 ~4,800 deaths. One of the largest earthquakes of the 20th century. Massive landslides blocked the Subansiri River.
1993 Latur (Killari), Maharashtra 6.2 ~9,748 deaths. Occurred in a supposedly stable peninsular region (Zone I/II), shocking seismologists. Led to revision of seismic zonation.
2001 Bhuj, Gujarat 7.7 ~20,000 deaths. 167,000 injured. India's deadliest earthquake in modern times. Destroyed Bhuj, Anjar, and surrounding areas.
2004 Indian Ocean (Andaman) 9.1 Triggered the devastating Indian Ocean Tsunami. ~230,000 deaths globally, ~12,000+ in India. Andaman-Nicobar islands permanently shifted.
2005 Kashmir (India-Pakistan border) 7.6 ~86,000 deaths (mostly Pakistan-side). Uri and surrounding areas affected in Indian Kashmir. Massive landslides.
2015 Nepal-India Border (Gorkha) 7.8 ~9,000 deaths. Significant damage in Bihar and UP. Aftershock of 7.3 followed. Highlighted Himalayan seismic gap concerns.
⚠️ The Himalayan Seismic Gap

Seismologists have identified a "Central Seismic Gap" in the Himalayas — the region between the 1905 Kangra and 1934 Bihar-Nepal earthquake rupture zones. This ~500km stretch (covering Uttarakhand and western Nepal) has not experienced a great earthquake (8.0+) in over 500 years. The accumulated strain on the Main Himalayan Thrust fault could potentially produce a massive earthquake of magnitude 8.5 or higher, posing an existential risk to northern India's densely populated cities including Delhi.

Section 06

Volcanic Features

While India is not currently on an active plate boundary with volcanism, it possesses one of the world's few island volcanoes, remnants of ancient super-eruptions, and the legacy of one of Earth's largest volcanic events.

🌋

Barren Island Volcano

📍 Andaman Sea, 138 km NE of Port Blair

Barren Island is the only confirmed active volcano in India and the only active volcano along the chain from Sumatra to Myanmar. It is a stratovolcano rising ~354 meters above sea level from the floor of the Andaman Sea. The volcano sits on the boundary between the Indian Plate (subducting) and the Burmese Plate.

The first recorded eruption was in 1787. After a long dormancy, it reawakened in 1991 and has been intermittently active since. Major eruptions occurred in 1991, 1994-95, 2005-07, 2008-09, and most recently in 2017. The island is uninhabited and access is restricted, though it is visible from chartered boats. The crater is approximately 2 km wide, and lava flows have created new land on the western shore.

🏔️ Narcondam Island

A dormant volcano located 256 km NE of Port Blair. Rising 710m above sea level, Narcondam is a conical volcanic island classified as dormant — no historical eruptions recorded. It is home to the endemic Narcondam Hornbill and is a protected wildlife sanctuary. Hot springs near the island suggest residual volcanic activity beneath the surface. It is part of the same volcanic arc as Barren Island.

♨️ Hot Springs & Geothermal

India has over 340 known hot spring sites, many associated with deep faults and ancient volcanic activity. Major clusters include: Manikaran (HP, 96°C), Tattapani (Chhattisgarh, 60°C), Bakreshwar (WB), Rajgir (Bihar), Unai (Gujarat), and Puga Valley (Ladakh — being explored for geothermal energy). The ONGC has identified 7 geothermal provinces across India with a combined potential of 10,600 MW.

Section 07

Mineral Belts of India

India is endowed with significant mineral resources distributed across well-defined geological belts. The country produces 95 minerals — 4 fuel minerals, 10 metallic, 23 non-metallic, 3 atomic minerals, and 55 minor minerals.

🗺️ Major Mineral Belts Overview

India's mineral wealth is concentrated in four major belts: (1) The North-Eastern Peninsular Belt (Chota Nagpur Plateau — richest in India), (2) The South-Western Peninsular Belt (Karnataka-Goa-Kerala), (3) The North-Western Belt (Rajasthan-Gujarat), and (4) The South-Eastern Belt (Odisha-Andhra Pradesh-Tamil Nadu). Together, these belts make India one of the most minerally diverse nations on Earth.

⛏️
Iron Ore
Metallic • Haematite & Magnetite

India has the 5th largest reserves globally (~33.7 billion tonnes). Haematite (red ore, 60-70% Fe) dominates. Found in Banded Iron Formations of Archaean age. India is the 4th largest producer worldwide.

Odisha (Keonjhar, Sundargarh) — Largest producer
Chhattisgarh (Bailadila, Dalli-Rajhara)
Jharkhand (Singhbhum — Noamundi, Gua)
Karnataka (Bellary-Hospet belt)
Goa (lateritic iron ore, now restricted)
🖤
Coal
Fuel • Bituminous & Lignite

India has the 5th largest coal reserves globally (~326 billion tonnes). 98% from Gondwana formations (bituminous), 2% Tertiary (lignite). India is the 2nd largest coal producer and consumer worldwide.

Jharkhand (Jharia — India's largest coalfield, Bokaro)
West Bengal (Raniganj — oldest coalfield)
Odisha (Talcher, Ib Valley)
Chhattisgarh (Korba, Hasdeo-Arand)
Madhya Pradesh (Singrauli, Sohagpur)
Tamil Nadu (Neyveli — largest lignite deposit)
🥈
Bauxite (Aluminium Ore)
Metallic • Lateritic Deposits

India has 5th largest bauxite reserves (~3.9 billion tonnes). Formed by tropical weathering of aluminium-rich rocks. Most deposits are on East Coast plateaus and the Deccan Trap region. India is the 5th largest producer.

Odisha (Koraput — Panchpatmali, world's largest single deposit)
Gujarat (Kutch, Jamnagar)
Jharkhand (Lohardaga, Palamu)
Maharashtra (Kolhapur, Ratnagiri)
Chhattisgarh (Amarkantak Plateau)
✨
Mica
Non-metallic • Muscovite & Phlogopite

India was historically the world's largest mica producer, contributing ~60% of global sheet mica. Found in pegmatite veins within metamorphic rocks. Used in electronics, cosmetics, and as insulation. The "Mica Belt" is world-famous.

Jharkhand-Bihar Mica Belt (Koderma-Giridih-Hazaribagh)
Andhra Pradesh (Nellore)
Rajasthan (Bhilwara, Udaipur, Ajmer)
💎
Diamonds & Precious Stones
Gemstones • Kimberlite & Alluvial

India was the world's only source of diamonds until the 18th century. The legendary Koh-i-Noor, Hope Diamond, and Regent Diamond all originated from Indian mines. Diamonds occur in kimberlite pipes and alluvial deposits.

Madhya Pradesh (Panna — only active diamond mine)
Andhra Pradesh (Krishna-Godavari basin, Wajrakarur kimberlites)
Chhattisgarh (Raipur belt — exploration stage)
🔷
Manganese
Metallic • Essential for Steel

India has the 5th largest manganese reserves. Essential for steel production (no steel without manganese). Found in Archaean and Dharwar formations. India is the 5th largest producer globally.

Odisha (Keonjhar, Sundargarh — largest producer)
Maharashtra (Nagpur, Bhandara)
Madhya Pradesh (Balaghat, Chhindwara)
Karnataka (Bellary, Shimoga, Chitradurga)
Andhra Pradesh (Srikakulam, Visakhapatnam)
🟡
Gold
Precious Metal • Lode & Placer

India's gold deposits are primarily in Archaean greenstone belts. The Kolar Gold Fields in Karnataka were among the deepest mines in the world (reaching 3,200m). Though most mines are now closed, exploration continues.

Karnataka (Kolar — historic, Hutti — currently active, Raichur)
Jharkhand (Singhbhum — exploration)
Andhra Pradesh (Ramagiri — exploration)
Rajasthan (Banswara — Jagpura deposit)
☢️
Atomic Minerals
Strategic • Uranium, Thorium, Beryllium

India has the world's largest thorium reserves (estimated ~963,000 tonnes). Uranium deposits are more modest. These minerals are managed by the Atomic Minerals Directorate (AMD) and are critical for India's three-stage nuclear power program.

Jharkhand (Jaduguda — India's largest uranium mine, Singhbhum belt)
Andhra Pradesh (Tummalapalle — world's largest single uranium deposit)
Meghalaya (Domiasiat — West Khasi Hills)
Kerala & Tamil Nadu (Monazite sands — thorium-rich beach placers)
Rajasthan (Rohil — recent discovery, significant reserves)
