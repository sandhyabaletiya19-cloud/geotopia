WORLD WAR I

1914 - 1918

"The war to end all wars"

〰️〰️〰️

World War I (1914-1918) was the first truly global conflict, involving over 30 nations and 70 million military personnel. The assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand triggered a chain reaction of alliances that engulfed the world. The Western Front in France and Belgium became synonymous with trench warfare—soldiers lived and died in muddy ditches separated by "No Man's Land." New weapons like machine guns, poison gas, tanks, and aircraft transformed warfare forever. The war killed 17 million people, collapsed four empires, and redrew the map of Europe and the Middle East.

THE GATHERING STORM

The two decades between world wars saw the rise of totalitarian regimes that would plunge the world into even greater conflict. The Treaty of Versailles left Germany humiliated and economically crippled, creating fertile ground for extremism. The Great Depression (1929) devastated global economies, pushing nations toward authoritarian solutions. Mussolini seized power in Italy (1922), Hitler in Germany (1933), and Japan's military dominated its government. The League of Nations proved toothless as aggression mounted: Japan invaded Manchuria (1931), Italy conquered Ethiopia (1935), and Germany remilitarized and annexed territory.

⚠️ Sensitive Historical Content

This section covers WWII including references to the Holocaust. This content is presented for educational purposes to ensure these events are understood and never repeated.

World War II in Europe was the most destructive conflict in history. Germany's Blitzkrieg ("lightning war") conquered Poland in weeks, France in six weeks, and most of Europe by 1941. Operation Barbarossa, the invasion of the Soviet Union, ultimately became Hitler's downfall. The tide turned at Stalingrad (1942-43) and the D-Day invasion (June 6, 1944) opened a second front. Allied forces liberated Europe from both directions, meeting at the Elbe River in April 1945. Germany surrendered on May 8, 1945—"V-E Day."

WORLD WAR II: PACIFIC THEATER

1937 - 1945

"Island hopping to victory"

🇯🇵 Japan
Hiroshima Aug 6, 1945
Nagasaki Aug 9, 1945

Japan's war in Asia began with the 1937 invasion of China. The attack on Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941) brought America into the war. Japan rapidly conquered Southeast Asia, the Philippines, and Pacific islands. The tide turned at Midway (June 1942), where four Japanese carriers were sunk. The U.S. then employed "island hopping"—bypassing heavily fortified islands to capture strategic ones. Each island—Guadalcanal, Tarawa, Saipan, Iwo Jima, Okinawa—was taken at enormous cost. The war ended with the atomic bombings of Hiroshima and Nagasaki, the only use of nuclear weapons in warfare.

The Cold War was a 45-year ideological, political, and military standoff between the United States and Soviet Union. Though the superpowers never fought directly, the world lived under the shadow of nuclear annihilation. The Iron Curtain divided Europe; the Berlin Wall symbolized this division. Proxy wars raged in Korea, Vietnam, Afghanistan, and dozens of other countries. The Cuban Missile Crisis (1962) brought the world closest to nuclear war. The Cold War ended with the fall of the Berlin Wall (1989) and the dissolution of the Soviet Union (1991).

The end of the Cold War did not bring lasting peace. The 1990s saw ethnic conflicts in Yugoslavia and genocide in Rwanda. The September 11, 2001 attacks launched the "War on Terror," with US invasions of Afghanistan (2001-2021) and Iraq (2003). The Arab Spring (2011) brought hope of democratization but devolved into civil wars in Syria, Libya, and Yemen. Russia's annexation of Crimea (2014) and full-scale invasion of Ukraine (2022) shattered the post-Cold War order in Europe. Today, over 50 armed conflicts rage worldwide, from Myanmar to Ethiopia to Gaza.
