AGE OF EXPLORATION

1400 - 1600 CE

"We shall find a way, or make one."

The Age of Exploration transformed humanity's understanding of the world. Driven by a desire for new trade routes to Asia, advances in navigation technology, and religious zeal, European sailors ventured into unknown waters. Portugal led the way under Prince Henry the Navigator, developing the caravel ship and mapping the African coast. Spain soon followed, sponsoring Christopher Columbus's fateful 1492 voyage that revealed the Americas to Europe. These explorations initiated the Columbian Exchange—a massive transfer of plants, animals, culture, human populations, technology, and diseases between the Americas and the Old World.

The Spanish and Portuguese built the first global empires, connecting continents through trade, conquest, and colonization. Spain's empire stretched from California to Patagonia, from the Caribbean to the Philippines, creating history's first "empire on which the sun never set." Portugal, though smaller, established a maritime network spanning Brazil, Africa's coasts, India, Southeast Asia, and even Japan. Both empires were driven by the extraction of precious metals—especially silver from Potosí and Zacatecas—which fueled European economies but devastated indigenous populations through forced labor systems like the encomienda and mita.

THE BRITISH EMPIRE

1583 - 1997 CE

"The empire on which the sun never sets"

The British Empire became the largest empire in human history, at its peak governing nearly 25% of the world's land surface and population. Beginning with colonial ventures in the Americas, Britain built a commercial and naval supremacy that spanned continents. The loss of the American colonies (1783) redirected focus to India, Africa, and Australasia. The British Raj in India alone encompassed 300 million subjects. The Empire operated through direct rule, protectorates, and self-governing dominions, connected by the Royal Navy—the world's most powerful fleet for over a century. Its legacy includes the English language, common law, parliamentary democracy, and contentious colonial borders that still cause conflicts today.

OTHER COLONIAL POWERS

1600 - 1975 CE

"The scramble for empire"

Beyond the Iberian and British empires, other European powers—and eventually Japan—carved out their own colonial domains. France built the second-largest empire, spanning Africa and Southeast Asia. The Netherlands dominated the spice trade through the Dutch East Indies (Indonesia). Belgium's King Leopold II turned the Congo into a personal horror; Germany entered the colonial race late but aggressively; Italy sought a new Roman Empire in Africa; and Japan built an Asian empire that would trigger World War II in the Pacific.

⚠️ Sensitive Historical Content

This section discusses the Atlantic slave trade and its horrific realities. Understanding this history is essential to comprehending modern racial inequalities and their origins.

The Atlantic slave trade was the largest forced migration in human history, lasting nearly 400 years and forcibly transporting an estimated 12.5 million Africans to the Americas. Only about 10.7 million survived the brutal "Middle Passage" across the Atlantic. This trade fundamentally shaped the economies of Europe and the Americas, built on the systematic dehumanization and exploitation of African peoples. The plantation economies of the Caribbean, Brazil, and the American South were entirely dependent on enslaved labor, producing sugar, cotton, tobacco, and coffee for global markets.

12.5M Africans forcibly transported
1.8M Died during Middle Passage
388 Years of transatlantic slavery
🔺 Triangular Trade
  • Europe to Africa: manufactured goods, weapons, alcohol
  • Africa to Americas: enslaved Africans (Middle Passage)
  • Americas to Europe: sugar, rum, cotton, tobacco, coffee
  • Each leg generated enormous profits
  • European ports (Liverpool, Nantes, Lisbon) grew wealthy
  • System lasted from 1500s to mid-1800s
🚢 The Middle Passage
  • 6-8 week voyage across Atlantic
  • Enslaved people chained in holds, barely room to move
  • Mortality rates: 10-20% on average
  • Disease, malnutrition, suicide common
  • "Tight pack" vs. "loose pack" loading methods
  • Resistance: shipboard rebellions (Amistad, 1839)
🌾 Plantation System
  • Sugar plantations: Caribbean, Brazil (largest importers)
  • Cotton: American South (4 million enslaved by 1860)
  • Tobacco: Virginia, Maryland, Caribbean
  • Coffee: Brazil, Caribbean
  • Brutal working conditions, high mortality
  • Family separation, physical punishment, rape
✊ Abolition & Legacy
  • Haiti Revolution (1791-1804): first successful slave revolt
  • Britain abolishes trade (1807), slavery (1833)
  • USA: 13th Amendment (1865) after Civil War
  • Brazil: last Western nation to abolish (1888)
  • Reparations debates continue today
  • Lasting impacts: racism, economic disparities

World War II shattered European colonial power and ignited a wave of independence movements across Asia, Africa, and the Middle East. Weakened imperial powers faced rising nationalism, Cold War pressures, and changing international norms. Some transitions were peaceful; others involved brutal wars. India's 1947 independence and partition set the pattern for Asia. Africa's "Year of Africa" (1960) saw 17 nations gain independence. The process fundamentally reshaped the world map, created the modern Global South, and left legacies—arbitrary borders, economic dependency, political instability—that persist today.
