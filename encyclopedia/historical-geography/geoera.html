4.5 Billion Years of Earth's Geological History

From a molten ball of rock to the blue planet we know today, Earth's journey spans 4.5 billion years. Continents have formed, drifted, collided, and split apart. Oceans have risen and fallen. Life has emerged, evolved, and repeatedly faced extinction. Explore the six major geological eras that shaped our world.

Hadean Eon

4.5 - 4.0 Billion Years Ago

Named after Hades, the Greek underworld, this era witnessed Earth's violent birth. The planet formed from a disk of gas and dust around the young Sun, experiencing intense heat, meteor bombardment, and the catastrophic collision that created the Moon.

Life

  • Stage set for Archean life

Archean Eon

4.0 - 2.5 Billion Years Ago

The era of "ancient life." Earth's crust solidifies, the first continents emerge, and most importantly, life appears. Single-celled organisms begin transforming the planet, eventually producing oxygen through photosynthesis.

Life Forms

  • Prokaryotes (bacteria, archaea)
  • Cyanobacteria (blue-green algae)
  • Stromatolites (bacterial mats)
  • Chemosynthetic organisms
  • Hydrothermal vent communities
  • All life in oceans

Proterozoic Eon

2.5 Billion - 541 Million Years Ago

The longest eon, witnessing the Great Oxygenation Event, formation and breakup of supercontinents (Rodinia, Pannotia), Snowball Earth glaciations, and the emergence of complex multicellular life at its end.

Climate

  • Ozone layer forms

Life Evolution

  • Mitochondria & chloroplasts (symbiosis)
  • First multicellular organisms
  • Ediacaran biota (soft-bodied)
  • Sexual reproduction
  • Red & green algae
  • Stage set for Cambrian explosion

Paleozoic Era

541 - 252 Million Years Ago

The "Age of Ancient Life." Witness the Cambrian Explosion of complex organisms, colonization of land by plants and animals, formation of Pangaea, and the devastating Permian extinction that wiped out 96% of species.

Extinctions

  • Ordovician (443 MYA): 86% species
  • Devonian (375 MYA): 75% species
  • Permian (252 MYA): 96% species
  • Massive volcanic eruptions
  • Ocean anoxia (no oxygen)
  • Worst extinction in history

Mesozoic Era

252 - 66 Million Years Ago

The "Age of Dinosaurs." Pangaea breaks apart into modern continents, dinosaurs dominate the land, mammals remain small, birds evolve from dinosaurs, and it all ends with a catastrophic asteroid impact.

Continental Drift

  • Pangaea begins splitting (200 MYA)
  • Laurasia & Gondwana separate

Dinosaurs

  • Herbivores: Sauropods, Stegosaurs
  • Carnivores: T-Rex, Velociraptor
  • Flying: Pterosaurs

Climate & Plants

  • Warm, tropical climate globally
  • No ice caps
  • High sea levels
  • Conifers dominate early
  • Flowering plants evolve (125 MYA)
  • Grasslands appear late Cretaceous

K-T Extinction

  • Asteroid impact (Chicxulub, Mexico)
  • 10 km wide impactor
  • Global firestorms
  • Impact winter (dust blocks sun)
  • 75% of species extinct
  • End of non-avian dinosaurs

Cenozoic Era

66 Million Years Ago - Present

The "Age of Mammals." After dinosaurs' demise, mammals diversify and dominate. Continents reach modern positions, ice ages cycle, mountain ranges rise, and primates evolve, culminating in humans reshaping the planet.
