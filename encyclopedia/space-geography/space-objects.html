OBSERVATION 01

Asteroids & Meteoroids

ROCKY REMNANTS OF PLANETARY FORMATION

Meteoroid → Meteor → Meteorite

  • Meteoroid: Small rocky/metallic body in space (grain to ~1m)
  • Meteor: Light streak when it enters atmosphere ("shooting star")
  • Meteorite: The piece that survives and hits Earth's surface

Remember: OID (space) → OR (atmosphere) → ITE (ground)

The Asteroid Belt

  • Location: Between Mars & Jupiter (2.1–3.3 AU)
  • Contains millions of asteroids
  • Total mass: Less than Earth's Moon!
  • Mostly empty space — sci-fi gets this wrong
  • Shaped by Jupiter's immense gravity

C-type (Carbonaceous)

Most common (~75%). Dark, carbon-rich. Found in outer belt. Primitive composition — oldest material in solar system.

S-type (Silicaceous)

Second most common (~17%). Rocky, silicate minerals + nickel-iron. Found in inner belt. Moderately bright.

M-type (Metallic)

Rare but valuable. Mostly nickel-iron. May be exposed cores of destroyed protoplanets. 16 Psyche = $10 quintillion!

DART Mission

Double Asteroid Redirection Test — humanity's first planetary defense test. On Sept 26, 2022, NASA deliberately crashed a spacecraft into Dimorphos (moon of asteroid Didymos) at 22,530 km/h. Successfully changed its orbital period by 33 minutes! Proved kinetic impact can deflect asteroids.

OBSERVATION 02

Comets

DIRTY SNOWBALLS FROM THE OUTER SOLAR SYSTEM

Comet Anatomy

  • Nucleus: Solid core, ice + rock + dust (1-50 km)
  • Coma: Fuzzy gas envelope as ice sublimes (~100,000 km)
  • Hydrogen envelope: Huge, invisible hydrogen cloud
  • Ion tail: Straight, blue — charged particles pushed by solar wind
  • Dust tail: Curved, white/yellow — heavier dust particles lag behind

Both tails ALWAYS point away from the Sun!

Where Comets Come From

  • Kuiper Belt (30-50 AU): Source of short-period comets (<200 years). Beyond Neptune's orbit. Contains Pluto.
  • Oort Cloud (2,000-100,000 AU): Source of long-period comets (>200 years). Spherical shell around entire solar system. ~1 trillion icy bodies.

Comets get "activated" when gravitational perturbations send them toward the inner solar system and the Sun's heat sublimes their ices.

"Dirty Snowball" vs "Icy Dirtball"

Fred Whipple (1950) proposed the "dirty snowball" model — mostly ice with embedded rock/dust. But missions like Rosetta revealed comets have more rock/dust than expected, leading some scientists to prefer "icy dirtball." Reality: varies comet by comet.

Comets & Life on Earth

Comets may have delivered significant amounts of water to early Earth, contributing to our oceans. They also carry complex organic molecules (amino acids found on comet 67P by Rosetta). Comets may have seeded Earth with life's building blocks!

Stars

NUCLEAR FURNACES LIGHTING THE COSMOS

Star Formation

Nebula (cold gas cloud) → gravity causes collapse → protostar forms (hot, dense core) → when core reaches ~10 million K, hydrogen fusion ignites → Main Sequence star is born! A star spends ~90% of its life on the Main Sequence.

Low Mass Star Life Cycle

Main Sequence → Red Giant → Planetary Nebula → White Dwarf

Stars like our Sun (<8 solar masses). Gentle death — outer layers blown off as beautiful planetary nebula. Core remains as white dwarf.

High Mass Star Life Cycle

Main Sequence → Red Supergiant → Supernova → Neutron Star / Black Hole

Stars >8 solar masses. Violent death — core collapses, outer layers explode in supernova. Remnant depends on mass.

Cepheid Variable Stars

Stars that pulsate with a regular period. The period is directly related to their intrinsic luminosity (period-luminosity relation). This makes them "standard candles" — compare apparent brightness with known luminosity to calculate distance. Henrietta Leavitt discovered this in 1912.

Star Clusters

  • Open Clusters: Loose groups, 100-1000 young stars. Found in galactic disk. Ex: Pleiades (M45), Hyades.
  • Globular Clusters: Dense spheres, 100,000-1M ancient stars. Orbit galaxy's halo. Ex: Omega Centauri, M13 (Hercules).

White Dwarfs

  • Size of Earth, mass of Sun
  • No more fusion — slowly cooling
  • Made of degenerate electron matter
  • Chandrasekhar limit: 1.4 M☉
  • Above this → collapses further
  • Type Ia supernovae: white dwarf in binary exceeds limit → explodes

Neutron Stars

  • ~20 km diameter, 1.4-2.1 M☉
  • Density: 1 teaspoon = 10M tons!
  • Pulsars: Rotating NS with beams from magnetic poles
  • Magnetars: Extreme magnetic fields (10¹⁵ gauss)
  • Spin rate: up to 716 times/sec!
  • Jocelyn Bell Burnell discovered first pulsar (1967)

Black Holes

  • Stellar: 3-20 M☉
  • Intermediate: 100-10,000 M☉
  • Supermassive: Millions-Billions M☉ (Sgr A*: 4M M☉)
  • Primordial: Theoretical, from Big Bang
  • Event horizon: point of no return
  • Singularity: infinite density center

Black Hole Physics

Schwarzschild Radius: r = 2GM/c²

Spaghettification: Tidal forces stretch objects into long thin strands as they approach. Difference in gravitational pull between your head and feet becomes infinite.

Gravitational Lensing: Light bends around massive objects. Einstein ring visible when source, lens, and observer align.

First Images:

  • M87* — April 10, 2019 (Event Horizon Telescope)
  • Sagittarius A* — May 12, 2022 (our own galactic center!)

Gravitational Waves: Ripples in spacetime. First detected by LIGO on Sept 14, 2015 — two black holes merging 1.3B ly away.

Hawking Radiation: Black holes slowly evaporate by emitting quantum particles at event horizon. Tiny ones evaporate faster.

OBSERVATION 05

Nebulae

STELLAR NURSERIES AND COSMIC ART

Emission Nebulae

Hot young stars ionize surrounding gas, which emits light (H-alpha red glow). Star-forming regions. Ex: Orion Nebula (M42).

Reflection Nebulae

Dust particles scatter/reflect light from nearby stars. Appear blue (shorter wavelengths scatter more — same reason sky is blue). Ex: Witch Head Nebula.

Dark Nebulae

Dense clouds of dust that block light from objects behind them. Appear as silhouettes. Cold gas. Ex: Horsehead Nebula, Coal Sack.

Planetary Nebulae

Shell of gas expelled by dying low-mass star. Central white dwarf illuminates it. NOT related to planets! Ex: Ring Nebula (M57), Cat's Eye.

Supernova Remnants

Expanding shockwave from supernova explosion. Contains heavy elements forged in the explosion. Ex: Crab Nebula (M1, from 1054 CE supernova).

Composition

Nebulae are mostly hydrogen (~90%) and helium (~10%) with trace amounts of heavier elements and microscopic dust grains. Stellar nurseries: nebulae collapse to form new stars.

The Universe Itself

FROM THE BIG BANG TO THE FATE OF EVERYTHING

The Big Bang

13.8 billion years ago, the universe began — not as an explosion in space, but as a rapid expansion of space itself. All matter, energy, space, and time emerged from an incredibly hot, dense singularity. Evidence: Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation — the afterglow, discovered 1965. Universe was opaque for first 380,000 years.

Dark Matter & Dark Energy

68%Dark Energy
27%Dark Matter
5%Ordinary Matter

We can only see 5% of the universe! Dark matter provides gravity holding galaxies together. Dark energy drives accelerating expansion. Neither has been directly detected — we infer them from their effects.

Fate of the Universe

  • Big Freeze (Heat Death): Most likely. Expansion continues forever, stars die, entropy maximizes. Cold, dark, empty. ~10¹⁰⁰ years.
  • Big Rip: If dark energy increases, it tears apart galaxies, stars, atoms. ~22 billion years from now.
  • Big Crunch: If gravity wins, universe collapses back. Currently ruled out.
  • Big Bounce: Cyclic — crunch then new Big Bang. Elegant but speculative.

Large Scale Structure

The universe has a "cosmic web" structure: galaxy clusters connected by filaments (threads of dark matter and gas), with vast empty voids between them. Filaments can be hundreds of millions of light-years long. The observable universe is 93 billion light-years in diameter (expansion makes it larger than 13.8B ly).

Fermi Paradox & Drake Equation

"If aliens exist, where is everyone?"

N = R* × fp × ne × fl × fi × fc × L

Where N = communicating civilizations. Even conservative estimates suggest thousands should exist — yet we've found zero evidence. Possible explanations: Great Filter, Zoo hypothesis, rare Earth, we're early/alone, or they use technology we can't detect.

OBSERVATION 09

Space Fundamentals

KEY CONCEPTS FOR UNDERSTANDING THE COSMOS

Electromagnetic Spectrum

Radio Micro IR Visible UV X-ray Gamma

Radio → Microwave → Infrared → Visible → Ultraviolet → X-ray → Gamma ray

Longer wavelength → lower energy. JWST primarily observes in infrared — perfect for seeing through dust and observing very distant (redshifted) objects.

Distance Units in Space

  • Speed of light: 299,792,458 m/s (≈300,000 km/s)
  • Light-year: Distance light travels in 1 year ≈ 9.46 trillion km
  • AU (Astronomical Unit): Earth-Sun distance ≈ 149.6 million km
  • Parsec: 3.26 light-years (parallax angle of 1 arcsecond)
  • Megaparsec (Mpc): 1 million parsecs — used for galaxy distances

Cosmic Distance Ladder

  • Radar ranging — Solar system distances (bounce signals off planets)
  • Parallax — Nearby stars, up to ~1,000 ly (measure angle shift)
  • Cepheid variables — Up to ~100 million ly (period-luminosity relation)
  • Type Ia supernovae — Distant galaxies (standard candles)
  • Hubble's Law / Redshift — Entire observable universe (v = H₀ × d)

Doppler Effect in Space

  • Redshift: Object moving away — wavelength stretches (red). Almost all galaxies are redshifted → universe is expanding!
  • Blueshift: Object moving toward us — wavelength compresses (blue). Andromeda is blueshifted — it's approaching!
  • Hubble constant: H₀ ≈ 70 km/s/Mpc
  • For every megaparsec of distance, galaxies recede 70 km/s faster

Orbital Mechanics

  • Kepler's 1st Law: Orbits are ellipses with Sun at one focus
  • Kepler's 2nd Law: Equal areas in equal times (faster when closer)
  • Kepler's 3rd Law: T² ∝ a³ (period² proportional to semi-major axis³)
  • Escape velocity: v = √(2GM/r)
  • Lagrange Points L1-L5: Gravitational balance points. JWST orbits Earth-Sun L2 (1.5M km from Earth, away from Sun)
