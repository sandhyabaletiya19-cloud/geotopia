🔭 Key Terms

Perihelion
The point in Earth's orbit closest to the Sun (~147.1 million km), occurring around January 3rd each year.
Aphelion
The point in Earth's orbit farthest from the Sun (~152.1 million km), occurring around July 4th each year.
Goldilocks Zone
The habitable zone around a star where liquid water can exist on a planet's surface — not too hot, not too cold.
Sidereal Day
Time for Earth to complete one rotation relative to distant stars: 23 hours, 56 minutes, 4 seconds.
Solar Day
Time for Earth to complete one rotation relative to the Sun: exactly 24 hours (average).
Obliquity
Earth's axial tilt of 23.5° relative to its orbital plane, responsible for seasons.
Coriolis Effect
The deflection of moving objects (winds, currents) caused by Earth's rotation — right in Northern Hemisphere, left in Southern.
Geoid
Earth's true shape — an oblate spheroid (slightly flattened at poles, bulging at equator).
Van Allen Belts
Two radiation belts of charged particles trapped by Earth's magnetic field, protecting us from solar wind.
Magnetic Declination
The angle between magnetic north (compass direction) and geographic north (true north).
Equinox
When day and night are approximately equal worldwide, occurring around March 21 and September 23.
Solstice
When the Sun reaches its highest or lowest point in the sky at noon, creating longest/shortest days — June 21 & December 22.

Section 03

Earth's Motions

Spinning on its axis and racing around the Sun — you're never truly standing still

Rotation

Earth spins on its tilted axis from West to East, creating the cycle of day and night. You're moving at 1,670 km/h at the equator — yet you don't feel a thing.

Period: 23h 56m 4s (sidereal)
Speed: 1,670 km/h at equator
Direction: West → East
Creates: Day & Night cycle
Causes: Coriolis Effect
Creates: 24 Time Zones

Revolution

Earth travels an elliptical path around the Sun at 29.78 km/s — that's 107,208 km/h. One complete orbit takes 365.25 days, giving us our year and seasons.

Period: 365.25 days
Speed: 29.78 km/s (107,208 km/h)
Orbit: Elliptical (not circular)
Perihelion: 147.1M km (Jan 3)
Aphelion: 152.1M km (Jul 4)
Creates: Seasons & Leap Year

Effects of Earth's Motions

Day & Night

Earth's rotation causes the Sun to appear to rise in the east and set in the west. At any given moment, half the Earth faces the Sun (day) while the other half is in shadow (night). The circle of illumination divides the lit and dark halves.

At the equator, day and night are nearly equal year-round. At the poles, you can experience 6 months of daylight followed by 6 months of darkness.

Coriolis Effect

Because Earth rotates, freely moving objects (like wind and ocean currents) appear to deflect from a straight path. In the Northern Hemisphere, deflection is to the right. In the Southern Hemisphere, it's to the left.

This effect drives global wind patterns, ocean circulation, and even the rotation direction of hurricanes. It's zero at the equator and maximum at the poles.

Seasons

Seasons are caused by Earth's 23.5° axial tilt combined with revolution around the Sun. When the Northern Hemisphere tilts toward the Sun, it's summer there and winter in the Southern Hemisphere.

The tilt affects the angle of solar radiation and the duration of daylight, creating seasonal temperature variations. Near the equator, seasons are minimal.

Leap Year

Earth takes 365.25 days to orbit the Sun. The extra 0.25 day accumulates, so every 4 years we add February 29th. However, century years must be divisible by 400 (so 1900 was not a leap year, but 2000 was).

Without leap years, our calendar would drift by ~24 days every century, eventually shifting summer to what we call "winter months."

Time Zones

Earth is divided into 24 time zones, each spanning 15° of longitude. As Earth rotates 360° in 24 hours, each 15° = 1 hour difference.

The Prime Meridian (0°) at Greenwich, London sets GMT/UTC. The International Date Line (180°) in the Pacific is where the calendar date changes. India follows IST = UTC+5:30.

Section 04

The Coordinate Grid

Latitude, Longitude — the GPS of the ancient world

🌐
Latitude (φ)

Angular distance north or south of the Equator. Ranges from 0° (Equator) to 90°N/S (Poles). Lines of latitude are also called parallels.

0° to 90° N/S
🧭
Longitude (λ)

Angular distance east or west of the Prime Meridian at Greenwich. Ranges from 0° to 180°E/W. Lines of longitude are called meridians and converge at the poles.

0° to 180° E/W
⭕
Important Lines

Equator: 0° — Divides Earth into N/S hemispheres
Tropic of Cancer: 23.5°N
Tropic of Capricorn: 23.5°S
Arctic Circle: 66.5°N
Antarctic Circle: 66.5°S
Prime Meridian: 0° — Divides into E/W hemispheres
IDL: 180° — Date changes here

📐
Great vs Small Circles

Great Circle: Any circle that divides Earth into two equal halves (Equator, all meridians). Shortest route between two points.
Small Circle: Divides Earth into unequal parts (all parallels except Equator).

Section 05

Earth's Magnetic Shield

An invisible force field protecting life from the deadliest cosmic radiation

🧲
Magnetic North vs True North

Earth's magnetic poles don't align with geographic poles. The angle between them is called magnetic declination. The magnetic north pole is currently in the Canadian Arctic, and it's moving toward Siberia at ~55 km/year!

🛡️
Van Allen Radiation Belts

Two doughnut-shaped belts of charged particles trapped by Earth's magnetic field. The inner belt (1,000-12,000 km) holds protons; the outer belt (13,000-60,000 km) holds electrons. They shield us from lethal solar radiation.

🌌
Aurora Borealis & Australis

When solar wind particles collide with atmospheric gases near the poles, they create spectacular light shows. Green = oxygen at 100km, Red = oxygen at 300km, Purple/Blue = nitrogen.

⚡
Dynamo Theory

Earth's magnetic field is generated by convection currents in the liquid iron outer core. This rotating, electrically conducting fluid acts like a giant electromagnet — the geodynamo.

Section 06

Time on Earth

From sidereal days to time zones — how we measure cosmic clockwork

🌟

Sidereal Day

23h 56m 4s

One complete rotation relative to distant stars. This is the "true" rotation period of Earth.

☀️

Solar Day

24h 00m 00s

One complete rotation relative to the Sun. ~4 minutes longer because Earth also moves along its orbit.

⏰

Local Time

Varies

Based on the Sun's position at your specific longitude. Noon = Sun at highest point in your sky.

🕐

Standard Time

15° = 1hr

Countries adopt standard time zones. Each zone covers 15° longitude. India: UTC+5:30 (IST).

🌐

GMT / UTC

0° Meridian

Greenwich Mean Time / Coordinated Universal Time. The global reference for all time zones.

📅

Intl Date Line

180° Meridian

Cross it westward = gain a day. Cross eastward = lose a day. Zigzags to avoid splitting countries.

Summer Solstice — June 21

The Sun is directly overhead at the Tropic of Cancer (23.5°N). Northern Hemisphere has its longest day. Areas beyond the Arctic Circle (66.5°N) experience 24 hours of sunlight (Midnight Sun). Southern Hemisphere experiences winter.

Autumnal Equinox — September 23

The Sun is directly over the Equator. Day and night are approximately equal (12 hours each) worldwide. Marks the beginning of autumn in the Northern Hemisphere and spring in the Southern Hemisphere.

Winter Solstice — December 22

The Sun is directly overhead at the Tropic of Capricorn (23.5°S). Northern Hemisphere has its shortest day. Areas beyond the Antarctic Circle (66.5°S) get 24 hours of sunlight. This is summer in the Southern Hemisphere.

Vernal Equinox — March 21

Again, the Sun is directly over the Equator. Equal day and night globally. Spring begins in the Northern Hemisphere; autumn in the Southern. After this, the Northern Hemisphere starts tilting toward the Sun.

Midnight Sun & Polar Night

Midnight Sun: At latitudes beyond 66.5°, the Sun doesn't set for continuous periods during summer. Norway's North Cape sees 76 days of continuous sunlight.

Polar Night: The reverse — the Sun doesn't rise for weeks or months during winter at the same latitudes. At the poles, this lasts nearly 6 months.
