CRUST

Earth's Outermost Shell

The crust is Earth's cool, rigid outer skin — the ground beneath your feet. It's divided into continental crust (thick, granite-rich, older) and oceanic crust (thin, basalt-rich, denser, younger). Relatively speaking, it's thinner than an eggshell compared to Earth's size.

MANTLE

The Engine Room of Earth

The mantle is Earth's thickest layer — a massive zone of hot, dense silicate rock. The upper mantle contains the asthenosphere, a semi-molten zone where tectonic plates slide. Giant convection currents here drive everything — plate tectonics, volcanoes, earthquakes. It's the engine of our living planet.

OUTER CORE

The Liquid Iron Sea

Welcome to the most dramatic layer — a churning ocean of liquid iron and nickel at temperatures rivaling the Sun's surface. The swirling currents of electrically conducting liquid metal here generate Earth's magnetic field through the geodynamo effect. Without this layer, life on Earth would be destroyed by solar radiation.

INNER CORE

The Beating Heart of Earth

You've reached it — the very center of Earth. A solid ball of iron roughly the size of the Moon, compressed by the weight of the entire planet above it. Despite temperatures hotter than the Sun's surface (~5,500°C), it remains solid due to immense pressure. It rotates slightly faster than the rest of Earth — a planet within a planet.

Rocks & The Rock Cycle

Nothing is permanent — every rock transforms over millions of years in an endless cycle

🔥

Igneous

Formed from cooling magma or lava. Intrusive (slow cool = large crystals) vs Extrusive (fast cool = fine texture).

Granite Basalt Obsidian Pumice Gabbro
🪨

Sedimentary

Formed by compaction of sediments over millions of years. Contains fossils. Covers 75% of Earth's surface.

Sandstone Limestone Shale Coal Chalk
💎

Metamorphic

Existing rocks transformed by extreme heat and pressure without melting. Foliated or non-foliated.

Marble Quartzite Slate Schist Gneiss

Module 07

Earthquakes & Seismic Waves

Earth speaks through vibrations — and we've learned to listen

Seismic Wave Types

P-Waves (Primary)

Compression waves — fastest, travel through solids AND liquids. First to arrive. Speed: ~6-8 km/s in crust.

S-Waves (Secondary)

Shear waves — slower, travel ONLY through solids. Stop at liquid outer core → reveals its liquid state.

Surface Waves (L & R)

Slowest but most destructive. Love waves (horizontal shaking) and Rayleigh waves (rolling motion).

Module 08

Volcanoes

Windows into Earth's interior — where the planet bleeds fire

🛡️
Shield Volcano

Broad, gently sloping dome formed by low-viscosity basaltic lava flows. Non-explosive but continuous eruptions. Largest volcanoes on Earth.

Mauna Loa, Hawaii Olympus Mons (Mars) Skjaldbreiður, Iceland
🌋
Composite (Stratovolcano)

Steep, cone-shaped, alternating layers of lava and pyroclastic material. Most violent eruptions. Classic "volcano" shape.

Mt. Fuji, Japan Mt. Vesuvius, Italy Mt. Pinatubo
💥
Cinder Cone

Smallest and most common type. Built from ejected lava fragments (cinders). Steep sides and bowl-shaped crater at summit.

Paricutín, Mexico Sunset Crater, USA Mt. Etna cones
🕳️
Caldera & Supervolcano

When a massive magma chamber empties and collapses. Supervolcanoes can alter global climate when they erupt.

Yellowstone, USA Toba, Indonesia Krakatoa
🔥

Ring of Fire

A 40,000 km horseshoe-shaped zone around the Pacific Ocean containing 75% of Earth's volcanoes and 90% of earthquakes. Runs through New Zealand, Philippines, Japan, Alaska, and the Americas.

Ring-shaped coral reef around a sunken volcanic island.

Fold Mountains

Formed by compressional forces crumpling rock. E.g., Himalayas, Alps, Andes.

⛰️

🗻
