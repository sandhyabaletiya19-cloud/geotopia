The Map Projection Problem

You cannot peel an orange and lay it flat without tearing or stretching. The same challenge faces cartographers when mapping our spherical Earth.

Earth is a Sphere

Our planet is roughly spherical (technically an oblate spheroid), with a continuous curved surface that cannot be perfectly represented on a flat plane.

Maps are Flat

Paper, screens, and most practical map formats are flat two-dimensional surfaces. We need a way to transform 3D to 2D.

Inevitable Distortion

Every flat map distorts reality in some way – whether it's shape, area, distance, or direction. The question is which distortion to minimize.

The Four Types of Distortion

Area (Size)

Regions appear larger or smaller than their true relative size

Shape (Form)

Features appear stretched, compressed, or skewed from true form

Distance (Scale)

Measured distances don't reflect true ground distances

Direction (Azimuth)

Angles and bearings between points are not preserved

Cartographer's Compromise

No single projection can preserve all four properties. Cartographers must choose which properties to preserve based on the map's purpose. This is why different projections exist – each optimized for different uses.

Classification System

Map projections are classified by three main criteria: the developable surface used, the properties preserved, and the aspect (orientation).

Cylindrical

Earth projected onto a cylinder

The globe is wrapped in a cylinder that touches along a line (usually the equator). When unrolled, produces a rectangular map.

Mercator Miller Gall-Peters Plate Carrée

Conic

Earth projected onto a cone

A cone is placed over the globe, touching along one or two parallels. Best for mid-latitude regions with east-west extent.

Albers Equal Area Lambert Conformal Equidistant Conic

Azimuthal (Planar)

Earth projected onto a flat plane

A flat plane touches the globe at one point. Creates circular maps centered on that point. Directions from center are true.

Stereographic Gnomonic Orthographic Azimuthal Equidistant

Conformal

Preserves: Shape (locally)

Maintains angular relationships and local shapes. Circles on the globe remain circles on the map. Essential for navigation charts where bearing accuracy is critical.

Mercator Stereographic Lambert Conformal

Equal-Area (Equivalent)

Preserves: Area (size)

Maintains accurate relative sizes between regions. If Brazil is 10x larger than France in reality, it will be 10x larger on the map. Critical for thematic maps showing distributions.

Gall-Peters Albers Mollweide

Equidistant

Preserves: Distance (from point/line)

Maintains accurate distances from the center point or along certain lines. Used in atlas maps and telecommunications planning where distance accuracy from a specific point is needed.

Azimuthal Equidistant Equidistant Conic Plate Carrée

Azimuthal (True Direction)

Preserves: Direction from center

Maintains correct directions (azimuths) from the center point. A straight line from the center shows true bearing. Used for polar maps and radio/satellite communication planning.

Gnomonic Orthographic Stereographic

Compromise

Balanced distortion

Doesn't perfectly preserve any single property but minimizes overall distortion. Creates visually pleasing maps where no property is extremely distorted. Popular for world maps in atlases.

Robinson Winkel Tripel Natural Earth

Normal (Equatorial)

Standard orientation

The projection surface is aligned with Earth's axis. For cylindrical projections, the cylinder is tangent at the equator. Most common and intuitive orientation.

Standard Mercator Plate Carrée

Transverse

Rotated 90°

The projection surface is rotated 90° from normal. For cylindrical projections, the cylinder is tangent along a meridian. Best for mapping north-south regions.

Transverse Mercator UTM

Oblique

Any other angle

The projection surface is tilted at an angle other than 0° or 90°. Can be centered on any point on Earth, minimizing distortion for specific regions.

Oblique Mercator Space Oblique Mercator

Major Map Projections

Explore the most important and widely-used map projections, their characteristics, and best use cases.

Conformal Cylindrical

Mercator

Gerardus Mercator, 1569

The most famous projection. Preserves angles and shapes locally, making it perfect for navigation. However, it dramatically exaggerates areas near the poles.

Navigation Web Maps Marine Charts
Compromise Pseudocylindrical

Robinson

Arthur H. Robinson, 1963

A compromise projection that "looks right" without being mathematically precise. It was National Geographic's standard world map projection from 1988 to 1998.

World Maps Atlases Education
Compromise Modified Azimuthal

Winkel Tripel

Oswald Winkel, 1921

Currently National Geographic's standard projection for world maps. "Tripel" (German for "triple") refers to minimizing distortion of area, direction, and distance.

World Maps Atlases Reference Maps
Equal-Area Pseudocylindrical

Mollweide

Karl Mollweide, 1805

An equal-area projection with an elliptical shape. Areas are accurate throughout, making it excellent for showing global distributions and comparing country sizes.

Thematic Maps Distribution Maps Choropleth Maps
Equal-Area Cylindrical

Gall-Peters

James Gall 1855 / Arno Peters 1974

An equal-area projection that preserves relative size at the cost of severe shape distortion. Controversial for political reasons – it shows developing nations at their true relative sizes.

Social Justice Area Comparison Education
Equal-Area Conic

Albers Equal Area Conic

Heinrich C. Albers, 1805

The standard projection for US government maps. Excellent for mapping mid-latitude regions with greater east-west extent, preserving area while minimizing shape distortion.

US Maps Continental Maps Statistical Maps
Conformal Conic

Lambert Conformal Conic

Johann Heinrich Lambert, 1772

Preserves angles and shapes, making it ideal for aeronautical charts. Used for official maps of many countries and for weather maps due to its angular accuracy.

Aviation Weather Maps Topographic Maps
Conformal Azimuthal

Stereographic

Ancient (Hipparchus, ~150 BC)

One of the oldest projections, used for polar maps and navigation. Preserves angles perfectly and maps circles on the sphere to circles on the plane.

Polar Maps Navigation Crystallography
Gnomonic Azimuthal

Gnomonic

Thales of Miletus, ~600 BC

The only projection where ALL great circles appear as straight lines. Essential for plotting the shortest route between two points (great circle navigation).

Air Navigation Sea Navigation Seismology
Perspective Azimuthal

Orthographic

Ancient (Hipparchus, ~150 BC)

Shows Earth as it appears from space – a hemisphere viewed from infinite distance. Creates the most realistic "globe" appearance on a flat surface.

Logos Illustrations Artistic Maps
Equidistant Azimuthal

Azimuthal Equidistant

Ancient, formalized ~1000 AD

Distances from the center point are accurate in all directions. Featured on the UN flag and emblem. Used for radio/telecommunications range calculations.

UN Emblem Radio Planning Air Routes
Equal-Area Interrupted

Goode Homolosine

John Paul Goode, 1923

An interrupted equal-area projection that minimizes distortion by "peeling" the map like an orange. Excellent for showing distributions across continents.

Thematic Maps Distribution Education
Equal-Area Pseudocylindrical

Sinusoidal

Jean Cossin, 1570

One of the oldest equal-area projections. Meridians are sine curves, parallels are equally spaced. Good for equatorial regions but distorts shapes at higher latitudes.

Equatorial Maps Africa Maps South America
Equal-Area Pseudocylindrical

Eckert IV & VI

Max Eckert, 1906

A series of six projections with rounded poles. Eckert IV and VI are equal-area, making them popular for thematic world maps. The rounded appearance is aesthetically pleasing.

World Maps Climate Maps Thematic Maps
Compromise Polyhedral

Fuller's Dymaxion

Buckminster Fuller, 1943

Projects Earth onto an icosahedron (20 triangles) that unfolds flat. Shows continents with minimal distortion and can be arranged to emphasize different perspectives.

Global Perspective One World Island Education
Near Equal-Area Polyhedral

AuthaGraph

Hajime Narukawa, 1999

A modern projection that divides the globe into 96 triangles projected onto a tetrahedron, then unfolded. Can tile seamlessly and won Japan's Good Design Grand Award in 2016.

Education Modern Atlases Seamless Tiling
Conformal Transverse Cylindrical

Transverse Mercator / UTM

Johann Heinrich Lambert, 1772 / UTM: 1947

The Mercator rotated 90°. UTM divides Earth into 60 zones, each using Transverse Mercator. The standard for topographic mapping and GPS coordinates worldwide.

Topographic Maps GPS/GIS Surveying
Conformal Web Standard

Web Mercator (EPSG:3857)

Google, 2005

A simplified Mercator used by virtually all web mapping services. Allows seamless tile-based zooming but treats Earth as a perfect sphere, introducing slight errors.

Google Maps OpenStreetMap All Web Maps

History of Map Projections

A journey through time exploring the evolution of cartographic projections.

~600 BC
Gnomonic Projection

Thales of Miletus creates the first known map projection. The gnomonic projection shows great circles as straight lines.

~150 BC
Stereographic & Orthographic

Hipparchus develops the stereographic projection. These ancient projections are still used today for polar maps and artwork.

1569
Mercator Projection

Gerardus Mercator publishes his revolutionary conformal projection, enabling accurate navigation for the Age of Exploration.

1772
Lambert's Contributions

Johann Heinrich Lambert creates multiple projections including the Lambert Conformal Conic and Transverse Mercator.

1805
Mollweide & Albers

Karl Mollweide and Heinrich Albers independently create influential equal-area projections still widely used today.

1921
Winkel Tripel

Oswald Winkel creates his "triple" projection, minimizing distortion in area, direction, and distance. Now used by National Geographic.

1943
Fuller's Dymaxion

Buckminster Fuller patents his innovative icosahedral projection, showing continents with minimal distortion.

1963
Robinson Projection

Arthur H. Robinson creates his compromise projection for Rand McNally, becoming the standard world map for decades.

2005
Web Mercator (EPSG:3857)

Google Maps launches using Web Mercator, making it the de facto standard for all online mapping services.

2016
AuthaGraph Recognition

Hajime Narukawa's AuthaGraph projection wins Japan's Good Design Grand Award for its innovative approach to equal-area mapping.

Mathematical Formulas

The mathematical foundations behind major map projections.

Mercator Projection

x = λ y = ln(tan(π/4 + φ/2)) or equivalently: y = arctanh(sin(φ))

Where λ is longitude, φ is latitude. The y-formula creates the characteristic stretching toward poles.

Stereographic Projection

R = 2tan(θ/2) x = R × cos(λ) y = R × sin(λ) where θ = 90° - φ (colatitude)

Projects from the antipodal point through the sphere onto a tangent plane. R is the radial distance from center.

Sinusoidal Projection

x = λ × cos(φ) y = φ (scaled by Earth's radius R)

The simplest equal-area projection formula. Longitude is multiplied by the cosine of latitude, creating the characteristic sinusoidal meridians.

Mollweide Projection

2θ + sin(2θ) = π × sin(φ) (solve for θ iteratively) x = (2√2/π) × λ × cos(θ) y = √2 × sin(θ)

An equal-area pseudocylindrical projection. The auxiliary angle θ must be solved numerically from the first equation.

Albers Equal-Area Conic

n = (sin(φ₁) + sin(φ₂)) / 2 C = cos²(φ₁) + 2n × sin(φ₁) ρ₀ = √(C - 2n × sin(φ₀)) / n ρ = √(C - 2n × sin(φ)) / n θ = n × λ x = ρ × sin(θ) y = ρ₀ - ρ × cos(θ)

Where φ₁ and φ₂ are the standard parallels, and φ₀ is the central latitude.

Tissot's Indicatrix

h = (1/R) × ∂y/∂φ (meridional scale) k = (1/(R×cos(φ))) × ∂x/∂λ (parallel scale) Area distortion = h × k × sin(θ') where θ' is the angle between transformed grid lines

h and k are the principal scale factors. For conformal projections, h = k. For equal-area, h × k = 1.
