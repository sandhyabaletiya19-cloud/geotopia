Geographic Coordinates

The fundamental system for pinpointing any location on Earth using angular measurements from reference planes.

Latitude (φ)

Range: 90°S to 90°N

Special Parallels

  • North Pole 90° N
  • Arctic Circle 66.5° N
  • Tropic of Cancer 23.5° N
  • Equator 0°
  • Tropic of Capricorn 23.5° S
  • Antarctic Circle 66.5° S
  • South Pole 90° S

Longitude (λ)

Range: 180°W to 180°E

Key Meridians

  • Prime Meridian 0°
  • Int'l Date Line ~180°

History: The Prime Meridian passes through Greenwich, London, established in 1884 at the International Meridian Conference.

Coordinate Formats

Geographic coordinates can be expressed in multiple formats. Understanding conversions is essential for working with different systems.

DMS

Degrees, Minutes, Seconds

Traditional format using 60-based subdivisions. Common on paper maps and in aviation.

Example (New York City)
40° 42' 46.08" N, 74° 0' 21.6" W

1 degree = 60 minutes
1 minute = 60 seconds

DDM

Degrees, Decimal Minutes

Hybrid format popular in marine navigation and some GPS devices.

Example (New York City)
40° 42.768' N, 74° 0.360' W

Seconds converted to decimal minutes for easier calculations.

DD

Decimal Degrees

Most common in GIS, databases, and web mapping. Uses +/- for hemispheres.

Example (New York City)
40.7128, -74.0060

Negative values: South latitude, West longitude

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM)

A global coordinate system that divides Earth into 60 zones, providing precise measurements in meters.

UTM Zone System

How UTM Works

• 60 zones, each 6° of longitude wide
• Each zone uses Transverse Mercator projection
• Measurements in meters from zone origin
• Easting: Distance east from zone central meridian (+ 500,000m false easting)
• Northing: Distance north from equator

UTM Advantages

• Distances in meters (easy calculations)
• Minimal distortion within each zone
• Consistent units worldwide
• Standard for topographic maps
• Used by military and surveyors

National & Regional Grid Systems

Many countries have their own coordinate systems optimized for local mapping and surveying.

🇬🇧

British National Grid

Ordnance Survey of Great Britain

Uses Transverse Mercator projection centered on 2°W. Divides Britain into 100km squares identified by two letters.

Big Ben, London
TQ 30163 79537
🇮🇪

Irish Grid

Ordnance Survey Ireland

Similar to British system but centered on 8°W. Uses single letters for 100km squares covering the island.

Dublin City Centre
O 15900 34100
🇺🇸

US State Plane

SPCS / NAD83

124 zones covering the US. Each state has 1-5 zones using Lambert Conformal Conic or Transverse Mercator.

New York (Long Island)
X: 301,341 Y: 62,834 (feet)
🇨🇭

Swiss Grid (CH1903)

Swisstopo

Oblique Mercator projection centered on Bern. Updated to CH1903+ (LV95) with 2 million meter offset.

Federal Palace, Bern
2,600,000 / 1,200,000

📐 The Haversine Formula

The mathematical formula for calculating great-circle distances between two points on a sphere.

a = sin²(Δφ/2) + cos(φ1) × cos(φ2) × sin²(Δλ/2) c = 2 × atan2(√a, √(1-a)) d = R × c where R = 6,371 km (Earth's radius)
φ1, φ2

Latitudes of points 1 and 2 (in radians)

λ1, λ2

Longitudes of points 1 and 2 (in radians)

Δφ

Difference in latitude (φ2 - φ1)

Δλ

Difference in longitude (λ2 - λ1)

a

Square of half the chord length

c

Angular distance in radians

Practical Tips

Common mistakes and best practices when working with coordinates.

Common Mistakes

  • ❌ Swapping latitude and longitude order
  • ❌ Forgetting negative signs for S/W
  • ❌ Mixing up coordinate formats (DMS vs DD)
  • ❌ Using wrong datum (WGS84 vs NAD27)
  • ❌ Ignoring the UTM zone number

Best Practices

  • ✅ Always specify the coordinate format used
  • ✅ Include the datum (e.g., WGS84)
  • ✅ Use decimal degrees for digital systems
  • ✅ Verify coordinates with a map before sharing
  • ✅ Store coordinates with adequate precision

Precision Guide

  • 1 decimal: ~11.1 km (country level)
  • 2 decimals: ~1.1 km (city level)
  • 3 decimals: ~111 m (neighborhood)
  • 4 decimals: ~11 m (street level)
  • 5 decimals: ~1.1 m (individual trees)
