Geographic Information Systems

GIS is a powerful framework for capturing, storing, analyzing, and displaying spatial data – the backbone of modern geospatial analysis.

What is GIS?

The spatial data powerhouse

GIS integrates hardware, software, and data to capture, manage, analyze, and display all forms of geographically referenced information.

  • Spatial data management
  • Geographic analysis
  • Map visualization
  • Decision support

Core Components

The GIS ecosystem

A complete GIS requires five essential components working together seamlessly.

  • Hardware: Computers, servers, GPS
  • Software: ArcGIS, QGIS, etc.
  • Data: Spatial & attribute data
  • People: Analysts & users
  • Methods: Procedures & workflows

Analysis Tools

Spatial operations

GIS provides powerful tools for spatial analysis and geographic problem-solving.

  • Buffer analysis
  • Overlay operations
  • Network analysis
  • Spatial interpolation
  • Terrain analysis

Vector vs Raster Data

Vector Data

Points, Lines, Polygons

  • Precise boundaries and locations
  • Small file sizes
  • Scales without quality loss
  • Attribute data per feature
  • Poor for continuous data
VS

Raster Data

Pixels / Grid Cells

  • Excellent for continuous data
  • Satellite imagery compatible
  • Simple data structure
  • Large file sizes
  • Quality loss when zooming

Remote Sensing

The science of obtaining information about objects or areas from a distance, typically from aircraft or satellites.

Passive Sensors

Detect natural radiation

  • Optical/multispectral imagery
  • Thermal infrared sensors
  • Affected by clouds/darkness
VS

Active Sensors

Emit their own energy

  • Works day and night
  • Penetrates clouds (SAR/Radar)
  • LiDAR for 3D mapping

Electromagnetic Spectrum in Remote Sensing

UV 0.01-0.4 µm
Visible 0.4-0.7 µm
Near IR 0.7-1.3 µm
SWIR 1.3-3 µm
Thermal IR 3-15 µm
Microwave 1mm-1m

Visible Light (RGB)

Natural color images we're familiar with. Used for general mapping, land use classification.

Near Infrared (NIR)

Excellent for vegetation health, water body detection, and agricultural monitoring.

Thermal Infrared

Measures heat emission. Used for fire detection, urban heat islands, geology.

Four Types of Resolution

Spatial

Ground area represented by each pixel. Ranges from 30cm (WorldView) to 1km (MODIS).

Spectral

Number and width of spectral bands. From RGB (3) to hyperspectral (200+).

Temporal

Revisit frequency. Daily (Planet) to 16 days (Landsat) to monthly.

Radiometric

Sensitivity to differences in energy. 8-bit (256 values) to 16-bit (65,536 values).

LiDAR Technology

Light Detection and Ranging uses laser pulses to create precise 3D models of the Earth's surface, even through vegetation.

How It Works

Laser pulses travel to the surface and back. Time × speed of light = distance.

Point Density

Modern systems: 100+ points/m². Results in centimeter-level accuracy.

Multiple Returns

Single pulse can return multiple times (canopy top, branches, ground).

Applications

DEMs, forestry, archaeology, flood modeling, power line inspection, autonomous vehicles.

Spectral Signature Viewer

Different materials reflect electromagnetic radiation differently - their unique "fingerprint" helps identify them.

🌿 Vegetation Characteristics

  • Low Red: Chlorophyll absorbs red light
  • Green Peak: Reflected green = appears green
  • High NIR: Cell structure reflects NIR strongly
  • Red Edge: Sharp transition at ~0.7µm

💧 Water Characteristics

  • Blue Peak: Highest reflectance in blue
  • Low Overall: Water absorbs most energy
  • Near Zero NIR: Almost complete absorption

🏜️ Bare Soil Characteristics

  • Gradual Increase: Smooth upward curve
  • No Red Edge: Unlike vegetation
  • Variable: Depends on moisture, minerals

🏙️ Urban/Built Characteristics

  • Flat Curve: Relatively uniform reflectance
  • Material Dependent: Asphalt vs concrete
  • High Thermal: Urban heat island effect

❄️ Snow/Ice Characteristics

  • Very High Visible: Up to 90% reflectance
  • NIR Drop: Decreases significantly
  • Unique Pattern: Easy to identify

Case Studies

Real-world examples demonstrating the power of GIS and remote sensing technologies.

Amazon Deforestation

Landsat Time Series Analysis

Using 40+ years of Landsat imagery, scientists track deforestation rates in the Amazon basin, identifying illegal logging and monitoring protected areas.

  • Change detection algorithms
  • NDVI temporal analysis
  • Alert systems for near real-time monitoring

Flood Mapping

SAR-Based Disaster Response

Sentinel-1 SAR imagery penetrates clouds to map flood extent during hurricanes and monsoons, enabling rapid emergency response.

  • All-weather imaging capability
  • Before/after comparisons
  • Population exposure analysis

Mayan Cities Discovery

LiDAR Archaeological Survey

Airborne LiDAR revealed over 60,000 previously unknown structures hidden under the Guatemalan jungle, rewriting Mayan history.

  • Penetrates dense canopy
  • Creates "bare earth" models
  • Reveals ancient infrastructure
