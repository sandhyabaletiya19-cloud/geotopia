Forests of the World — Earth's Ancient Green Giants

The forests of the world are the lungs, the library, and the life support system of our planet. Covering approximately 31% of Earth's total land area — nearly 4.06 billion hectares of trees, undergrowth, soil organisms, and wildlife — forests are the most complex and biodiverse terrestrial ecosystems on Earth. They produce the oxygen we breathe, filter the water we drink, regulate the climate we depend on, and shelter an estimated 80% of the world's terrestrial plant and animal species. The Amazon Rainforest alone contains more species of trees in a single hectare than exist in all of Europe. That is not just a statistic. That is a window into the staggering complexity of life on our planet.

On DharaVerse, we explore the world's forests with the depth, reverence, and scientific accuracy they deserve. From the steaming tropical rainforests of the equatorial belt to the vast boreal forests of the subarctic, from the ancient temperate forests of the Pacific Northwest to the mangrove forests that guard tropical coastlines — our comprehensive forest geography content covers every major forest biome on Earth.   • 5.5 million km² — Size of the Amazon Rainforest — the world's largest tropical forest, covering 40% of South America

Major Forest Regions of the World

Forests are not uniform — they are extraordinarily diverse in structure, species composition, climate, and ecology. Geographers classify the world's forests into several major biome types, each found in a specific climatic zone and each with its own unique characteristics:

  • The Amazon Rainforest — The Lungs of the Earth: The Amazon is the world's largest tropical rainforest, covering 5.5 million km² across nine countries — primarily Brazil, but also Peru, Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Bolivia, Guyana, Suriname, and French Guiana. It is home to an estimated 390 billion trees of approximately 16,000 species, more than 2.5 million insect species, 2,000 species of birds and mammals, and tens of thousands of plant species — many of which have not yet been scientifically described. The Amazon produces approximately 20% of the world's oxygen and is the single most important carbon store in the terrestrial biosphere. It also generates its own rainfall — trees in the Amazon release enormous quantities of water vapour that form "flying rivers" of moisture that bring rainfall to southeastern Brazil and beyond. The Amazon is not just a forest. It is a weather system.
  • The Congo Rainforest — Africa's Green Heart: Covering 3.7 million km² across six Central African countries — primarily the Democratic Republic of Congo — the Congo Rainforest is the world's second largest tropical forest. It is home to extraordinary wildlife including gorillas, chimpanzees, forest elephants, okapis, and thousands of endemic plant species. The Congo River — the world's deepest river — flows through this forest, and the entire ecosystem stores an estimated 66 billion tonnes of carbon. Despite its global importance, the Congo forest receives far less attention than the Amazon in international conservation discussions.
  • The Taiga — World's Largest Forest Biome: The boreal forest, or taiga, is the world's largest terrestrial biome, stretching in a vast belt across Canada, Russia, Scandinavia, and Alaska — covering approximately 17 million km². Dominated by coniferous trees — spruce, pine, fir, and larch — the taiga experiences extremely cold winters and short, warm summers. It stores vast quantities of carbon — both in living trees and in the permafrost beneath the soil — making it critically important in the context of climate change. As global temperatures rise, the taiga is warming faster than almost any other biome, with profound implications for global carbon budgets and climate stability.
  • The Daintree Rainforest — Earth's Oldest Forest: Located in Queensland, Australia, the Daintree Rainforest is the world's oldest continuously surviving tropical rainforest, with origins dating back at least 180 million years — predating even the dinosaurs. It covers approximately 1,200 km² and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The Daintree is home to extraordinary endemic species including the Bennett's tree kangaroo, southern cassowary, and numerous ancient plant lineages that are the evolutionary ancestors of flowering plants worldwide.
  • The Sundarbans — World's Largest Mangrove Forest: Spanning the delta of the Ganges, Brahmaputra, and Meghna rivers across Bangladesh and the Indian state of West Bengal, the Sundarbans cover approximately 10,000 km² and form the world's largest mangrove forest. They are home to the largest population of Bengal tigers in the world and protect millions of coastal inhabitants from tropical cyclones and storm surges. The Sundarbans are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and one of the world's most ecologically important coastal ecosystems.
  • Temperate Rainforests — The Pacific Northwest's Ancient Giants: The temperate rainforests of the Pacific Northwest of North America — stretching from northern California through Oregon, Washington, and British Columbia to Alaska — are home to the largest trees on Earth by volume. The coastal redwood (up to 115 metres tall) and the giant sequoia (up to 1,400 tonnes in mass) are the tallest and most massive living organisms ever to have existed on Earth. These ancient forests store more carbon per hectare than any other forest type in the world.

Forests and Our Planet — Why Forest Geography Matters

Forests are not a peripheral concern — they are central to every major environmental challenge facing humanity in the 21st century. In terms of climate change, forests act as both a carbon source and a carbon sink. When standing, they absorb enormous quantities of CO2. When cleared by fire or chainsaw, they release that stored carbon back into the atmosphere. Deforestation currently accounts for approximately 10% of global greenhouse gas emissions — more than the entire global transportation sector. Protecting existing forests and restoring degraded ones is one of the most cost-effective strategies available for climate change mitigation.

Furthermore, forests are the foundation of the global water cycle. They intercept rainfall, regulate its flow into rivers and aquifers, reduce flooding, and maintain the moisture levels of the soil and atmosphere. The Amazon's "flying rivers" demonstrate that forests can generate their own precipitation — meaning that large-scale deforestation can reduce rainfall not just locally but across entire continents. Additionally, forests are the world's greatest biodiversity reserves. At current rates of deforestation, scientists estimate that the world is losing 137 species every day — the vast majority from tropical forests. This is not just a biodiversity tragedy. Many of these species hold potential as sources of medicines, materials, and genetic resources that humanity has not yet had the chance to discover.
